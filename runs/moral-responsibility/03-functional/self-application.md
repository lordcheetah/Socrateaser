# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Functional — self-application tester
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.

---

SELF-APPLICATION: SURVIVES

## The test

The thesis asserts: "No agent is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense."
Self-application asks: when this thesis is asserted, does the act of asserting it — and
the epistemic/rational standing that makes it worth asserting — require a kind of
responsibility the thesis denies?

Concretely, two sub-tests:

**Sub-test A (doxastic/epistemic responsibility):** The skeptic who holds and defends
this thesis is doing something — reasoning, arguing, offering premises for assessment.
Does that activity presuppose basic-desert responsibility? If the skeptic can only "owe"
you a good argument in the basic-desert sense (deserving censure *for its own sake* if
they argue badly), then demanding rational answerability would invoke precisely the
concept they deny, producing a performative contradiction.

**Sub-test B (normative force):** The argument has normative force — it is supposed to
give you *reason* to believe the conclusion. Does that normativity require that the
arguer could be held responsible in the basic-desert sense for bad reasoning?

## Result

SURVIVES. Neither sub-test produces a contradiction, for a reason internal to the
thesis's own framing.

**On Sub-test A:** The thesis denies basic desert and explicitly preserves three other
senses of responsibility: (i) forward-looking consequentialist responsibility, (ii)
Strawsonian reactive-attitude responsibility, and (iii) *answerability/attributability*
— the agent's act is genuinely their own and they owe an account of it. Epistemic
responsibility — being answerable for one's reasoning, owing interlocutors good
grounds, being assessable as a thinker — falls squarely under answerability/
attributability. The skeptic can coherently say: "My argument is attributably mine;
you can demand I answer for it, and I owe you valid inference and true premises — but
nothing about this requires that I intrinsically *deserve blame for its own sake*,
backward-lookingly, independent of all forward-looking and relational purposes, if I
reason badly." The thesis denies one specific narrow concept, not the broader normative
standing that rational argumentation requires. The two tracks of normativity —
basic-desert and epistemic/attributability — are analytically separated by the thesis's
own framing.

**On Sub-test B:** The normative force of an argument is logical and epistemic
normativity: validity, truth, good grounds. That is a distinct normative domain from
backward-looking, intrinsic, consequence-independent desert. The inference from P1 and
P2 to C has normative grip because the argument is valid and the premises may be true
— not because the arguer deserves praise for its own sake if they get it right. An
argument compels by the lights of reason, not by the lights of retributive desert.

**On the classic self-defeat charge:** The canonical version of this objection runs: "If
no one is ever responsible, then the skeptic is not responsible for their own argument,
so we need not take it seriously." This charge is blunt and targets the wrong concept.
"Taking the argument seriously" requires that the argument be attributably the skeptic's
(they can be asked to defend it) — not that the skeptic intrinsically deserves eternal
censure if it fails. The classic charge works only if one conflates basic-desert
responsibility with epistemic answerability. That conflation is precisely what the
thesis's pinned definitions are designed to foreclose. A skeptic in the tradition of
Pereboom or Caruso readily acknowledges: "I am answerable for my reasoning; hold me to
it. I deny only that I am a fitting target for ultimate, consequence-independent
resentment or moral indignation." No self-refutation follows.

**Scope check:** The thesis is universal — it ranges over all agents and all actions.
The act of asserting the thesis is an action by an agent, so the thesis does formally
apply to itself. The self-application is genuine, not manufactured. It just does not
yield a contradiction because the normative demand the assertion places on the arguer
(be answerable, reason well) is not the normative concept the thesis negates.

## If it fails: can it be repaired?

The thesis survives as stated, so no repair is needed. For the record: if a critic
insisted that rational answerability just *is* a form of basic desert, the thesis
would face a restriction problem and might need to carve out epistemic contexts. But
that insistence would be a substantive philosophical claim that P1 and the key-term
definitions already resist — it would amount to denying that the four senses of
responsibility are genuinely distinct, which is the thesis's foundational conceptual
move. Any "repair" in that direction costs the thesis nothing it isn't already
prepared to defend on independent grounds.
