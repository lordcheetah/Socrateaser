## Suppressed premises

---

### HP1 — The univocity assumption

- **Stated form:** "Ultimate source" picks out exactly the same property in P1 (the
  conceptual claim about what basic desert requires) and in P2 (the metaphysical
  claim about what no agent possesses) — the term does not silently shift meaning
  between the two premises.
- **Where it's needed:** Without it, the modus tollens is invalid: P2 could deny
  *compatibilist* sourcehood while P1's requirement is for *regress-ending*
  sourcehood, or vice versa, leaving the inference as an equivocation rather than a
  valid deduction. The reconstructor's Variant A (buck-stopping originator) is an
  attempt to supply this premise explicitly.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — compatibilists explicitly charge the argument
  with equivocation: they read "ultimate source" in P1 as compatible with ordinary
  reasons-responsiveness, and the skeptic's P2 then attacks a straw target.
- **Who would deny it:** Fischer–Ravizza source compatibilists, Frankfurt, Sartorio;
  any view that reads P1's sourcehood requirement as already satisfied by
  reasons-responsive mechanisms and therefore sees the argument as trading on two
  different senses.

---

### HP2 — Basic desert is a coherent, non-empty concept

- **Stated form:** The concept of basic desert (desert intrinsic and backward-looking,
  independent of consequences) is coherent — it is not a confused or contradictory
  notion — and it is at least in principle instantiable: there is a possible world
  where an agent could satisfy it.
- **Where it's needed:** If basic desert is incoherent, the conclusion ("no one ever
  has it") is trivially true and the argument proves nothing interesting — the same
  way "no bachelors are married" is trivially true but uninformative. The argument
  is only philosophically significant if the concept is coherent enough that its
  non-instantiation is a substantive discovery, not a definitional consequence.
  Additionally, P1 only establishes a genuine necessary condition if the concept is
  coherent; a necessary condition on an incoherent concept proves nothing.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — eliminativists and some deflationists deny
  that backward-looking desert for its own sake is coherent at all (it cannot be
  cashed out without some appeal to consequences, relationships, or social
  function); on this view the concept dissolves rather than being proven absent.
- **Who would deny it:** Hard eliminativists (some readings of Wittgenstein on
  punishment), certain pragmatists (Dewey-style), and deflationists like Manuel
  Vargas who argue the ordinary concept is fragmented and that "basic desert" is a
  philosophers' reconstruction with no determinate grip on folk practice.

---

### HP3 — Philosophical analysis of a concept is authoritative over whether it is instantiated

- **Stated form:** The correct philosophical analysis of what "basic desert" requires
  (namely, ultimate sourcehood) determines whether agents actually have it. Put
  differently: there is no gap between what the concept analytically entails and
  what moral practice is actually tracking when people ascribe desert.
- **Where it's needed:** The argument moves from "basic desert as analyzed requires
  ultimate sourcehood" (P1) plus "no agent has ultimate sourcehood" (P2) to "no
  one has basic desert." This only reaches moral practice if the philosophical
  analysis accurately captures what ordinary desert-attributions are about. If
  ordinary practice is tracking something looser or different, then showing the
  philosophers' concept is uninstantiated leaves actual moral practice untouched.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — this is the revisionary-versus-vindicatory
  analysis debate. Vargas (revisionary naturalist) argues our concepts are messy
  and that philosophy should reconstruct rather than analyze; on that view, showing
  the strict concept is uninstantiated is not the same as vindicating skepticism
  about what people actually do when they blame.
- **Who would deny it:** Vargas, moral-responsibility revisionists, ordinary-language
  philosophers who insist the term of art diverges from the folk concept.

---

### HP4 — The self-authorship requirement for ultimate sourcehood

- **Stated form:** To count as the ultimate source of an action, an agent must be (at
  least in part) the source of the character, values, or reasons from which the
  action flows — i.e., being the proximate cause of a physical movement is
  insufficient; one must also author the self that did the causing.
- **Where it's needed:** This premise is what gets the regress started in the
  sub-argument (Variant B, P2a). Without it, an agent could qualify as an ultimate
  source simply by being the proximate, uncoerced cause of their own action —
  which is precisely what compatibilists do claim. The regress is only vicious if
  sourcehood requires tracing back through character to its origins.
- **How contestable:** hidden crux candidate — this is not stated anywhere in the
  main argument or in P1 as written. It is the premise that separates the skeptic's
  reading of "ultimate source" from the compatibilist's. Compatibilists deny it
  outright: sourcehood is conferred by the right kind of reasons-responsiveness at
  the time of action, not by the agent's causal ancestry. The entire fight over P1
  is in part a fight about whether HP4 is built into what "ultimate source" must
  mean.
- **Who would deny it:** Frankfurt (hierarchical mesh compatibilism — what matters
  is second-order identification with one's will, not its causal history),
  Fischer–Ravizza (reasons-responsive mechanism need not be self-produced to ground
  responsibility), Wolf (the ability to act in accordance with Reason is sufficient
  without authoring one's rational capacities).

### HP5 — Indeterminism does not restore sourcehood

- **Stated form:** A break in the causal chain introduced by quantum-level or
  macro-level indeterminism does not make an agent the ultimate source of an
  action; chance is not authorship, so replacing deterministic causation with
  probabilistic causation does not satisfy the sourcehood requirement.
- **Where it's needed:** Without this, P2 is not universal. Libertarians and some
  compatibilists argue that indeterminism at the right level (e.g., in practical
  reasoning, as in Robert Kane) is precisely what introduces genuine authorship.
  If chance events can constitute acts of will, P2 fails for indeterministically
  generated actions.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — this is one of the two central contested
  moves in the P2 sub-argument (P2d in Variant B). Robert Kane explicitly argues
  that self-forming actions in which the outcome is probabilistically sensitive to
  the agent's effort of will constitute genuine sourcehood. The premise is often
  stated as if obvious but is resisted by a significant libertarian tradition.
- **Who would deny it:** Robert Kane (event-causal libertarianism), Carl Ginet,
  and agent-causation theorists who hold that indeterminism, properly structured,
  is the vehicle for sourcehood rather than an obstacle to it.

---

### HP6 — Agent-causation is not a genuine metaphysical possibility (or is insufficient)

- **Stated form:** The appeal to agent-causation — the idea that the agent qua
  substance initiates a causal series without being caused to do so by prior events
  — either is metaphysically incoherent, or even if coherent, does not satisfy the
  ultimate-sourcehood requirement in a way that would underwrite basic desert.
- **Where it's needed:** Without this, the universality of P2 is open to a
  libertarian objection. Timothy O'Connor and Roderick Chisholm argue that
  agent-causation is precisely what stops the regress: the agent as substance is
  the terminus of explanation in a way event-causation never can be. The argument
  needs to have ruled this out, but it does not state that it has.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — agent-causation is a minority but serious
  metaphysical view (O'Connor, Chisholm, Clarke). The reconstructor's Variant B
  sub-argument (P2c–P2d) gestures at this without explicitly addressing the
  agent-causationist reply.
- **Who would deny it:** O'Connor, Chisholm, and agent-causation libertarians;
  also some theists who hold that the soul constitutes a genuinely self-initiating
  substance.

---

### HP7 — The backward-looking requirement is real and cannot be reduced to forward-looking considerations

- **Stated form:** Basic desert's defining feature — that the response would be
  warranted even in a world with no beneficial consequences, no deterrence, no
  relational repair — is a real and robust requirement, not a philosopher's
  artifice. Actual moral practice contains genuine backward-looking desert
  attributions that cannot be explained away as disguised forward-looking
  reasoning.
- **Where it's needed:** The scope of the conclusion — that *no one* has basic
  desert — is only significant if basic desert is what people are actually after
  when they blame. If all genuine desert-attributions reduce on analysis to
  forward-looking or Strawsonian reactive-attitude judgments, then P1 targets an
  empty or negligible class: there are no basic-desert ascriptions in practice to
  lack. The thesis would be trivially true (the concept is never instantiated
  because it is never really deployed) rather than a surprising skeptical result.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — consequentialists and many Strawsonians
  hold exactly this: "desert" in ordinary use either is or should be cashed out in
  terms of attitudes or consequences. If they are right, the target of the
  argument collapses and the conclusion, while technically correct, has no critical
  bite.
- **Who would deny it:** Smart, Bentham-style consequentialists about punishment;
  P.F. Strawson (reactive attitudes exhaust the normatively relevant sense of
  responsibility); forward-looking expressivists about blame.

---

### HP8 — The same account of sourcehood applies uniformly to all agents and all action types

- **Stated form:** There is no class of agents (e.g., idealized rational agents,
  morally exemplary persons) or action type (e.g., reflectively endorsed
  actions, self-forming willings under indeterminism) for which the sourcehood
  requirement is satisfied even though it fails universally. The regress that
  defeats sourcehood is equally vicious for every agent across the full range of
  actions.
- **Where it's needed:** The conclusion is universal: "no one ever." The
  sub-argument for P2 is presented through the regress, but the regress could
  conceivably bottom out differently for some agents or action types — for
  instance, at moments of genuine self-formation (Kane), or for agents whose
  characters were self-sculpted over a lifetime of deliberate effort. If any such
  class exists, the universal conclusion fails even if the regress defeats sourcehood
  in typical cases.
- **How contestable:** live/disputed — Kane's self-forming-actions account is
  precisely a theory of the conditions under which the regress does not apply,
  carving out a restricted class of actions as genuine sourcehood instances. The
  universal claim needs this premise to hold its scope, but the premise is never
  argued for directly.
- **Who would deny it:** Kane; also, some virtue-ethical accounts that treat
  character formed through long habituation as constituting a kind of authorship
  that mere causal ancestry does not extinguish.

## Summary note for the synthesizer

HP4 (self-authorship requirement) is the strongest candidate for a hidden crux.
It is not stated in either premise but is the load-bearing assumption that
distinguishes the skeptic's reading of "ultimate source" from the compatibilist's.
Every major compatibilist objection to P1 is, at bottom, a denial of HP4. The
reconstructor correctly identifies P1 as the hinge premise; HP4 is the hidden
joint inside P1.

HP3 (philosophical analysis is authoritative over whether the concept is
instantiated) is the crux for the revisionary camp (Vargas et al.) who accept the
philosophical analysis but deny it reaches folk practice. It is less often made
explicit in the debate and therefore more likely to be a source of talking-past.
