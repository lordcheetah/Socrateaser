# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Functional — counterexample generator
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.

---

COUNTEREXAMPLES: [5 strong | 2 weak | none decisive]

---

The thesis is a universal skeptical claim: no one is ever morally responsible in
the basic-desert sense. Because it is universal, a single clean case of a
basic-desert-responsible agent refutes it. The cases below are ordered by
directness of attack. Cases 1–3 target P1 (the conceptual premise: basic desert
requires ultimate sourcehood). Cases 4–5 target P2 (the metaphysical premise: no
one is an ultimate source). Cases 6–7 are weak or merely narrowing. The
"force" verdict is for the thesis as stated; the likely-repair column tracks
whether the repair preserves the thesis's core point.

---

## Cases

---

### Case 1 — Frankfurt's willing addict / the mesh theorist's clean case

**Case:** Harry Frankfurt's "willing addict" (*Freedom of the Will and the Concept
of a Person*, 1971). The addict takes heroin because he wants to and wholeheartedly
wants to want to. His first- and second-order desires mesh perfectly; he acts from
his deepest self, with no internal conflict or alien compulsion. He is not an
ultimate source in the regress-ending sense: his character, desires, and
evaluative structure were shaped by factors he did not himself produce. Yet many
theorists — Frankfurt included — judge that he is the appropriate target of desert-
constituting reactive attitudes precisely because the action fully expresses who he
is.

**What the thesis predicts:** Even the willing addict lacks basic-desert
responsibility, because his desires were fixed by prior causes he did not author.
He may be attributable-responsible (the act is his) but not desert-responsible.

**The tension:** The case is designed to isolate the intuitive traction of self-
expression / mesh independently of ultimate sourcehood. If the addict's seamless
self-expression is still insufficient for desert, the skeptic must explain why the
residual regress-gap matters when nothing in the phenomenology or normative
structure of the case tracks it. The burden shifts: why does origination matter
once mesh is perfect?

**Force:** Narrowing — does not refute the thesis outright, because a committed
skeptic can grant the act is attributable while denying it is desert-eligible.
But the case narrows the thesis: the skeptic must show the *further* desert
requirement (not just mesh but ultimate origination) is doing real conceptual
work, not merely philosophical housekeeping.

**Likely repair:** "Basic desert requires more than deep-self mesh; it requires
that the self itself be self-produced." The repair is coherent but costs the
skeptic a burden: they must now argue that the intuitive pull of the willing-addict
case — which feels like a responsibility case — is entirely illusory. That is
defensible but not free.

### Case 2 — Fischer and Ravizza's reasons-responsive mechanism

**Case:** Fischer and Ravizza (*Responsibility and Control*, 1998) describe an
agent — call him Marcus — who acts from a moderately reasons-responsive mechanism
he has "taken ownership of" through his history of rational self-governance. He
recognizes reasons, acts on them, would respond differently if the reasons were
different. He is not an ultimate source (his mechanism was shaped by genes,
upbringing, luck), but he has *guidance control*: the mechanism is his own and it
tracks reasons. Fischer and Ravizza explicitly accept P2 and deny P1. They
hold Marcus deserves blame, in the basic-desert sense, for wrongdoing.

**What the thesis predicts:** Marcus lacks basic-desert responsibility because he
lacks ultimate sourcehood, which P1 declares necessary.

**The tension:** Fischer–Ravizza's entire position is that P1 is false as stated:
the concept of basic desert, properly analyzed, requires guidance control (reasons-
responsiveness + ownership), not ultimate sourcehood. They are not changing the
subject from basic desert to some forward-looking substitute — they are contesting
what basic desert is. The case forces the skeptic either to argue that Fischer–
Ravizza are secretly talking about a different concept, or to defend P1 by
argument rather than stipulation.

**Force:** Narrowing (strong). Not decisive because "basic desert" is the disputed
concept: the skeptic can say Fischer–Ravizza's notion of guidance-control desert is
not the intrinsic, backward-looking sense the thesis denies. But the narrowing
pressure is severe — the skeptic must explain why guidance-control desert is not
genuine backward-looking desert, and the explanation will require a substantive
argument rather than a terminological move.

**Likely repair:** "The Fischer–Ravizza concept is really forward-looking desert
or answerability in disguise; genuine intrinsic desert requires the buck to stop."
The repair is the standard hard-incompatibilist reply (Pereboom makes it
explicitly). It costs the skeptic nothing logically, but it shifts the entire
debate to whether P1 is true, which is precisely the crux.

### Case 3 — The clear-eyed atrocity: Adolf Eichmann

**Case:** Adolf Eichmann coordinated the logistical machinery of the Holocaust with
deliberateness, attention to detail, and awareness of what he was doing. He was
not coerced, not deceived, not under duress. He acted from stable values,
long-formed dispositions, and a full rational understanding of his actions. He was
reasons-responsive in every ordinary sense; alternative possibilities were
available to him (others in analogous positions refused). On the thesis, he is not
a candidate for basic desert — his character was formed by prior causes he did not
author, so he cannot intrinsically deserve condemnation for its own sake.

**What the thesis predicts:** Eichmann does not deserve punishment in the
backward-looking, intrinsic sense. He may be incapacitated, treated, or socially
managed, but one cannot say he *deserves* condemnation just because of what he did,
independently of any further consequence.

**The tension:** This is the strongest intuitive pressure on the thesis. The
judgment that Eichmann intrinsically deserves condemnation — not merely that
condemning him is useful or expressive — is as firm and widely shared as any moral
intuition in the literature. The skeptic's denial is not incoherent, but it runs
against one of the most deeply entrenched reactive-attitude judgments humans make.
Pereboom (*Living Without Free Will*, 2001) explicitly bites this bullet: we should
replace retributive desert with forward-looking justifications for punishment.
Whether that bullet-biting is acceptable is precisely the question.

**Force:** Narrowing (very strong). Not technically decisive, because the skeptic
can accept the counterintuitive conclusion and argue we should revise the
intuition. But the case is the canonical "tollensing the ponens" moment: if the
skeptic's premises are sound, the conclusion follows; if the conclusion is
monstrous, reject a premise. The case raises the cost of the thesis without
refuting it.

**Likely repair:** "Our intuition that Eichmann deserves condemnation is
explicable as a very strong reactive attitude (resentment, indignation) that
does not require basic desert in the intrinsic sense — it is the attitude that is
appropriate, not a backward-looking desert fact." This is the Strawsonian re-
description. It is internally consistent but costs the skeptic: it concedes that
ordinary moral discourse *sounds* like basic-desert discourse, and requires
explaining why the sound is systematically misleading.

### Case 4 — Kane's self-forming action (SFA): the businesswoman at the crossroads

**Case:** Robert Kane (*The Significance of Free Will*, 1996) describes a
businesswoman rushing to an important meeting who witnesses a mugging. She faces a
genuine internal conflict: self-interest versus moral duty. Kane argues that at
moments of such "torn" decisions, quantum-level indeterminacies in neural firing
can propagate upward so that neither outcome is determined by prior causes. If she
stops to help, that act is (partly) undetermined — not wholly fixed by prior causes
— and she is, in that limited sense, an ultimate source. She is not *causa sui*
in the full regress-ending sense, but she is the originator of which of two
competing self-expressions wins out.

**What the thesis predicts:** Even the businesswoman's SFA fails to confer
ultimate sourcehood because P2b of the sub-argument says the character / reasons
from which she chooses were not themselves self-produced, and P2d says indeterminism
adds only chance, not authorship.

**The tension:** The case attacks P2d directly. Kane's reply is that when an
agent is genuinely torn between competing motivational sets, either outcome would
count as *her* choice — both are expressions of her self — so the indeterminacy
does not add mere chance but rather makes the choice irreducibly hers. The
skeptic's P2d assumes that all indeterminism is "mere" chance unmixed with
authorship; the SFA case challenges that assumption.

**Force:** Narrowing (contested). Decisive if Kane is right that torn-decision
indeterminism is a genuine form of authorship. Weak if the skeptic's reply —
that luck-at-the-moment-of-choice is just displaced luck — is correct. This is a
live metaphysical dispute; neither side can claim the case is settled.

**Likely repair:** "The indeterminacy in Kane's SFA is irrelevant: whichever way
it fires, the resulting choice traces back to a character and set of reasons the
agent did not produce, so the regress is not broken, only interrupted by noise."
This is Pereboom's standard reply. It preserves P2 but requires the skeptic to
defend the claim that interrupting the causal chain with indeterminism never
constitutes genuine authorship — a claim many find counterintuitive.

### Case 5 — The manipulation argument reversed: the hard-line reply

**Case:** Pereboom's four-case argument (*Living Without Free Will*, 2001) runs
from Case 1 (overt manipulation) to Case 4 (ordinary determinism), arguing that
if the manipulated agent lacks responsibility, so does the ordinary determined
agent. The hard-line reply (Fischer, Sartorio) runs the inference in the opposite
direction: if the ordinary reasons-responsive agent in Case 4 clearly has
responsibility (strong intuition), then the manipulated agent in Case 1 does too —
manipulation per se does not destroy the relevant mechanism's ownership or reasons-
responsiveness. The hard-liner concedes that the cases are analogous and draws the
opposite conclusion.

**What the thesis predicts:** Both the manipulated and the ordinary determined
agent lack basic-desert responsibility, because neither is an ultimate source.

**The tension:** The hard-line reply shows that Pereboom's argument is a double-
edged modus ponens/modus tollens: which direction you run it depends on which
intuition you treat as more secure — that obvious manipulation excuses, or that
ordinary rational agency confers desert. The case is not a straightforward
counterexample to the thesis; it is a counterexample to the manipulation
*argument for* the thesis. It attacks P2 indirectly by showing that the
manipulation argument cannot establish P2 without begging the question against the
compatibilist.

**Force:** Narrowing. Decisive against using the manipulation argument to *argue
for* P2, but not by itself against P2. The skeptic can still assert P2 on other
grounds (the basic argument's regress). The case reveals that Pereboom's
positive argument for P2 is less watertight than it appears, without refuting P2.

**Likely repair:** "P2 does not rest solely on the manipulation argument; it rests
on the basic argument's regress (P2a–P2d). The manipulation argument is a
rhetorical device to elicit intuitions about the regress; even if the hard-line
reply defeats the rhetoric, the regress stands." This is correct and costs the
skeptic nothing — but it shifts the burden entirely to defending P2a–P2d directly.

### Case 6 — Susan Wolf's JoJo and the sanity condition (WEAK)

**Case:** Susan Wolf (*Freedom Within Reason*, 1990) introduces JoJo, the son of a
vicious dictator, raised to endorse cruelty. JoJo's character was wholly shaped by
a morally corrupt upbringing; he has no access to correct moral reasons. Wolf uses
JoJo to argue that deep-self theories (where acting from one's deep self suffices
for responsibility) are inadequate: JoJo has a deep self but lacks the sanity
(reason-access) required for desert. The case is typically used *against*
compatibilist theories, but a defender of the thesis can also read it as a
counterexample to any view that makes responsibility too easy: if JoJo is not
responsible even on mesh theories, then mesh is insufficient, and perhaps ultimate
sourcehood is the missing ingredient.

**What the thesis predicts:** JoJo is not basic-desert responsible — agreed on
all sides. The thesis correctly predicts this case.

**The tension:** Minimal. The case does not pressure the thesis; it pressures
compatibilist rivals. It does no work against the universal skeptical claim.

**Force:** Weak — the thesis correctly predicts the JoJo verdict. This case
cannot be a counterexample to the skeptical thesis.

**Likely repair:** Not needed.

### Case 7 — The defiant saint: clear-eyed, unrestricted praise-desert (WEAK)

**Case:** Consider a person who, across decades of temptation, repeated deliberate
choice, and at significant personal cost, builds a deeply just character and
performs acts of genuine moral excellence. She is not coerced; her acts express
the full depth of who she is. The intuition that she intrinsically deserves praise
— not merely that praising her is useful or expressive — seems almost as firm as
the Eichmann intuition.

**What the thesis predicts:** She too lacks basic-desert praise. Her character
was formed by initial luck (good genes, good upbringing) she did not author, so
the regress cannot stop with her.

**The tension:** The case tests whether the thesis applies symmetrically to
praise-desert, which is often granted more reluctantly by skeptics in informal
discourse. Pereboom is explicit: hard incompatibilism denies both blame-desert
and praise-desert. The case does not technically pressure the argument but does
surface whether the skeptic's position is held consistently.

**Force:** Weak — the thesis is explicitly committed to this verdict (the
"never... blame or praise" formulation in the original thesis). The case reveals
a costs asymmetry (denying praise-desert feels more counterintuitive to many
audiences than denying blame-desert), but it is not a logical pressure point.

**Likely repair:** None needed structurally. The skeptic accepts the verdict and
notes that the pull toward praise-desert is equally explicable as a reactive
attitude rather than a metaphysical desert fact.

## Summary verdict

No case here is *decisive* in the strict sense of showing the thesis false as
stated given only uncontested assumptions. The thesis is set up to absorb
counterexamples: every case that generates a strong responsibility intuition can be
re-described as involving attributability, reactive-attitude fittingness, or
guidance-control desert rather than the narrow intrinsic-backward-looking desert the
thesis denies.

The genuinely dangerous cases are **Case 2** (Fischer–Ravizza: P1 is contested as
a conceptual matter, not just an intuition clash) and **Case 3** (Eichmann: forces
a costly bullet-bite that the skeptic must be willing to own explicitly). Case 4
(Kane's SFA) is the live attack on P2 but is caught in a live metaphysical dispute.
Case 5 reveals that Pereboom's own positive argument for P2 has a structural
vulnerability without defeating P2 itself.

The thesis survives all five strong cases — but only by invoking P1 as a conceptual
stipulation (not an argued result) in Cases 1, 2, 3, and by accepting highly
counterintuitive conclusions in Case 3. That pattern makes P1 the load-bearing
crux, exactly as the reconstruction identified.
