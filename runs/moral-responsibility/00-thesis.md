# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Thesis & framing
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Frame
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.

---

## Thesis
No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense — never such that they
intrinsically deserve blame or praise for its own sake — because basic desert
requires the agent to be the ultimate source of their action, and no agent is ever
the ultimate source of anything they do.

## Classification
- **Type:** mixed (primarily metaphysical, with a load-bearing conceptual premise)
- **Why this type:** The thesis is conceptual at the joint where it claims basic
  desert *requires* ultimate sourcehood — that is a claim about what the concept of
  intrinsic, backward-looking desert presupposes, settled by analysis of the
  concept, not by facts. But the thesis is metaphysical at the joint where it claims
  no agent *is* an ultimate source — that turns on the actual structure of agency
  and causation (whether anything could break the regress of prior causes: luck in
  constitution, determinism, or the randomness of indeterminism). It is not a
  normative dispute about whether we *should* blame people; the forward-looking
  question of whether blaming is useful or fitting is explicitly bracketed out by the
  "for its own sake" clause. Evidence downstream is therefore split: the conceptual
  premise is tested by argument from what "deserve... for its own sake" must mean,
  the metaphysical premise by the manipulation/luck arguments and their rebuttals.

## Key terms to pin
- **basic desert:** Desert that is *intrinsic* and *backward-looking* — the agent
  deserves the blame or praise *because of what they did and in virtue of who they
  are*, full stop, such that the blame would be deserved even in a world where it did
  no good, deterred nothing, expressed no useful attitude, and repaired no relationship.
  This is Pereboom's sense: desert that justifies a response "just because" of the
  act, independent of any further consequence or social function. RIVAL SENSES that
  would change the dispute: (a) *forward-looking / consequentialist* responsibility —
  blaming is warranted because it deters, reforms, or protects; (b) *Strawsonian
  reactive-attitude* responsibility — blame is "fitting" as an expression of resentment
  or indignation embedded in our interpersonal practices; (c) *answerability /
  attributability* — the act is genuinely the agent's own and they owe an account of it.
  The thesis denies only (and exactly) basic desert. It is compatible with affirming
  all three rivals, which is precisely what makes it sharp rather than nihilistic.
- **ultimate source:** The agent originates the action in a way not wholly inherited
  from factors the agent did not themselves produce — i.e. the buck of explanation
  stops with the agent, not with their genes, upbringing, prior character, or chance.
  RIVAL SENSE: a *sourcehood* compatibilists accept — the action flows from the
  agent's own reasons-responsive mechanism — which does *not* require stopping the
  regress. The whole fight is over which sourcehood basic desert needs.
- **"never / no one":** The thesis is deliberately UNIVERSAL (the skeptical claim),
  not restricted. See note below.

## Universal vs. restricted (decision)
I chose the UNIVERSAL skeptical claim ("no one ever") rather than a restricted one
("agents under condition C are not responsible"). Reasons: (1) The seed's question is
itself universal ("Is *anyone ever*...?"), and the live philosophical dispute —
Pereboom's hard incompatibilism, Caruso's skepticism, against Strawsonians and
source-compatibilists — is fought at the universal level. (2) A restricted claim
("the manipulated/the addicted/the determined are not responsible") is far less
contestable and risks collapsing into a tame consensus that hard cases excuse. (3)
The universal claim is maximally falsifiable: a single clear case of a basic-desert-
responsible agent refutes it, which is exactly the form a good seed should take.
I framed the *skeptical* direction (rather than "someone is responsible because...")
because it carries the stronger, cleaner burden — it must defend both the conceptual
requirement and the metaphysical impossibility — and because the author should be
movable on it: it is a claim one can be argued *out of* by a single counterexample or
by rejecting the sourcehood requirement.

## Is it contestable?
Yes, on multiple independent fronts:
- **P.F. Strawson / contemporary Strawsonians (e.g. R. Jay Wallace, Pamela
  Hieronymi):** deny that responsibility needs any metaphysical sourcehood at all.
  The reactive attitudes are not hostage to the truth about ultimate origination;
  desert just *is* fittingness of those attitudes. On the strongest reading they deny
  the thesis by denying its conceptual premise (basic desert ≠ requires ultimate
  source).
- **Source / reasons-responsiveness compatibilists (Fischer & Ravizza, Frankfurt,
  Sartorio):** grant that we are not ultimate sources but deny that basic desert
  requires it — the right kind of ownership of one's mechanism suffices for desert.
  They attack the conceptual premise directly.
- **Libertarians (Robert Kane, Timothy O'Connor):** grant that basic desert requires
  ultimate sourcehood but deny the metaphysical premise — agent-causation or
  event-causal libertarian agency *does* make us ultimate sources, so the regress can
  be broken. They concede the first premise and reject the second.
A thesis denied by three distinct, serious traditions for three distinct reasons is
robustly contestable. This is not an uncontestable seed.

## Pseudo-dispute check
There is a real risk of pseudo-dispute here, and the framing is built to defuse it.
Much apparent disagreement about "moral responsibility" dissolves the moment one
distinguishes the four senses above: a skeptic and a Strawsonian can talk past each
other forever, one saying "no responsibility!" and the other "obviously yes!", while
agreeing on every fact, because one means basic desert and the other means
fittingness of reactive attitudes. The thesis forecloses *that* pseudo-dispute by
pinning "basic desert... for its own sake" and explicitly conceding the forward-
looking and reactive-attitude senses. What remains is a genuine disagreement: even
once everyone fixes on basic desert in the same sense, Strawsonians/compatibilists
and skeptics still disagree about whether that very concept requires ultimate
sourcehood, and skeptics and libertarians still disagree about whether we possess it.
Those residual disagreements survive every distinction I can draw — they are about
the nature of the concept and the structure of agency, not about word choice — so the
core dispute is real, not verbal.

## Suggested specialists to summon
- A Pereboom-style hard incompatibilist (defends the thesis; manipulation argument).
- A Strawsonian reactive-attitudes theorist (denies the conceptual premise).
- A Fischer–Ravizza source/reasons-responsiveness compatibilist (denies that desert
  needs ultimate sourcehood, grants we lack it).
