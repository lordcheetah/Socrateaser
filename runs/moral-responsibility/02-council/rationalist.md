# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Rationalist (council)
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.
> **Seat:** Rationalist — Kantian/rationalist
> **Defending:** VERDICT: The thesis is FALSE — P1 is false, because conceptual analysis of "deserves in virtue of who they are" yields a condition the agent can satisfy (rational ownership of and guidance-control over a reasons-responsive mechanism), not the regress-ending sourcehood the skeptic stipulates.

---

VERDICT: The thesis is FALSE — P1 is false, because conceptual analysis of "deserves in virtue of who they are" yields a condition the agent can satisfy (rational ownership of and guidance-control over a reasons-responsive mechanism), not the regress-ending sourcehood the skeptic stipulates.

## Case

I grant P2 outright. We are not regress-ending originators; no one authors the self from nothing. That concession costs me nothing, because P1 is where the argument is unsound. The skeptic's whole edifice rests on an *analytic* claim — that "basic desert" *requires* ultimate sourcehood — and an analytic claim is refuted not by metaphysics but by getting the concept right. My route is a priori conceptual analysis of the desert concept itself.

**Begin from what the thesis stipulates desert to be.** The reconstruction pins basic desert as desert the agent has "*in virtue of who they are*" — such that blame would be deserved even in a world where it did no good (00-thesis.md, lines 23–27). I take that clause seriously and ask: what does "in virtue of who they are" actually require? It requires that the action issue *from the agent* — from their own evaluative standpoint, their reasons, their will — rather than from something that merely happens *to* them or *through* them. That is a condition about the *relation between agent and action*. It is not, on its face, a condition about the causal history *behind* the agent. The skeptic smuggles the second in under cover of the first.

**The concept that "in virtue of who they are" picks out is ownership of a reasons-responsive mechanism, not regress-ending causation.** Fischer and Ravizza's *Responsibility and Control* gives the analysis: moral responsibility requires *guidance control*, which has exactly two components — (i) the mechanism issuing in the action must be *the agent's own*, and (ii) that mechanism must be *moderately responsive to reasons* (Fischer & Ravizza, *Responsibility and Control*, 1998; PhilPapers précis). Crucially, guidance control by its nature does *not* require alternative possibilities or regulative control — and a fortiori does not require that the agent originated their own constitution. The "who they are" that grounds desert is the *evaluative author* of the deed: the one whose recognition of and response to reasons explains the action. That is a real, instantiable relation, fully compatible with P2.

**The agent makes the mechanism their own — this is rational ownership, and it does the desert-grounding work.** Fischer and Ravizza add a historical *ownership* condition: an agent "takes responsibility" by coming to see themselves as a fair target of the reactive attitudes (gratitude, resentment, praise, blame), and — decisively for a rationalist — adopting that self-conception *in a way appropriately based on the evidence* (Fischer & Ravizza; Watson, "Responsibility, Manipulation and Ownership," *Philosophical Explorations* 2005). Note the structure: ownership is constituted by a *rationally answerable* self-relation, an exercise of the very capacity for reasons-governed self-assessment. This is not a brute causal fact; it is a normative achievement. Desert attaches to the agent precisely *because* the deed expresses a will that is answerable to reasons and that the agent has rationally claimed as their own.

**Frankfurt supplies the complementary point: personhood, hence the locus of desert, is constituted by reflective self-relation, not by causal origination.** What distinguishes a person from a mere wanton is the capacity for *reflective self-evaluation* expressed in second-order volitions; one acts freely, and as oneself, when one's will accords with the desires one *wills* to be moved by — identification (Frankfurt, "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person," 1971). The self that deserves is the *reflectively endorsing* self. Frankfurt explicitly severs this from the regress: he held identification can be wholehearted and decisive without any demand that the agent have authored the springs of identification all the way down. The "who they are" is fixed at the level of reflective endorsement, not at the level of first causes.

**The transcendental clinch: the skeptic's required sense of "source" is not a coherent strengthening of the desert concept — it is incoherent simpliciter.** This is where a priori analysis is decisive. Consider what "ultimate, regress-ending source" would have to be. As the reconstruction's own sub-argument concedes (Variant B, P2d): indeterministic breaks add only *chance*, and chance is not authorship (01-reconstruction.md, lines 108–110). So the skeptic's "ultimate sourcehood" is a notion that *nothing could satisfy* — not a determined agent, not an indeterministic one, not a self-creating one (self-creation being viciously circular). A condition that is *logically* unsatisfiable cannot be what a *working, applicable* concept like desert requires. When we analyze what competent users mean by "she deserves blame for that, given who she is," we find a concept they apply, withhold, and dispute over evidence — paradigmatically by asking whether she acted from her own reasons, whether she could recognize and respond to the considerations against acting, whether she was coerced or compelled or manipulated. *That* is the concept's actual content. The skeptic has substituted a different, empty concept and then triumphantly shown it is empty. The right verdict is not "no one deserves"; it is *that was never the desert concept*.

**Korsgaard makes the normative authority self-standing and immune to the regress demand.** Desert is grounded in agency, and agency is *self-constituting*: a rational being constitutes herself as an agent by acting on principles she reflectively endorses; normativity springs from autonomy — the will's giving of law to itself (Korsgaard, *The Sources of Normativity*, 1996). The authority by which an agent owns her action is the authority of practical reason, which is not the kind of thing that *could* be inherited from a prior cause — it is exercised, here and now, in the deed. To demand that this authority be underwritten by regress-ending causation is to misunderstand the kind of necessity at issue: the bindingness of one's own reasons on one's own will is a normative, not a causal-historical, fact. The is/ought gap cuts *against* the skeptic: no story about causal ancestry (an *is*) can dislodge the normative fact that an agent who acts from reasons she has made her own *answers for* the act and so can deserve in virtue of it.

So: P1 says basic desert requires ultimate sourcehood. The correct analysis of "deserves in virtue of who they are" yields *rational ownership of and guidance-control over a reasons-responsive mechanism* — a condition agents routinely satisfy and the skeptic concedes is satisfiable (the compatibilist sourcehood the thesis itself names at 00-thesis.md lines 39–41). P1 is therefore false. With P1 false, P2 is harmless — a true claim about a condition desert never needed. The conclusion does not follow.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P1**. The rationalist reason: P1 is offered as an analytic/conceptual truth ("basic desert *requires* ultimate sourcehood"), so it stands or falls by conceptual analysis — and analysis shows the requirement is a *substitution*, not a discovery. The desert concept's genuine content is answerability: an action's deserving response in virtue of expressing a will that recognizes and responds to reasons and that the agent has rationally owned. The skeptic conflates two senses of "source" (the thesis itself flags both — buck-stopping vs. reasons-responsive, 00-thesis.md lines 36–41); only the reasons-responsive sense figures in the concept actually deployed, and that sense is satisfiable. The buck-stopping sense, meanwhile, names nothing coherent — neither determinism, indeterminism, nor self-creation could realize it — so it cannot be a *requirement* of any applicable concept. A condition no possible agent could meet is not the hidden essence of a concept we successfully apply every day; it is a philosopher's artifact mistaken for an essence.

I do *not* deny P2, and I do not need to. That is the discipline of this route: I let the metaphysics go and win on the meaning of the word.

## Attack round

**Against the manipulation specialist.** The Plum intuition is unreliable exactly where you lean on it. Case 1 differs from Case 4 in the feature my analysis names: *rational ownership*. The real-time-manipulated agent's reasoning is, at the moment of action, not answerable to *his* reasons — an external will overrides the verdict he could otherwise reach and contest from within. The garden-variety determined agent's reasoning *is* his own deliberative authority, exercised here and now. So the hard-line reply is principled, not stubborn: manipulation defeats desert by defeating rational ownership, not by tracing causal pedigree. Your "no relevant difference" begs the question — it presupposes that only causal history could distinguish the cases, the very claim in dispute. Determination and manipulation differ in answerability, not ancestry.

**Against the phenomenologist.** Fittingness of resentment is necessary, never sufficient. Resentment can *feel* fitting toward a hypnotized or deeply conditioned agent who never rationally owned the deed — the attitude fires; that does not make blame *deserved*. Without a rational-ownership condition you cannot separate desert from a merely conditioned reaction, and your own appeal to "quality of will" already smuggles in what I supply: a will answerable to reasons. You owe an account of *why* the participant stance is correct, not merely that we stand in it. That "why" is normative — and normativity is not exhausted by describing a stance we happen to occupy. Mere standing is not warrant.

**Against the naturalist.** You assert P2 and demand assent *because the evidence supports it*. That "because" is a normative claim — that one ought to proportion belief to evidence — and no causal story about genes and upbringing delivers its bindingness; the is/ought gap is yours to close. If reasoning is merely caused, your conclusion is caused too, carrying no more rational authority than a sneeze. You cannot consistently demand assent on rational grounds while denying that rational grounds bind. Your asserting presupposes the very autonomous normativity that grounds rational ownership — and hence desert. Self-undermining is fatal.
