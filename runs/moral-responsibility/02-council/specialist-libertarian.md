# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Specialist: libertarian (council)
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.
> **Seat:** Specialist: libertarian — summoned specialist
> **Defending:** POSITION: Libertarian about free will in the Robert Kane (event-causal) / Timothy O'Connor (agent-causal) register — we possess the ultimate sourcehood basic desert requires because libertarian agency genuinely breaks the causal regress.

---

POSITION: Libertarian about free will in the Robert Kane (event-causal) / Timothy O'Connor (agent-causal) register — we possess the ultimate sourcehood basic desert requires because libertarian agency genuinely breaks the causal regress.
VERDICT: The thesis is false: I concede P1 — basic desert does require ultimate sourcehood — and deny P2, because at least some human choices (Kanean self-forming actions, or O'Connor-style agent-caused decisions) make us genuine ultimate sources, so the regress the skeptic relies on is in fact broken.

## Case

I accept the skeptic's own terms. P1 is correct and I will not weaken it: basic desert — blame or praise deserved *just because of what the agent did and who they are*, even in a world where the response does no good — does require that the agent be the buck-stopping originator of the action, the one in whom the explanation bottoms out rather than passing through to genes, upbringing, or chance. This is exactly Kane's condition of **Ultimate Responsibility (UR)**: an agent is ultimately responsible for an action only if the agent is responsible for anything that is a *sufficient reason, cause, or motive* for the action's occurring; if some sufficient ground of the act traces wholly to factors the agent did not produce, the buck has not stopped with the agent (Kane, *The Significance of Free Will*, 1996; SEP, "Incompatibilist (Nondeterministic) Theories of Free Will"). Conceding P1 is not a tactical retreat — it is the whole reason libertarianism exists. We agree with the skeptic about *what desert requires* and disagree only about whether anyone *meets* the requirement.

So the entire dispute is P2. P2 says no agent ever satisfies UR because the regress (P2a–P2d in the reconstruction) cannot be completed and indeterminism only adds chance. I deny P2 on two complementary models.

**Kane's event-causal model: self-forming actions break the regress without surrendering authorship.** The regress argument is decisive *only against a deterministic agent*, who inherits every sufficient condition from prior causes she did not produce. But UR does not require that *every* act be regress-ending. It requires only that *some* acts in a life history be undetermined "regress-stopping voluntary actions" — what Kane calls **self-forming actions (SFAs)** — through which the agent forms the very character from which her later determined acts flow (SEP; Kane, *A Contemporary Introduction to Free Will*, 2005). SFAs occur in moments of motivational conflict — Kane's businesswoman, late for a meeting, who sees an assault and is torn between the moral pull to stop and the prudential pull to rush on. Here the will is genuinely divided. The skeptic's P2c assumes character must be inherited or chosen on a *prior* character ad infinitum; the SFA blocks this because at the moment of torn decision the agent does not yet have a settled character that determines the outcome — she is *making* it.

The naturalist will now press the luck objection: if the choice is undetermined, then holding everything fixed up to the moment, it could have gone either way for no further reason, so the outcome is *chance* — noise, not authorship. This is the crux, and Kane answers it head-on with **plural voluntary control** via **dual efforts of will**. In the torn decision the agent is *simultaneously trying* to make the moral choice and trying to make the self-interested choice — there are two indeterminate efforts running in parallel (Kane's "parallel processing"; SEP). The indeterminism is located *in the effort itself*. So whichever way the choice resolves, it is the success of *something the agent was actively trying to do*, done for her own reasons, voluntarily, and on purpose. That is why it is not luck in the responsibility-cancelling sense. Kane's analogy: a man trying to break a glass table by pounding it succeeds in breaking it *through his own effort* even if the precise molecular path was indeterministic and he might (by chance) have failed — the indeterminism does not erase that *he* did it and *meant* to. The agent satisfies the **plurality conditions**: whichever option is chosen, it is chosen voluntarily, intentionally, and rationally — for reasons the agent then and there *makes her own* by choosing them (Kane 1996; SEP). The "luck" objection equivocates: it slides from "the outcome was not determined" to "the agent did not do it," but indeterminacy of outcome is compatible with full voluntary authorship of the trying.

**O'Connor's agent-causal model: a stronger guarantee of sourcehood.** Even if one doubts whether event-causal libertarianism fully secures authorship, agent-causation closes the gap. On O'Connor's view (*Persons and Causes*, 2000), a free decision is caused not by a prior *event* but by the **agent herself**, a persisting substance with an irreducible causal power. The agent "is quite literally an ultimate source or origin of her action" (SEP, paraphrasing O'Connor): she causes her decision, and *neither* the agent's causing of it *nor* the decision itself is causally determined by prior events. The agent stands at the *head* of the causal chain — she is "not caused to cause" her intention. This directly answers the **disappearing-agent** version of the luck objection. The skeptic and the naturalist say indeterminism leaves a gap where nothing settles the outcome, so the agent vanishes into chance. O'Connor's reply: the gap is not filled by chance and not by a prior event — it is filled *by the agent as substance-cause*. There is no "extra" event that must itself be explained, hence no further regress: substance-causation is a basic ontological category, not a chain of events demanding prior links. The buck stops with the agent because the agent, not an event, is the terminus of explanation. That is precisely the buck-stopping originator P1 demands.

Both models converge on the same denial of P2: the regress is completed *by the agent*, either by undetermined self-forming willings (Kane) or by an agent-causal power that is itself the unmoved origin (O'Connor). Either suffices to falsify "no agent is *ever* an ultimate source" — and recall the thesis is universal, so a single self-forming choice in a single life refutes it.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P2** — specifically the sub-premises P2c and P2d.

- **P2c** (character must be chosen on the basis of a prior character, launching an uncompletable regress) is false because self-forming actions are precisely the points where character is *originated* rather than *inherited*. The regress presupposes determinism; in an SFA there is no fully formed prior character that fixes the outcome, so there is nothing for the regress to climb back through. The agent is, at that moment, a first mover of her own will.

- **P2d** (indeterministic breaks add only chance, not authorship) is the load-bearing skeptical claim, and it is false. Its plausibility rests on picturing indeterminism as a randomizer *between* the agent and the act — an external dice-roll. But Kane locates the indeterminism *inside* a dual, goal-directed effort the agent is making, so the undetermined outcome is still the success of the agent's own trying for her own reasons; and O'Connor locates the origination *in the agent's substance-causal power*, so what fills the indeterministic gap is not chance but the agent herself. P2d trades on the false dichotomy "either determined-and-inherited, or undetermined-and-random." Libertarian agency is the third option the skeptic's argument never refutes — it only *assumes* it away.

What I concede, pointedly: **P1 is true.** Against the compatibilists who will deny P1, I side with the skeptic — reasons-responsive ownership of a mechanism the agent did not ultimately author is *not* enough for desert *for its own sake*; it leaves the buck resting on factors outside the agent. The skeptic and I agree on the requirement. We disagree only on a matter of fact about agency, and on that matter the skeptic has given an argument (the regress) that holds only on the assumption of determinism plus the false dichotomy about indeterminism — neither of which is established.

## Attack round

**Against the naturalist.** You defend P2 by demanding I "produce the causal node" inherited from nothing prior — but that demand silently assumes the causal closure of the physical: that every explanation must terminate in a *prior event* in the natural causal stream. That is the very point at issue. O'Connor's agent-causation is not a placeholder for a missing event-cause; it denies that event-causation is the only causal category. You cannot refute it by insisting it produce an event-cause — that begs the question against the ontology proposed. Your "one world, the natural causal one" is the conclusion you owe an argument for, not a premise you may help yourself to. Strip the closure assumption and the regress simply stops: it ran only on inputs you assumed rather than established.

**Against the manipulation specialist.** Your four cases run on covert determination — at every stage Plum's decision is *sufficiently caused* by factors he did not produce (the device, the program, the training). So the no-relevant-difference chain compares determined agents to determined agents and concludes, unsurprisingly, that all lack sourcehood. But a Kanean self-forming action has *no prior sufficient cause*: at the torn moment nothing settles the outcome but the agent's own dual effort. Case 4, as you describe it, is just Case 1 with the manipulator deleted — still fully determined — so your argument never touches the libertarian agent at all. The analogy to manipulation breaks precisely where determination is absent. You have shown determined agents lack desert; I agree. You have not shown that *agents* do.

**Against the rationalist and phenomenologist.** You save the *word* "desert" only by redefining it. The rationalist grounds it in "rational ownership of a reasons-responsive mechanism the agent did not ultimately author"; the phenomenologist in "fittingness inside the reactive stance." But ordinary blame presupposes *buck-stopping authorship* — that the deed traces back to the agent and stops there, not to a mechanism or a stance the agent inherited. Concede, as you both do, that no one is the ultimate source, and the resentment you license becomes resentment of someone for being the conduit of causes he did not lay down — the very thing the manipulation intuition exposes as unfitting. You keep the honorific and discard what made it more than a useful feeling, which is exactly why I, with the skeptic, hold P1 against you.
