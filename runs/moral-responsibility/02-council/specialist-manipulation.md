# Basic-Desert Moral Responsibility — Specialist: manipulation (council)
> **Run:** moral-responsibility · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** No one is ever morally responsible in the basic-desert sense, because basic desert requires being the ultimate source of one's action, and no one is ever the ultimate source of what they do.
> **Seat:** Specialist: manipulation — summoned specialist
> **Defending:** POSITION: Hard incompatibilist, Pereboom register — defending P1 (basic desert requires ultimate sourcehood) via the four-case manipulation argument.

POSITION: Hard incompatibilist, Pereboom register — defending P1 (basic desert requires ultimate sourcehood) via the four-case manipulation argument.
VERDICT: The thesis is true: the manipulation argument shows that every compatibilist condition can be met without basic desert, so basic desert requires a sourcehood we never have, and no one is ever morally responsible in that sense.

## Case

My assigned target is P1 — the conceptual premise that basic-desert responsibility *requires* the agent be the ultimate source of the action. The compatibilist (Fischer–Ravizza, Frankfurt, Sartorio) grants P2 cheerfully: of course no one stops the causal regress. Their whole game is to deny P1 — to insist that the right *kind* of ownership of one's reasons-responsive mechanism is sufficient for desert, no regress-ending origination needed. The four-case argument is built precisely to break that move. It does not assert P1 by stipulation; it *earns* P1 by exhibiting an agent who satisfies every compatibilist sufficiency condition and still, on reflection, lacks basic desert — so the condition that is actually doing the work, the thing whose absence explains the missing desert, can only be sourcehood.

First, fix what is at stake. Pereboom's basic desert is precise: an agent is morally responsible "in such a way that she would deserve to be blamed if she understood that it was morally wrong" — and deserve it "just because she has performed the action, given an understanding of its moral status," not by way of any consequentialist or contractualist payoff (Pereboom, *Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life*, 2014; SEP, "Skepticism About Moral Responsibility"). The blame would be deserved even in a world where it deterred nothing and repaired nothing. That is the only sense the thesis denies, and it is the only sense the manipulation argument is engineered to attack.

Now the argument. Professor Plum kills Ms. White, and in each case his decision satisfies the leading compatibilist conditions: it issues from a moderately **reasons-responsive** mechanism (he is sensitive to moral and prudential reasons and would have refrained had reasons been sufficiently different); it accords with his character; he **identifies** with the egoistic desire on which he acts via second-order endorsement (Frankfurt's condition); and no desire he acts on is irresistible (Pereboom, 2014; Philosophical Disquisitions summary).

- **Case 1 (direct, real-time manipulation):** Neuroscientists, by radio-like technology, locally manipulate Plum's reasoning process moment-to-moment so that it is *strongly egoistic*, deterministically producing the decision to kill. The manipulation works through his ordinary reasons-responsive faculties — it does not bypass them. He satisfies every compatibilist condition. Yet he is intuitively *not* a candidate for basic desert: he does not non-instrumentally deserve blame just for what he did. He is a victim of the device.

- **Case 2 (programming at the beginning of life):** The same neuroscientists program Plum at the start of his life so that his reasoning is regularly, but not exceptionlessly, egoistic, and this programming causally determines the present decision. The current-time neural realization is identical to Case 1; only the causal history is shifted earlier.

- **Case 3 (ordinary upbringing, rigorous training):** No neuroscientists. The "rigorous training practices" of Plum's home and community, acting before he could resist, instill the same egoistic character, which then deterministically yields the decision.

- **Case 4 (garden-variety determinism):** Plum is an ordinary human in a deterministic universe, normally raised, with the same reasons-responsive but egoistic character producing the same decision.

The conceptual force lies in the **no-relevant-difference / best-explanation** move. Pereboom's claim is that "there are no differences between Cases 1 and 2, 2 and 3, and 3 and 4 that can explain in a principled way why he would not be responsible in the former of each pair but would be in the latter" (Pereboom, 2014). The candidate differences are all impotent:

1. *Manipulation by an agent vs. by no agent.* But responsibility is not undermined by there being a manipulator; it is undermined by Plum's being **produced by factors he played no part in producing**. Replace the neuroscientists in Case 1 with a spontaneously generated neural device that does exactly the same work, and the intuition that he lacks desert is untouched. So the presence of a manipulating agent was never the operative feature.

2. *Local/real-time vs. historical causation.* Case 2 already collapses this: identical present-time mechanism, earlier cause. If present-time reasons-responsiveness were sufficient for desert, Case 2 Plum would be responsible — but he is not, for the same reason as Case 1.

3. *Ignorance of one's causal history.* In Cases 3 and 4 Plum need not know how he came to be as he is. But epistemic access to one's etiology is not a desert-maker; it changes nothing about whether the buck of explanation stops with him.

This is where the **best-explanation** formulation closes the argument and directly delivers P1. Across Cases 1–3 there is a single feature that uniformly explains the absence of basic desert: in each, Plum's action traces back, without remainder, to factors he did not produce — a device, a program, a training regime. That very feature is *also present in Case 4*: deterministic causes reaching back before his birth. The most parsimonious explanation of the shared verdict is the shared feature. Therefore the absence of basic desert in Case 4 has the same source as in Case 1 (Pereboom, 2014; SEP). And the contrapositive is exactly P1: the only thing that could have made the difference — the only thing whose presence would underwrite desert — is Plum's being the **ultimate source**, the originator from whom the explanation does not flow through to prior unowned causes. Reasons-responsiveness, identification, and guidance control are all present in Case 1 and all insufficient. So basic desert requires more than mechanism-ownership; it requires sourcehood. P1 stands.

Notice what this does to the compatibilist's denial of P1. The compatibilist cannot say "Case 1 Plum lacks an *additional* ownership condition," because Pereboom builds the cases so that whatever ownership condition the compatibilist names — Frankfurt's identification, Fischer–Ravizza's "taking responsibility" for one's mechanism, Sartorio's actual-sequence reasons-sensitivity — is *stipulated to obtain* in Case 1. The dialectic is a vise: either Case 1 Plum is a full basic-desert candidate (the hard-line reply, which I address below), or he is not, in which case the no-difference chain runs all the way to Case 4 and P1 is vindicated.

## What I deny and why

**I deny the soft-line reply**, which concedes Case 1 Plum lacks desert but claims a *principled* difference excuses him while the ordinary determined agent is responsible. Pereboom's rejoinder is decisive and is the heart of defending P1: any difference the soft-liner proposes (a non-manipulated history, "taking responsibility" over time, a self that was not externally installed) can be neutralized by **revising the vignette** so that the manipulated Plum *also* satisfies it, while the manipulation intuition survives — or else the proposed condition turns out to apply equally to the ordinary determined agent, who also did not produce the factors that produced him (Pereboom, 2014; SEP). The soft-liner is chasing a difference that, once it is genuinely desert-relevant, the determined agent lacks just as much as the manipulated one. There is no stable resting place between "manipulated Plum lacks desert" and "determined Plum lacks desert."

**I deny the hard-line reply** — the claim (e.g., McKenna, Jeppsson) that Case 1 Plum *is* morally responsible because he meets the compatibilist conditions, so the argument's first intuition is simply mistaken. Two points. First, the hard-liner pays an enormous intuitive price: they must affirm that an agent whose every reasoning step was just now installed by an external device, against the grain of who he would otherwise be, non-instrumentally *deserves* blame just for what he did — deserves suffering for its own sake. That verdict is far less credible than the premise it is meant to unseat. Second, and more importantly, the hard-liner's move actually concedes my conceptual point and relocates the fight to P2's terrain: if mechanism-ownership genuinely sufficed for desert even under total external installation, then "ownership" has been redefined so thinly that it no longer tracks anything desert could plausibly be *about*. The compatibilist wins the word "responsible" and loses the thing — *basic* desert, the non-instrumental deservingness of a response just because of the deed. My thesis denies exactly that thing, and the hard-liner has not produced a case for it; they have only refused to feel its absence.

**What I do not deny.** I grant — indeed insist — that the thesis leaves untouched forward-looking responsibility (deterrence, protection, moral formation), attributability (the act is genuinely Plum's), and even a deflated Strawsonian fittingness of reactive attitudes shorn of their desert-presupposing core (Pereboom, *Living Without Free Will*, 2001, ch. 7). Hard incompatibilism is not nihilism. It denies one thing precisely: that anyone ever *intrinsically deserves* blame or praise. The manipulation argument shows that the only candidate ground for that intrinsic desert is a sourcehood the cases reveal we never have.

## Attack round

**Against the rationalist.** You say Case 1 Plum lacks *rational ownership*, since an external will "overrides the verdict he could otherwise reach." But re-read the case: the device works *through* his reasons-responsive faculties, producing a deliberation that *is* moderately reasons-responsive and that he reflectively endorses — your guidance-control and Fischer–Ravizza "taking responsibility" are stipulated to obtain. There is no overriding from outside the deliberation; the deliberation itself is the manipulated thing. So manipulated Plum satisfies rational ownership *and* lacks desert. Either you add a no-prior-manipulation clause — which is just sourcehood renamed, and which the ordinary determined agent equally fails — or you bite the bullet that an installed will deserves suffering for its own sake. Answerability without authorship cannot ground desert.

**Against the phenomenologist.** Your own engine indicts you. The four-case argument *operates* by your reactive attitudes: resentment toward Plum drains away as you learn his etiology. You call that a relational fact about whether "you-and-I" obtains — but Case 2's programming and Case 4's determinism *equally* sever buck-stopping origination while leaving the second-personal address intact, and your withdrawal tracks the *etiology*, not the presence of a manipulator. That is the tell: the practice withdraws fittingness precisely on learning of unowned sourcing. So the participant stance *presupposes* a sourcehood condition it cannot state in its own terms. "It feels fitting" is a datum about the attitude, never a warrant for desert.

**Against the libertarian.** You concede P1 — thank you — so we fight only on P2, and indeterminism still buys no authorship. Kane's dual efforts relocate the chance, they do not abolish it: *which* effort succeeds is settled by nothing the agent controls, so the torn agent is, in Pereboom's phrase, "unable to *settle*" the outcome. The table-breaker controls *that* he tries, never *which* trying wins. O'Connor's substance-cause merely names the gap "agent" and declares the regress closed by fiat — but a power exercised for no determining reason is, for purposes of desert, indistinguishable from luck. Authorship requires the outcome flow *from* the agent; indeterminism guarantees it partly does not.

**Against the naturalist.** Ally, not rival — but you leave P1 under-defended. You rest everything on P2 and treat P1 as mere stipulation of what "deserve for its own sake" means, conceding the compatibilist the word and reserving "basic desert" by definition. That is too cheap: the compatibilist will say you have *assumed* P1, not *earned* it, and Dennett answers your "produce the causal node" demand directly — the node is the reasons-responsive system. Only the four-case argument earns P1, by exhibiting an agent who meets every mechanism-ownership condition and still lacks desert, forcing sourcehood as the operative ground. The Basic Argument alone does not close that gap; the manipulation cases do.
