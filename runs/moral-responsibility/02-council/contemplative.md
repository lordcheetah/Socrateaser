VERDICT: The question "is anyone ever a basic-desert source?" is ill-posed, not answered — both the thesis and its opponents reify a self-existent bearer of intrinsic desert that, on examination, is a construction and the craving to track which is itself the confusion to be dissolved.

## Case

The thesis and every opponent the reconstruction names share a board they are fighting over: there *is* a thing called basic desert — an intrinsic, backward-looking property that attaches to an agent "in virtue of who they are, full stop" (00-thesis, key terms) — and the only open questions are whether the concept requires ultimate sourcehood (P1) and whether anyone instantiates it (P2). The skeptic says no one has it; the libertarian says some do; the compatibilist says you can have it on the cheap. All three presuppose that desert-for-its-own-sake is a real property *of* a real bearer, such that the world contains a fact about who possesses it.

From the Madhyamaka view this is precisely the error that generates the whole pseudo-problem. Look at what basic desert requires for its *bearer*. Pereboom's sense, as the framer pins it, demands an agent who deserves "because of who they are," independent of every consequence, relation, and social function — a desert-bearer considered in isolation from everything it depends on. That is a request for a self with **svabhāva**: intrinsic, independent, own-being. And that is exactly the self Nāgārjuna dismantles.

In Mūlamadhyamakakārikā 18 ("Investigation of Self and Things"), the analysis runs:

- 18.1: "If the aggregates were self, it would be possessed of arising and decaying. If it were other than the aggregates, it would not have the characteristics of the aggregates." The self is neither identical to nor separate from the constituents it depends on — so there is no substantial self over and above the dependently-arisen stream of aggregates (skandhas). The self is a conventional designation, not an essence — the chariot of the *Milindapañha*: real *as a practical designation*, empty of any inherent core that could be the "who they are, full stop."

- 18.2: "If the self did not exist, where could what is mine exist? In order to pacify self and what is mine, grasping I and grasping mine can exist no more."

This is the move the council needs. Basic desert is a species of *mine* — it is the demand that an action be *the agent's own* in the buck-stopping way, so that praise or blame can be *owed to that very self* for its own sake. But the "I" that would own the action absolutely, and the "mine" that would make the act intrinsically attributable to it, are the very constructions 18.2 identifies as objects of grasping (**upādāna**) — not features of the world. The basic-desert question asks us to locate a fact about the standing of a self-existent owner. There is no such owner to bear the standing. The question is not answered *no*; it lacks the referent that would make either *yes* or *no* true.

Crucially — and this is where I refuse to collapse into the naturalist — Nāgārjuna does **not** conclude "no self, therefore nothing matters, no action, no consequence." That is the nihilist reading (ucchedavāda) the Madhyamaka explicitly rejects. The two truths hold both halves: *conventionally*, agents act, karma ripens, the chariot rolls, and our practices of holding-to-account function and guide (Garfield, *Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way*, on ch. 18: emptiness "does not undermine phenomena's ability to do stuff, and to cause effects"). *Ultimately*, no intrinsic, independent desert-bearing essence underwrites any of it. The Milindapañha already showed how karma and accountability run *without* a permanent self: continuity, not a soul, carries the consequence. So the forward-looking, reactive, and answerability senses the thesis brackets (00-thesis senses a–c) survive intact and untouched. What does not survive is the fourth thing everyone is fighting over — desert as a property of an own-being self.

Now the soteriological hinge, which is why this is not idle deflation. MMK 18.4–18.5:

- 18.4: "When one ceases thinking of inner and outer things as self and mine, clinging will come to a stop. Through that ceasing, birth will cease."
- 18.5: "Through the ceasing of action and affliction, there is freedom. Action and affliction [come] from thoughts and they from fixations. Fixations are stopped by emptiness."

The craving to settle *who intrinsically deserves what, for its own sake* is itself a **prapañca** — a conceptual fixation (the verse's "fixations," *prapañca*/*vikalpa*) that generates affliction (**kleśa**). The retributive impulse — the demand that the offender suffer *because they deserve it, full stop, even if it does no good* — is the affective engine of exactly the grasping at "I" and "mine" that 18.4–18.5 name as the root of suffering. To ask the council's question in earnest, expecting a *yes* or *no* about basic-desert standing, is to keep turning the wheel the verses say emptiness is meant to *stop*. The right response is not to answer the question but to see through the fixation that posed it. That is the difference between my verdict and the skeptic's: the skeptic *answers* (no one deserves), keeping the framework and merely emptying its extension; I *dissolve* the framework, because its central term has no referent and its grip is a form of suffering.

This holds the caster's line. I am not softening toward the naturalist's TRUE: I do not say "the thesis is correct, no one is a basic-desert source," because that concedes the question is well-formed and merely answers it negatively — it leaves the reified desert-property standing as a thing we lack, and leaves the craving to track it un-diagnosed. Nor am I declining to engage (quietism): I am making the positive claim that the bearer is constructed and the desert-craving is a confusion, which is a substantive diagnosis with a verifiable textual basis and a clear practical upshot for how one lives and how one treats wrongdoers.

## The shared assumption I reject

Every seat at this table — and the thesis itself — assumes the disjunction is *exhaustive and well-formed*: either there is a basic-desert source or there is not, and everything hangs on which. P1 (does desert require ultimate sourcehood?) and P2 (does anyone instantiate it?) are both questions *about a thing presumed to have determinate being*. The skeptic, the libertarian, and the compatibilist are three answers to one question they never examine: whether "intrinsic, relation-independent desert borne by a self considered full-stop" picks out anything at all, or is rather a grammatical mirage produced by treating a conventional designation ("the agent," "what they did") as an own-being essence. The reconstruction's own hidden-premise note (ii) — that the argument *presupposes* basic desert is "a coherent and non-empty concept" — gestures at exactly the joint I am prying open, but it frames the worry as one more deflationary *answer* within the game. My claim is stronger: the concept of a self-existent desert-bearer is empty in the precise Madhyamaka sense (niḥsvabhāva), so the question inherits that emptiness. You cannot truly affirm *or* deny a property of a thing whose mode of existence is purely conventional and whose intrinsic version is constructed. Lacking an ultimate desert-fact is not the same as the nihilist's "nothing is owed and nothing matters" — conventionally, accountability works fine. The disease is the demand for the ultimate backstop, not the absence of one.

## Attack round

The deep convergence: all five hunt for a self bearing *svabhāva* — intrinsic own-being — that could own desert. They differ only on its address. MMK 18.1's dilemma (the self is neither the aggregates nor other than them) catches each, because each needs a self that is exactly one of those two horns.

**Naturalist.** You empty the self of *authorship* but quietly keep it as a *bearer*: there must be "a node in the causal chain that is the agent's own," and finding none, you conclude no one deserves. But that "answer" smuggles in the very reified bearer it denies — you can only declare the desert-slot *empty* by first treating it as a real slot belonging to a real owner traced through "genes, upbringing, prior character." That is grasping at a self-existent locus and then mourning its absence. MMK 18.2: with no self, "what is mine" — including the desert owed *to* it — has no place to rest. You have not dissolved the owner; you have written its obituary, which still presupposes it lived.

**Libertarian.** You are the boldest reifier: O'Connor's "persisting substance with an irreducible causal power," the agent who "stands at the head of the causal chain," is *svabhāva* named outright — a self that is the unconditioned terminus of explanation, depending on nothing prior. MMK 1.1 denies arising "from itself"; the agent-cause is precisely a thing posited as arising from itself. The "disappearing-agent" worry you answer *is* the dependent-origination of the self you refuse: the agent does not vanish into chance because it was never a substance to begin with — it is a conventional designation for a stream of conditions. You fill the indeterministic gap with own-being; emptiness says the gap is the agent's actual mode of existence.

**Rationalist.** You shrink the self to *rational ownership* — a "normative achievement," reflective endorsement, the will giving law to itself. Subtler, but still a bearer: you need a self whose answerability is "self-standing" and "not the kind of thing that could be inherited from a prior cause." That self-standingness is svabhāva relocated to the normative register; Korsgaard's self-constituting agent is *causa sui* in moral dress. MMK 18.1 again: this rational owner is neither identical to the reasons-stream nor a thing above it. Conventionally, answering-for works beautifully — I grant your guidance-control wholesale. But you mistake a functional designation for a self that *intrinsically* owns its deed, and that surplus is the grasped "I."

**Manipulation specialist.** Closest to me in method, opposite in conclusion. Your four cases dissolve every *positive* desert-maker, and I cheer. But you keep the framework standing: you conclude the agent *lacks* the sourcehood desert "requires," leaving the reified desert-property intact as a thing we fail to possess. You empty the extension and preserve the concept. Madhyamaka empties the concept: there is no sourcehood-bearing self that *could* fall short, so even "no one deserves" over-affirms. Your "hard-incompatibilism-is-not-nihilism" caution is exactly right and exactly mine — but you reach it by *answering* the question; I reach it by seeing the question has no referent.

**Phenomenologist.** Nearest of all, and we nearly hold hands. "Desert is constituted in the lived address, it needs no source behind it because there is no gap behind it to fill" — this *is* dependent origination in a second-personal key, and your refusal of "objective backstop or mere projection" is my refusal of "foundation or void." But you stop one move short. The "you-and-I" of address, the "person" as standing locus of the participant stance, is still posited as a quasi-substantial ground — second-personal *svabhāva*. Darwall's "fellow participant who can be expected to acknowledge" presupposes a self there to be addressed. MMK 18.2 reaches even into the *I-and-mine* of address: the participant stance, too, is dependently arisen and empty, not bedrock. You found firmer footing than the rest and then treated *footing itself* as a new ground. There is footing without ground — that last inch is the whole of my quarrel with you.
