# Cosmic Backstop for Morality — Contemplative (council)
> **Run:** cosmic-backstop · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** There is no accessible cosmic backstop for morality, because warrant requires causal/practical contact and a mind-independent normative ground is by its nature sealed off from such contact.
> **Seat:** Contemplative — Madhyamaka Buddhist, Nāgārjuna
> **Defending:** VERDICT: The thesis's conclusion is correct at the ultimate level — there is no svabhāva-bearing backstop to find — but its argument and its anxiety both grow from a single confusion: it treats "ground" as something morality must have *behind* it, and so reads the absence of an inherent ground as a loss; on the Madhyamaka analysis there was never such a thing to lose, morality functions perfectly well as a dependently-arisen convention, and the craving in P1 is the disease, not the diagnosis.

---

VERDICT: The thesis's conclusion is correct at the ultimate level — there is no svabhāva-bearing backstop to find — but its argument and its anxiety both grow from a single confusion: it treats "ground" as something morality must have *behind* it, and so reads the absence of an inherent ground as a loss; on the Madhyamaka analysis there was never such a thing to lose, morality functions perfectly well as a dependently-arisen convention, and the craving in P1 is the disease, not the diagnosis.

## Case

I accept the conclusion and reject the framing. Let me show how both can be true.

### 1. There is no svabhāva backstop — and this is not a discovery the thesis can claim as bad news

Nāgārjuna's whole project is to show that nothing has *svabhāva* — own-being, self-existent intrinsic nature that holds independently of conditions and relations. A "cosmic backstop" in the thesis's *robust* sense (00-thesis: "authority does not depend on us, our practices, or our constitution") is precisely a thing posited to have svabhāva in the normative register: a ground that *is what it is* in total independence of the practice it underwrites. So far the thesis and I agree: no such thing is available.

But for Nāgārjuna this is not a special fact about transcendent moral grounds. It is the universal fact about *everything*. Tables, fire, the self, the Buddha, nirvāṇa, and emptiness itself are all equally empty of svabhāva (MMK 24.18; the emptiness of emptiness, 13.7–8). The thesis writes as though morality has been singled out and found to be missing a support that other things possess. Nothing possesses it. To notice that morality lacks an inherent ground is to notice that morality is like everything else — dependently arisen — not that it has been orphaned.

### 2. The two truths: morality functions conventionally without any ultimate ground

The load-bearing move is Nāgārjuna's distinction of two truths (MMK 24.8–9):

> "The Dharma taught by the buddhas rests on two truths: a truth of worldly convention (saṃvṛti-satya) and an ultimate truth (paramārtha-satya). Those who do not understand the distinction between these two truths do not understand the profound reality in the Buddha's teaching." (MMK 24.8–9)

These are not two *realms*, one real and one fake. They are two ways the same dependently-arisen phenomenon is true. Conventionally, the table holds your cup; ultimately, "table" is empty of own-being. Both hold without competition — and Nāgārjuna insists the ultimate is *only accessible through* the conventional: "Without relying on convention, the ultimate is not taught; without reaching the ultimate, nirvāṇa is not attained" (MMK 24.10).

Applied to morality: conventionally, cruelty is worse than kindness, promises bind, the bodhisattva's vow to relieve suffering is *more correct* than indifference. These judgments guide action, can be done better or worse, and are answerable to how things go. *Ultimately*, none of them rests on a self-existent normative fact. The thesis assumes these two claims are in tension — that if there is no ultimate ground, the conventional verdicts must be "mere projection" (00-thesis flags exactly this forced choice as the pseudo-dispute). Madhyamaka denies the tension flat. The conventional is not *demoted* by being empty; emptiness is *what makes it work*.

This is Nāgārjuna's startling inversion (MMK 24.14):

> "For whom emptiness is possible, everything is possible. For whom emptiness is not possible, nothing is possible."

And its converse warning (MMK 24.20): "If all this were non-empty, there would be no arising and ceasing" — a thing with svabhāva is frozen, unconditioned, unable to enter into the relations of cause, learning, repair, and moral growth. It is *precisely the demand for an inherent ground* that would make morality impossible, because an inherent moral fact, sealed from all contact (the thesis's own P3–P4), could do no work in any actual deliberation. The thesis half-sees this in charitable variant 3 ("an inaccessible ground is inert") — but reads inertness as a defect of *this* ground rather than as the giveaway that the *whole picture of grounding-from-behind* was confused.

### 3. Emptiness is not nihilism — refusing that inference is the point

The thesis's animating worry (and the pseudo-dispute it must clear) is that "no accessible backstop" slides into "nothing is better or worse, anything goes." Nāgārjuna anticipated exactly this charge — his opponent in MMK 24 accuses him of destroying the Four Noble Truths, karma, the Saṅgha, all of it (24.1–6) — and his answer is that *the accuser has misunderstood emptiness as nothingness*. Emptiness of svabhāva is not non-existence; it is dependent existence. As Jay Garfield argues, Madhyamaka is "not nihilism" precisely because to see something as empty is to see it *as conventional, as dependent* — not as illusory or unreal (Garfield, "Madhyamaka Is Not Nihilism," 2015). Nāgārjuna's own diagnosis: to mistake emptiness for nothingness is the fatal error — "emptiness wrongly grasped is like a snake wrongly held" (MMK 24.11).

So the inference the thesis fears — no ultimate ground, therefore no normative footing — is exactly the inference Nāgārjuna spent Chapter 24 dismantling. Worse, he argues it runs backwards: it is the *foundationalist*, not the emptiness-theorist, who threatens morality. One who "undermines emptiness undermines all the conventions of the world" (MMK 24.36) — because to insist that morality must have svabhāva or be worthless is to make its actual, functioning, conventional reality hostage to a fantasy that can never be cashed. Lacking an ultimate backstop is not lacking footing. The footing was always conventional, relational, dependent — and that is sturdy enough to live and die by.

### 4. The craving itself is the suffering

This is not merely theoretical. The demand in P1 — that judgments need an accessible *ground* "behind" them to confer warrant — is a form of grasping (upādāna) for fixity in a field that is irreducibly relational. The Stoics reach a parallel resting place from a different road: Epictetus' *Enchiridion* opens by sorting what is "up to us" from what is not, and counsels that serenity comes from ceasing to demand that the cosmos supply guarantees on our terms (Ench. 1). Marcus, likewise: "Live according to nature" — not according to a backstop standing outside nature (Meditations, e.g. 4.3, 10.6). The Zhuangzi presses the same against the rationalist's confidence that disputation reaches a foundation that settles the "this/that" (shì/fēi): the cook Ding cuts well not by grasping a fixed principle but by moving with the grain of things (Zhuangzi, ch. 3, "The Secret of Caring for Life"; ch. 2, "On the Equality of Things"). Across all three, the move is the same: the search for an external guarantor is itself the knot, and untying it does not leave you adrift — it lets you respond.

## The shared assumption I reject

All four voices — the thesis included — share a foundationalist grammar: *either* morality has a ground (natural, a priori, divine, or constitutive) *or* it is left dangling, and everything hangs on which. The naturalist and realist fight over *what kind* of ground; the phenomenologist relocates the ground into the lived relation; even the thesis, in denying a backstop, concedes the framing by treating its absence as a *lack*. The word "access" in P1–P2 already encodes it: access is a relation *across a gap* between a knower here and a ground over there.

I deny the gap, and with it the grammar. On the two-truths analysis there is no knower-over-here standing in or failing to stand in a contact-relation to a ground-over-there; there is a dependently-arisen field of practice in which "judgment," "warrant," and "norm" all arise together, none of them prior, none of them a foundation for the rest (MMK 24.18: the dependently-arisen, emptiness, and conventional designation are one). The thesis's P4 — "whatever is in contact with us is thereby part of the natural/practical order, hence not transcendent" — is *correct*, but it is not the bad news the thesis takes it for. It is the good news, badly worded: there is nothing transcendent because there is nothing with svabhāva *anywhere*, and morality, being empty, joins everything else in working *because* of that emptiness, not despite it. The error is not in any premise; it is in the prior assumption that not-finding-a-backstop is a deprivation. You cannot be deprived of what could never have existed and which, had it existed, would have done no work.

## Attack round

The common error is not which ground each names but that each still needs a *svabhāva*-bearing terminus — a stopping-place whose authority holds of itself (MMK 24.16: to grasp things as having own-being is to see them "as without cause and condition"). Three idioms of grasping (upādāna) at fixity:

**Naturalist (nature).** You unmask the realist's skyhook beautifully, then quietly install a crane that *bottoms out*: the "tracking condition" treats the causal-evolutionary-practical order as the self-standing ground that confers or withholds warrant. But that order is itself dependently arisen, empty of own-being — fire, selection, faculties, all without svabhāva (MMK 1, 10). You have not escaped grounding-from-behind; you have renamed the ground "nature" and made it the one thing not asked to explain *its* own reliability. The regress you press on the realist applies to you: what grounds the grounding order? Emptiness, not a final crane. (≤120)

**Rationalist (agency).** The cleverest move — no gap to leap, because the norm is the form of the agent. But Nāgārjuna's examination of agent and action (MMK 8) dismantles exactly this: agent and act are mutually dependent, neither established in its own right; "the doer depends upon the deed, the deed upon the doer" (8.12). Your "constitutive standard of agency" is a self that legislates to itself — a svabhāva-bearing agent grasped as inescapable, self-grounding, closed under its own assessment. That inescapability *is* the reification. Agency too is empty; it works conventionally without being the ground it must be for your transcendental argument to bind.

**Realist (non-natural fact).** The purest grasping: a normative fact "mind-independent, non-natural, necessary, causally inert" — this is *svabhāva* named in plain text (MMK 15.1–2: own-being is "not produced, not dependent on another"). Your safety/modal reply leans on *necessity* — but a thing whose distribution is fixed across all worlds, dependent on nothing, is precisely the frozen unconditioned Nāgārjuna shows can do no work and enter no relation (24.20). You posit the most untouchable ground and call its untouchability a virtue. The queerness you wave off is the tradition reporting, correctly, that you have reified.

**Phenomenologist (closest).** You alone reject the gap, and the two-truths reader should embrace most of this. But "constituted in being-in-the-world" still erects a quasi-ground: the lifeworld, the engaged relation, the face's command become a *standing field* that does the founding work — a soft svabhāva, dependable underfoot rather than inert above. Nāgārjuna would press the emptiness of the relation itself (MMK 24.18: the dependent, the empty, the designated are one — none foundational). "Being-in-the-world" risks becoming the last thing not seen as empty. Drop it as ground, keep it as convention, and we converge.
