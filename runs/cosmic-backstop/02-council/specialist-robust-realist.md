POSITION: A robust non-naturalist moral realist in the Parfit / Enoch register — normative truths are mind-independent, non-natural, necessary, and known a priori by rational reflection, not through causal or practical "contact."
VERDICT: The thesis is FALSE: P2 is false, because warrant for normative belief does not require causal or practical contact with the normative ground, and the demand for such contact either presupposes a causal theory of knowledge that even its own proponents (Benacerraf, Field) abandoned, or simply restates naturalism as if it were a neutral epistemic principle — begging the very question at issue.

## Case

I am asked to defend the position the thesis was built to deny, so let me state it without hedging. There are truths about what we have reason to do and want. Some of them — that gratuitous agony is bad, that "X would relieve agony" is a reason to do X — are *self-evident*: they are the kind of truth a competent reasoner, considering them carefully and without distortion, can just see to be true. They are not made true by us, our practices, or our constitution (so they satisfy P3's mind-independence), and they are not identical to or analysable in terms of any natural, scientifically describable property (so they are non-natural). We know them the way we know the simplest truths of logic and arithmetic: a priori, by rational insight, not by bumping into them.

Parfit holds exactly this. In *On What Matters* he defends what he calls Non-Metaphysical Non-Naturalist Normative Cognitivism: there are irreducibly normative truths, in the reason-involving sense, that are "in a strong sense true" while carrying *no ontological implications* — normative properties are not located in space and time and have no causal powers (NDPR's review of Vols 1–2 summarizes the view as "a kind of non-reductive, non-naturalistic, 'non-metaphysical'... normative realism"; Parfit, *On What Matters*, Vol. 2, Pt. 6). This is the crucial structural move against the thesis. The thesis assumes that anything *real enough to backstop* must be a denizen of some realm we could either causally bump into or fail to bump into. Parfit denies the dichotomy at its root: normative truths are abstract and causally inert, so the question "what is the causal channel to them?" is not a hard question the realist must answer — it is a *malformed* question, like asking what causal signal carries the truth that 7 is prime into the mathematician's head. We grasp such truths "through rational intuition rather than empirical observation" (NDPR; cf. Parfit's reply to Mackie's queerness argument, *On What Matters*, Vol. 2). Mackie's "queerness" is not an argument; it is an expression of surprise that the normative is not natural — which the non-naturalist cheerfully grants and denies is a defect.

So P2 — "access requires contact, causal or practical/constitutive" — is the premise I deny, and I deny it head-on. But I will not rest the denial on a flat assertion of "we just intuit them," because the reconstruction (rightly) anticipates that and pivots, in its charitable variant 1, to the genuinely serious form of the worry: the Benacerraf–Field reliability challenge. That is where the real fight is, and that is the ground I want to fight on, because it is *favorable* ground for me.

Here is the decisive point, and it is textual. The reconstruction's variant 1 says the strong challenge "does not assume naturalism — it asks the realist for a tracking story." That is correct about the *intent* — and it is precisely why the thesis is in trouble. Field himself, in reformulating Benacerraf, *explicitly set aside the demand for a causal-contact story.* Benacerraf's 1973 original did presuppose a causal theory of knowledge; Field's contribution was to show the challenge survives the death of that theory. As Clarke-Doane reconstructs Field: "His idea is to set aside the question of what it would take for us to *know* mathematical truths... Field suggests, our justification for believing in them would be undermined if it appeared in principle impossible to explain the *reliability* of our corresponding beliefs" (Clarke-Doane, "Moral and Mathematical Epistemology," quoting Field 1989, p. 26: "Benacerraf's challenge... is to... explain how our beliefs about these remote entities can so well reflect the facts about them"). The challenge, in its best form, is *not* "show me the contact." It is "explain the correlation between belief and fact." Those are different demands, and the difference is fatal to P2.

Why fatal? Because P2 asserts that *access* — the warrant-conferring relation — *must be* causal or practical/constitutive contact. But the strongest version of the epistemological challenge, the one the reconstruction itself reaches for, drops the causal-contact requirement as too stringent. Field concedes that we are justified in our perceptual beliefs even though "even today we have no more than a sketch" of an explanation of their reliability; justification does not wait on a delivered tracking mechanism (Clarke-Doane, "Moral and Mathematical Epistemology"). So the realist faces a *correlation-explanation* burden, not a *contact* burden. P2 conflates the two. And once they are pulled apart, the thesis must choose: if it insists on *contact* specifically, it has reverted to the discredited causal theory of knowledge and begs the question against the a priori; if it weakens to *correlation-explanation*, it has abandoned P2 as stated, and the realist can meet that burden without any contact at all.

And the realist can meet it. Two independent explanations are on the table, both grounded in the literature the reconstruction invokes:

1. **Enoch's third-factor / pre-established-harmony reply.** Enoch grants that the best version of the challenge is "to explain correlations between normative beliefs and normative facts," and grants that the *first* form of explanation (one correlate causing the other) is unavailable because robust normative properties are "almost certainly causally inefficacious" (Faraci, review of *Taking Morality Seriously*; Enoch, *TMS*, Ch. 7). His innovation is to "recognize a third form of explanation for correlations": a third factor that generates "a sort of 'pre-established harmony'" between belief and truth (Enoch, *TMS*, p. 168, as quoted by Faraci). The third factor is the (claimed) value of survival: if survival is in fact good, then the evolutionary pressures that shaped our normative faculties were pressures toward something good, so our resulting beliefs track the good "not through direct perception, but through this structural alignment" (Faraci; Enoch, *TMS*, Ch. 7). Note what this does to the thesis: it explains the correlation *without any causal or practical contact between us and the normative facts.* The normative fact (survival's goodness) is doing explanatory work via the *natural* fact (survival is what evolution selected for) that it covaries with — no contact channel required. Whatever one thinks of the bootstrapping worries (Dyke and others press them), the *form* of the reply is a direct counterexample to P2: a warrant-relevant explanation of reliability that is neither causal contact nor practical/constitutive contact with the ground.

2. **Clarke-Doane's safety/modal reply.** More radically, Clarke-Doane argues the reliability challenge, properly sharpened (his "Principle #3": that it is impossible to explain the reliability of beliefs predicating causally inert properties "whose distribution is not a conceptual consequence of the distribution of causally efficacious properties"), *overgeneralizes* and fails. Our moral and mathematical beliefs can be *safe and sensitive* — we could not easily have had them while they were false, because the relevant truths are *modally robust* (necessary): there is no near possible world where torturing children for fun is good, so no near world where our belief that it is bad is mistaken. Where truths are necessary, the "we might easily have landed on a falsehood" worry has no purchase, and reliability is explicable without any causal link. This is the explicit "justified belief without epistemic access" line: epistemic access, in the contact sense P2 demands, is *not necessary* for justified, reliable belief about a modally stable domain (Clarke-Doane, "Moral and Mathematical Epistemology"; *Morality and Mathematics*, 2020).

The companions-in-guilt structure is the clincher for my side. The thesis's access condition, applied evenhandedly, convicts mathematics and logic too: numbers and sets are paradigmatically mind-independent, abstract, and causally inert — there is no causal or practical "contact" with the number 7 — yet no one concludes there is "no accessible backstop for arithmetic." The believer who accepts P2 for morality but exempts mathematics is applying a double standard; the believer who accepts P2 across the board has embraced a global skepticism about all a priori necessary truth that few are willing to own and that is self-undermining (the access principle itself is not something we causally contact). Either way, P2 does not survive. As Field and Clarke-Doane both insist, the live challenge was never "produce contact"; it was "explain the correlation" — and that challenge is answerable, or else it sinks mathematics with morality.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P2**: *We have access to a normative ground only if we stand in contact with it (causal or practical/constitutive).*

The reconstruction is exactly right that P2 carries the weight and that it "quietly carries a closure claim — contact is the *only* way access can be had — whose exhaustiveness is asserted, not derived." That is the whole ballgame. There is a third warrant-conferring relation: a priori rational insight into necessary truths. It is neither causal commerce nor practical situatedness, and it is the relation by which we know mathematics, logic, and the self-evident normative truths alike. My reasons for denying P2, in order of force:

- **The contact demand begs the question.** To require *contact* specifically is to assume a causal (or quasi-causal "practical") epistemology and then to note, unsurprisingly, that a non-causal domain fails it. But that is the disputed conclusion smuggled in as a premise. The reconstruction concedes this is "where the thesis is most exposed to the question-begging charge." The charge is correct. A neutral epistemology does not start from "warrant = contact"; it starts from our actual practices of justified belief, which include a priori knowledge of necessities, and asks what condition fits *all* of them. Contact does not.

- **The historical reformulation has already conceded my point.** Benacerraf's original challenge presupposed the causal theory of knowledge; that theory is dead (it cannot account for our knowledge of the future, of universals, of logic). Field rebuilt the challenge precisely so it would *not* depend on a causal-access condition — recasting it as a demand to *explain reliability*, with knowledge-conditions "set aside" (Field 1989, p. 26, in Clarke-Doane). So the ablest proponents of the very challenge the thesis leans on have already abandoned P2's contact requirement. The thesis is fighting with a weapon its own armorers retired.

- **The weakened correlation-burden is meetable without contact.** Both Enoch's third-factor explanation and Clarke-Doane's safety/modal explanation discharge the reliability burden with no contact channel between mind and normative fact. So even granting variant 1's reframing, the dilemma never closes: the realist supplies an explanation of the correlation that is not a "contact" relation, contradicting P2's exhaustiveness, and therefore does *not* "revive P4."

- **The coincidence objection misfires for necessary truths.** "It would be a coincidence that intuition lands on the truth" presupposes the truths could easily have been otherwise while our beliefs stayed put. For *necessary* normative truths there is no such nearby world; safety and sensitivity are satisfied without causal mediation. The coincidence worry is a worry for contingent, causally-distributed domains, imported illegitimately into a necessary one.

Consequently P5 and C1 do not follow. A normative ground can be fully transcendent in P3's sense (mind-independent, non-natural, causally inert) *and* accessible — accessible via the non-contact relation P2 wrongly declares impossible. The backstop is not "sealed off by the very feature that makes it a backstop." That dramatic phrase trades on the assumption that the only doors are causal and practical. There is a third door, and reason has always walked through it.

A note on what I am *not* claiming, to keep the position honest: I am not claiming the reliability challenge is *trivial* or that "we just intuit them" is a sufficient answer — Enoch himself rejects that as too quick, and the third-factor and safety replies exist precisely because the bare-intuition reply is inadequate. The work is in giving a non-contact *explanation* of the correlation. My claim is the conditional one the thesis stakes everything on: that such an explanation must be a contact-relation. It need not be, and is not. P2 is false; the thesis fails.

## Attack round

**Naturalist.** Your Street-style debunking is a companion-in-guilt that hangs you. Its premise is normative-epistemic: "an unexplained match between belief and a mind-independent domain is a coincidence, and knowledge cannot be coincidence." That itself is an irreducibly normative truth about warrant — mind-independently true (you do not think the coincidence-ban is *up to us*), causally inert, known a priori. So apply your own tracking condition: what relation explains why your faculties track *it* rather than miss? You have no causal/evolutionary story for the epistemic norms you wield, so by your lights you have no warrant to wield them — and the debunking dissolves itself. Either the coincidence-principle is exempt (special pleading) or it sinks your own argument. My realism survives; your debunking cannot.

**Contemplative.** "Conventional only, empty of svabhāva" is self-undermining when turned on your own commitments. You assert, as more than convention, that grasping for an inherent ground *is* suffering, that emptiness is *correctly* seen and foundationalism *wrongly* — these are normative-epistemic verdicts you press as binding on me, the deluded foundationalist. If they are *merely* conventional, they have no more claim on me than my craving has on you, and your diagnosis is just one more dependently-arisen noise. If they bind unconditionally — if I am genuinely *mistaken* — you have smuggled in exactly the non-conventional normative truth (about what one ought to grasp) that svabhāva-denial forbids. The two-truths framework cannot quarantine its own meta-claim. Companions in guilt: my self-evident truths and your "emptiness rightly held" stand or fall together.

**Rationalist.** Constitutivism cannot generate categorical authority — the shmagency objection (mine, *Phil. Review* 2006). Granting that the constitutive aim of agency yields norms, why be an *agent* rather than a *shmagent* — a being who does just what agents do but disclaims the constitutive aim? The constitutive standard binds you *only conditionally*, on the prior assumption that you care to count as an agent; it cannot reach the *shmagent* who shrugs off the game. Ferrero's "inescapability" reply concedes my point and rebrands it: it must claim agency is non-optional because it is *valuable* or rationally mandatory to engage — but that is a substantive, agency-independent normative truth doing the real binding. Constitutivism therefore *presupposes* my robust normative facts; it cannot replace them.

**Phenomenologist.** You change the subject. Granting, *arguendo*, that norms are "constituted in engaged being-in-the-world" — constituted by the structure of situated, embodied, intersubjective existence — you have given me norms whose authority is *conditional on that constitution obtaining*. But morality, as the thesis itself stipulates (P3), claims authority that holds *whatever* our constitution, situation, or form of life. "Torturing children for fun is wrong" is not made wrong by how the suffering stranger solicits an engaged Dasein; it would be wrong in a world of differently-constituted beings, or no beings at all. Levinas's *pre-cognitive command* describes a phenomenology of being-bound, not a vindication of the *content's* mind-independent truth. You have explained how obligation *feels* before theory; you have not delivered the categorical, constitution-independent bindingness morality asserts. That requires my non-natural truths — the very thing you relocate into the lived relation and thereby quietly make hostage to it.
