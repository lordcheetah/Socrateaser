# 01 — Reconstruction: the cosmic backstop

> Run slug: `cosmic-backstop`
> Reconstructed from `00-thesis.md`.

## Standard form

P1. A normative ground confers warrant on our moral judgments only if we have *access* to it (i.e., only an accessible ground can do justificatory work; a merely posited ground constrains nothing).

P2. We have access to a normative ground only if we stand in *contact* with it — contact being either causal (the ground figures in the causal order that produces or shapes our judgments) or practical/constitutive (the ground shows up in what we do and how we are situated).

P3. A *cosmic backstop* is a normative ground that is mind-independent and transcendent — its authority does not depend on us, our practices, or our constitution.

P4. Whatever is in contact with us — causally or practically/constitutively — is thereby part of the natural/practical order, i.e., dependent on or entangled with us, our practices, or our situation.

P5. Therefore, nothing that is in contact with us is a cosmic backstop. (from P3, P4)

C1. Therefore, no cosmic backstop is accessible to us. (from P1, P2, P5)

C.  Therefore, there is no *accessible* cosmic backstop for morality. (restates C1 — the thesis)

## Validity

The argument is valid. Trace the chain:

- P3 and P4 generate P5 by a straightforward dilemma. P4 says contact entails membership in the natural/practical order; P3 says a backstop is, by definition, *not* in that order (transcendent, independence from us). So anything in contact with us fails the definition of a backstop — nothing contacted is a backstop. This is a categorical exclusion (the "by the very feature" move from the thesis): the property that qualifies something as contactable is the same property that disqualifies it as a backstop.

- P1, P2, and P5 yield C1 by chained conditionals plus elimination. From P1 and P2: access requires warrant-relation, which requires contact (hypothetical syllogism — warrant ⊃ access, access ⊃ contact, so warrant via this ground ⊃ contact). P5 says no backstop is contacted. So no backstop satisfies the access condition; no backstop is accessible. Formally this is a *modus tollens* on the backstop: if a backstop were accessible it would be contacted (P1–P2), but no backstop is contacted (P5), therefore no backstop is accessible.

- C1 is the thesis (C). No gap between them.

So the inference is deductively valid: **if** P1–P4 hold, C follows necessarily. The form is a definitional dilemma (P3+P4 → P5) feeding a hypothetical-syllogism/modus-tollens elimination (P1+P2+P5 → C1).

One structural note for the hidden-premise-excavator, not a validity defect: P2 builds the disjunction "causal OR practical/constitutive" into the *necessary* condition on access. The argument's validity does not require that this disjunction be *exhaustive* of all access-relations — it only requires that these are the only access-relations that confer warrant. But the thesis's force depends on there being no *third* warrant-conferring relation (notably a priori rational insight) that is neither causal nor practical. If such a third relation exists and confers warrant, P2 is false. P2 thus quietly carries a closure claim — "contact (in these two modes) is the *only* way access can be had" — whose exhaustiveness is asserted, not derived. This is a suppressed-premise site, flagged for excavation, not a hole in the deduction as written (P2 already states the biconditional-strength "only if").

## Where the weight sits

**P2 carries the weight.** It is the access condition: *access requires contact (causal or practical/constitutive)*. Everything else is either definitional (P3), nearly analytic (P1 — a ground you cannot reach cannot justify), or a metaphysical reading of "contact" (P4) that the realist can grant without conceding the conclusion.

The reason P2 is the crux:

- Deny P2 and the argument collapses outright. The robust moral realist (Parfit/Enoch) does exactly this: warrant for necessary, abstract normative truths comes via *a priori rational insight*, which is neither causal commerce nor practical situation. If that is a genuine warrant-conferring relation, then "access" can be had without "contact," P2 is false, and a transcendent ground can be both genuinely transcendent (satisfying P3) and accessible — the dilemma never closes.

- P2 is also where the thesis is most exposed to the question-begging charge from the framing: it looks like it imports a broadly naturalist epistemology ("show me the causal/practical story") and then convicts the non-natural ground of failing a test it was never going to pass. The realist's complaint is precisely that P2 is the disputed conclusion smuggled in as a premise.

- P4 is the *secondary* pressure point (the phenomenologist/contemplative target). They attack not P2's content but its presupposition — that "access" names a relation across a gap between knower and ground at all. If that subject/object split is itself a confusion, then P4's clean partition of "contacted (hence natural)" vs. "transcendent (hence uncontacted)" is a false dichotomy and P5 fails. But this is a deeper, less direct route; the first and sharpest blow lands on P2.

So the eventual crux is: **does warrant require contact, or can a priori (or otherwise non-contact) insight confer it?** That is exactly where the thesis was framed to put the fight.

## Charitable variants

The reconstruction above is already the strong version (it states the access condition as a necessary condition and grounds the dilemma in a definitional feature rather than an empirical generalization). Three refinements make it harder to dismiss:

1. **Weaken P1/P2 from "warrant" to "discriminating warrant" (the reliability/safety version).** Instead of "access requires contact," claim: *a ground confers warrant only if our being in the relation explains why our judgments track it rather than miss it* — i.e., the relation must be one that could have made our judgments different had the ground been different. Causal and practical contact obviously can do this; the charitable challenge to the realist is that bare a priori insight, absent any such tracking-explanation, leaves it a coincidence that our intuitions land on the normative truths. This is the Benacerraf/Field-style "access problem" run for ethics, and it is stronger because it does not assume naturalism — it asks the realist for a *tracking story* and notes that "we just intuit them" supplies none. The realist must then either supply a tracking relation (which looks like contact, reviving P4) or accept the coincidence (which undercuts warrant).

2. **Reframe P4 as a definitional truth about "backstop," not a metaphysical claim about contact.** The strongest reading does not say "everything contactable happens to be natural." It says: the work the word *backstop* is doing — guaranteeing from *outside* our practice that the practice tracks something real — *requires* independence from the practice; but any relation rich enough to confer warrant on the practice is *ipso facto* a relation the practice stands in, hence not fully outside it. On this reading P4 is conceptual: a ground that genuinely backstops is by definition one we are not in a warrant-relation to, because being in such a relation is what it is to be inside, not behind, the practice. This blocks the "you just defined naturalism into P4" complaint by relocating the burden to the *function* a backstop is supposed to serve.

3. **Restrict the conclusion to "no backstop does backstop-*work* for us."** Rather than the bald metaphysical-epistemic "no accessible backstop," conclude: *even if a transcendent ground exists, it cannot play the role for which it is wanted* — settling first-order moral disputes, adjudicating between practices, providing the external check. An inaccessible ground, whatever its metaphysical status, is inert in exactly the dispute it was summoned to resolve. This is the pragmatist's strongest form: it concedes the realist's metaphysics for free and still wins the practical point, sidestepping the a priori-access fight by granting it and showing it makes no operative difference. (Note this trades epistemic reach for dialectical safety, and the realist who thinks a priori-accessed truths *do* settle first-order disputes will deny the inertness — so it relocates rather than removes the P2 crux.)

The author's best line is **variant 1**: it keeps the deductive spine, refuses the question-begging charge by demanding a tracking story rather than assuming causation, and forces the realist onto the genuinely hard ground that has historically been hardest for non-naturalism to answer.
