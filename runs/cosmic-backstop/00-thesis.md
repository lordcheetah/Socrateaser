# 00 — Thesis: the cosmic backstop

> Run slug: `cosmic-backstop`
> Framed from `theses/cosmic-backstop.md`.

## Thesis

There is no accessible cosmic backstop for morality, because the only ground we could ever stand on is one we are in causal or practical contact with, and a mind-independent normative ground — by the very feature that would make it a *backstop* rather than just another natural fact — is sealed off from exactly that kind of contact.

## Classification

- **Type:** mixed (primarily epistemic/metaphysical, with a normative payload)
- **Why this type:** The thesis turns on a metaphysical question (does a mind-independent normative ground exist and what is it like?) only as far as that question bears on an epistemic one (could we *access* such a ground?). The load-bearing move is epistemic: it asserts an access condition — that warrant requires some form of contact, causal or practical — and then argues that the postulated backstop fails it. The normative subject matter (morality) is what gives the dispute its stakes, but the fight is not settled by moral argument; it is settled by adjudicating the access condition. This is why it is mixed and not purely normative: a moral realist and a moral anti-realist could *both* accept the thesis if they agreed the realist's ground is inaccessible, and could both reject it if they agreed it is not. The crux is access, not the first-order moral facts.

## Key terms to pin

- **Cosmic backstop:** a mind-independent ground that *guarantees* our morality tracks something real — something whose authority does not depend on us, our practices, or our constitution. Used here in the *robust* sense (a Parfit/Enoch non-natural normative fact, or a theistic ground). The rival, deflationary sense — "backstop" as merely whatever stabilizes our practice from within it (reasons, coherence, a form of life) — would change the dispute entirely: on that sense the thesis is trivially false, because such grounds are accessible by definition. The thesis only bites against the robust sense.
- **Access:** standing in a relation to a ground that confers warrant — that lets the ground actually constrain belief or judgment, rather than merely being posited. The thesis claims this relation must be *contact*: causal (the naturalist's "what's the causal story?") or practical/constitutive (the ground shows up in what we do and how we are situated). The rival sense — *a priori* access, where reason's own structure grasps necessary truths without contact (the rationalist's claim) — is precisely what the thesis denies is sufficient, and is therefore the first place the dispute will be contested.
- **Morality (not knowledge):** This run deliberately restricts to the normative case. (See "Other theses in the topic" below.)
- **Sealed off "by the very feature":** the thesis presses a dilemma — anything we *can* contact is thereby part of the natural/practical order and so not a transcendent guarantor; anything transcendent enough to guarantee is thereby out of contact. This conditional is the spine; break it and the thesis falls.

## Is it contestable?

Yes — sharply, from at least three directions:

- **Robust moral realists (Parfit, Enoch, Scanlon-adjacent):** deny that warrant requires *contact* at all. Normative truths are necessary, abstract, and known the way mathematical truths are known — not by causal commerce but by rational insight. The "access requires contact" premise simply imports a naturalist epistemology and then complains the non-natural fails it; that is question-begging, not argument.
- **Rationalists (broadly Kantian / a priori traditions):** the bindingness of norms is given in the structure of reason itself, accessed *a priori*. Reason is not "out of contact" with its own necessary structure — that framing is a category error.
- **Theists / classical natural-law theorists:** there is an accessible ground (a divine or teleological order) and access runs through participation, conscience, or revelation, not Humean causation.

This is a healthy seed: the denial is held by serious, living positions, not strawmen.

## Pseudo-dispute check

There is a real dispute, but it sits on top of a genuine pseudo-dispute that the run must clear first, or the council will fight over nothing.

**The pseudo-dispute:** "Is there a backstop, or is morality mere subjective projection?" That forced choice equivocates on "backstop." On the *robust* sense, the question is substantive. On the *deflationary* sense (reasons, practices, a form of life that stabilizes judgment), there obviously is something that does backstop-like work, and calling it "mere projection" is tendentious. Two people using "backstop" in the two senses will talk past each other indefinitely. **The distinction dissolves that surface fight.**

**Why a real dispute survives the distinction:** once "backstop" is pinned to the robust sense, a genuine disagreement remains — not about whether such a ground would be nice to have, but about the *access condition*. The realist says warrant can be non-causal/a priori; the thesis says warrant requires contact and the robust ground cannot supply it. That is a real crux: the two sides cannot both be right, and nothing dissolves it.

So the thesis is framed to put the **access condition** in the council's crosshairs, not the existence question. That is deliberate — per the seed, the single premise the whole dispute turns on is *whether access requires causal (or practical) contact*, and this thesis is built so that premise is the thing that breaks.

**A note on the dichotomy the seed flags:** the phenomenologist and contemplative are seated precisely to attack the "contact-or-nothing" framing as itself a smuggled subject/object split. The thesis does *not* assert the dichotomy is exhaustive; it asserts a conditional (contact is necessary for access, and the robust ground lacks contact). Those two voices will press that even the word "access" presupposes a gap between knower and ground that, on their view, does not exist. That pressure is welcome — it is the live alternative to both realist and naturalist, not a reframing that makes the dispute disappear.

## Other theses in the topic (future runs, not this pass)

The seed correctly notes the topic contains several theses. This run targets exactly one. Flagged for later:

1. **The knowledge variant:** the same access argument run against a backstop for *knowledge* rather than morality. Splitting it out is the right call — the conjunction ("knowledge and morality stand or fall together") is itself a contestable thesis, and the access stories differ (the realist's a priori reply is far more natural for normative necessities than for empirical knowledge). Running them separately keeps each crux clean.
2. **The existence variant:** "there is no robust backstop, full stop" — a metaphysical thesis where access is downstream. Weaker as a seed for *this* author, because the pragmatist can be moved on access in a way they cannot on bare existence.

## Suggested specialists to summon

1. **A robust non-naturalist moral realist (Parfit/Enoch-style).** Non-negotiable for this run. The thesis is built to deny exactly their position; without a live voice asserting non-causal a priori access to normative facts, the naturalist has no opponent and the access premise never gets tested. The seed's own recommendation, and the right one.
2. **A Kantian/constitutivist rationalist (Korsgaard-adjacent).** Distinct from the realist: argues the backstop is the constitutive structure of agency/reason, accessed by being a rational agent — a *practical*-contact reply that threatens the thesis from a different angle than abstract a priori insight. Worth summoning to keep the rationalist council-voice from collapsing into the realist's reply.
