# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Thesis & framing
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Frame
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

---

## Thesis
Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower cost: it explains why introspection reports qualitative character *without* convicting introspection of systematic error, whereas illusionism can only discharge the same explanandum by positing a global introspective misrepresentation it cannot itself non-circularly motivate.

## Classification
- **Type:** mixed — primarily a meta-theoretic / explanatory-virtue claim (which model better accounts for the agreed data, and at what cost), resting on a contested conceptual claim (whether introspection is reliable about phenomenal character) and shading into a metaphysical one (whether there is any phenomenal character to be reliable *about*).
- **Why this type:** The seed is comparative — "better model than" — and that word does the load-bearing work, so the thesis is not first-order ("do qualia exist?") but second-order ("which theory better discharges its explanatory obligations?"). That makes the dispute turn on theory-choice criteria: parsimony, conservatism (saving the phenomena), and the cost of error-theories. None of those are settled by new empirical facts — transparency theorists and illusionists agree on every third-person fact about brains and reports — so this is not an empirical dispute, even though both camps fly the flag of naturalism. It is settled by *analysis*: of what the agreed explanandum actually is, and of which model incurs which theoretical debt to explain it. But it cannot be purely conceptual, because the central premise — that introspection is or is not systematically reliable about the character of experience — is itself the crux, and that premise has a metaphysical shadow: if there is literally no phenomenal property in the world, "reliable about it" has no truth-maker. Naming the type matters because it tells the council where the fight is *not*: not in neuroscience, not in who can recite Tye more accurately, but in the comparative bookkeeping of explanatory cost. The distinction-watchdog must keep the council from "refuting" the thesis by re-litigating whether qualia exist (a first-order move) when the thesis only claims one model prices the data better than the other.

## Key terms to pin
- **"better model":** Pinned here to a single axis — *minimizing the joint cost of (a) saving the appearances and (b) the theoretical debt incurred to do so*, where "saving the appearances" means accounting for the manifest datum that experience presents qualitative, felt character to introspection. The thesis claims transparency wins on this axis because it treats introspection as broadly veridical (cheap on conservatism) while still reducing character to content (cheap on parsimony), whereas illusionism buys parsimony only by going into debt on conservatism (a global error theory). Rival senses of "better" that would change the dispute entirely: (i) *better at dissolving the hard problem* — on which illusionism plausibly wins, since it denies the explanandum that generates the problem; (ii) *better at ultimate ontological economy* — on which illusionism, positing no phenomenal properties at all, may be the leaner theory. The thesis must not be allowed to slide between these axes mid-argument; it stakes axis (a)+(b), the "saving the phenomena at lowest cost" reading, and the council should attack or defend *that*.
- **qualia / phenomenal character:** This is the pseudo-dispute landmine. The thesis uses "qualia" in the *thin, neutral* sense — the felt, qualitative aspect of experience *as it presents itself to introspection*, the thing-to-be-explained — without prejudging whether that aspect is an intrinsic non-representational property. The rival sense, the one illusionism targets, is *thick qualia*: intrinsic, ineffable, non-physical, non-representational properties of experience (the "classical qualia" Frankish denies). If "qualia" means thick qualia, transparency *also* denies them (Tye, Dretske, Harman are anti-qualia in exactly this sense) — and then the two views are near-allies, not rivals, which is a crucial finding for the pseudo-dispute check.
- **transparency / diaphanousness:** Used in the *strong* sense (Harman/Tye): introspection of an experience of X reveals only the represented properties of X, no intrinsic mental qualities — and, critically, introspection so construed is *veridical*. The rival weak sense ("we are merely hard put to find intrinsic qualities") would defang the thesis, because it concedes that introspection might be misleading, which is the illusionist's foot in the door.
- **illusion / misrepresentation:** Illusionism's core claim that introspection *systematically misrepresents* functional/representational states as having phenomenal character. Pin which illusionism: *strong* illusionism (there is nothing feely, only quasi-phenomenal properties wrongly introspected as phenomenal) is the thesis's real opponent; *weak* illusionism (some phenomenal-seeming properties are real but our concepts of them are distorted) collapses toward representationalism and would soften the fight.
- **"saves the phenomena":** Means: accounts for the data without declaring the data illusory. The thesis's whole wager is that an error theory pays a conservatism penalty a non-error theory avoids. An opponent who denies that "introspection seems veridical" is even part of the explanandum attacks this term directly.

## Is it contestable?
Strongly, and from named opponents on multiple sides. The thesis is denied head-on by **Keith Frankish's illusionism**: Frankish argues precisely that introspection *does* systematically misrepresent — that phenomenal seeming is itself the illusion to be explained — and that the "conservatism" the thesis prizes is a vice, because what looks like saving the phenomena is really preserving the very error that generates the hard problem. **Daniel Dennett** denies the thesis from the same direction and would reject its key term outright: there is no theory-neutral "appearance" to be saved, because "how things seem" is not a datum prior to theory (the "first-person operationalism" / heterophenomenology line). The thesis is *also* pressured from the realist side it might seem to flatter: a **Chalmers/Goff-style phenomenal realist** denies that transparency saves the phenomena *at all*, holding that representationalism's reduction of character to content leaves out the very feel it claims to have explained — so on their view transparency does no better than illusionism at the thing that matters, and possibly worse, because at least illusionism is honest that it has dropped the explanandum. Finally, **higher-order theorists (Rosenthal)** and **Ned Block** (who distinguishes phenomenal from access consciousness and holds character outruns representational content) supply premise-level denials of the reduction the thesis relies on. There is no consensus winner; this is a live frontier dispute. It is a good seed.

## Pseudo-dispute check
Partly verbal at the surface, genuinely substantive underneath — and the verbal layer is itself the most important thing for the council to see, so I draw it rather than dissolve into it.

The surface verbal trap: on the word "qualia." Transparency representationalism and illusionism *agree* in denying intrinsic, non-representational, non-physical qualia. Tye is an anti-qualia physicalist; Frankish is an anti-qualia physicalist. If the dispute were "do thick qualia exist?", they would be on the *same side*, and the seed would be a near-pseudo-dispute between two reductive naturalisms quibbling over branding. The distinction that exposes this: **representational content** (what experience presents) versus **the introspective report of qualitative character** (how experience presents itself). Both deny intrinsic qualia; they split on the *status of introspection*.

That split is where the real, non-verbal residue lives, and it does not dissolve: **Is introspective access to phenomenal character broadly veridical (transparency) or systematically erroneous (illusionism)?** This is a genuine disagreement with opposite truth-conditions — one theory says our reports of felt redness track a real represented property correctly, the other says they misfire by attributing a qualitative character nothing has. No distinction collapses that; they cannot both be right about whether introspection is reliable.

But there is a second, deeper risk the council must police, and it is where the dispute could *re-collapse*: whether transparency's "veridical introspection of represented properties" and illusionism's "misrepresentation of functional states" are even talking about the same target. The transparency theorist says introspection veridically delivers *represented worldly properties* (redness-out-there); the illusionist says introspection erroneously delivers *phenomenal properties of experience* (felt-redness-in-here). If these are different objects, both could be true and the "rivalry" is two answers to two questions. The non-verbal core that survives even this: **does the manifest datum of consciousness include an apparent qualitative character of experience itself — and if so, is taking that appearance at face value (transparency) cheaper than treating it as a systematic error (illusionism)?** That is a real contest about explanatory cost, and it is the target the council should be handed.

## Suggested specialists to summon
- A **Frankish-style strong illusionist** — the thesis's named direct opponent. Defends that introspection systematically misrepresents, that conservatism about the appearances is exactly the bug not the feature, and that replacing the hard problem with the illusion problem is a net theoretical *gain*, not a cost. Essential; the fixed council cannot supply this position without it.
- A **Chalmers/Goff-style phenomenal realist** — denies the thesis from the opposite flank: transparency does not save the phenomena, it relocates them, leaving the felt character unexplained; so the comparative claim is false because *neither* model saves the phenomena and illusionism is at least candid about the loss. This gives the council the anti-reductionist corner that pressures the thesis's central term ("saves") rather than its conclusion.
- (Optional) A **Dennett-style heterophenomenologist** — denies there is any theory-neutral "appearance" to be saved, attacking the thesis's explanandum itself. Summon only if the council needs the position that the whole "save the phenomena" framing begs the question; otherwise the strong illusionist covers adjacent ground.
