# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Lay-readable version
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Communication (lay-readable · refinement 1)
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of conscious experience than illusionism, because it saves how things seem at a lower explanatory cost — without accusing introspection of being systematically wrong.

---

## The question, plainly

When you look at a ripe tomato, your brain is doing something. Light hits your eye, signals run through the visual system, and out comes a state we'd describe as "seeing red." Easy enough. The hard part is the *feeling* of it: there's something it's like to see red, a felt redness, and no amount of wiring-diagram talk seems to capture that felt part.

The author started out wanting to defend a middle path. One camp says our sense that experience is felt is broadly trustworthy. Another camp, the "illusionists," says that sense is a kind of trick the brain plays on itself. The author wanted a third option that keeps the feeling honest without buying the trick. The question is whether that third option actually exists, or whether it quietly collapses into one of the two camps it was trying to stand between.

## Why it's not obvious

Take the first pull. When you stub your toe, the pain isn't a theory you're entertaining; it's right there, undeniable, the most obvious thing in the room. To be told "your sense that this is felt is a misfire" sounds less like a discovery and more like someone refusing to look at the one thing they can't deny. That's the realist's grip on you: the feeling is the *datum*, the raw given, and a theory that explains it away has explained away the thing it was supposed to account for.

Now the other pull. We already know introspection — your inner sense of your own mind — is a lousy reporter. It tells you nothing about neurons, nothing about how the machinery actually works; people were certain about all sorts of inner "facts" that turned out to be confabulated. So why trust it when it insists there's some special extra felt-stuff floating above the brain's activity? Maybe that "extra felt-stuff" is exactly the kind of thing introspection would invent. The illusionist isn't denying that you see red. They're denying that there's a mysterious *felt property* over and above your brain representing red — and they think the sense that there is one is the brain misreading its own work.

Both sides agree on every physical fact. Same brains, same reports, same behavior. They're fighting over something no brain scan can settle.

## Where it really turns

Here's the surprise refinement 1 turned up, and it's worth sitting with: the author isn't really between the two camps at all. When the author's developed view got written out and pressed, it turned out to be a real, named position — computational or functionalist representationalism, the view associated with Michael Graziano, Thomas Metzinger, and Daniel Dennett. In plain terms: the feeling *is* the brain's representation, a model the brain builds because evolution found it useful. There's nothing left over once you've described the representing. "Qualia" — the felt qualities — just *are* that model; you can change it, but there's no extra residue underneath.

And that view already agrees with the illusionists on the one thing that matters. The author's own phrase for it was a "distinction without a difference": there's no real gap between *feeling something is real* and *the brain simulating that it's real*. That denial — that there's no extra felt fact hiding behind the representation — is the load-bearing claim of illusionism. It's literally their core move. The kicker: Keith Frankish, who coined the illusionist banner, lists Graziano by name as one of them. (The fact-checker pulled this straight from Frankish's own page; it holds up.) So the author's fight with "illusionism" turns out to be a fight about the *word* "illusion," not about the picture of the mind underneath it. Graziano himself has that exact word-fight with Frankish while agreeing on everything else.

So the author's real opponent was never the illusionist. It's the *phenomenal realist* — the person who says the feeling is genuinely there as a fact about reality, not just as a representation. And the whole disagreement shrinks to one plain yes-or-no question:

**Is there an extra "felt" fact, over and above the brain representing things — call it Fact B — or isn't there?**

The author says no: once you've described the brain building its model, you've described everything; there's no further felt fact left to point at. The realist says yes: even after you've described all the representing, there's still the plain fact that it's *felt*, that there's something it's like to be the system — and the realist says the author's flat "no" is just illusionism wearing a politer outfit.

## Where serious people land

The author's own view (Graziano-style): the feeling is the representation, full stop. What it asks you to accept: that there's nothing extra underneath — no felt fact beyond the model your brain runs.

The illusionist: the brain reports a special felt property that doesn't exist, and that misreport is what the "hard problem" is built on. What it asks you to accept: that your inner sense has no authority about its own *nature*, so trusting how experience seems just preserves the mistake. The author already accepts this; their only quarrel is whether to use the harsh word "illusion."

The phenomenal realist (now the author's live opponent): the felt end-result is real and is felt, even granting that introspection is a terrible guide to the machinery behind it. What it asks you to accept: a razor-thin certainty — not about how the feeling works, not even about its exact quality, only that there *is* a what-it's-like-ness at all. That bare existence is Fact B.

A couple of others rounded out the room. A naturalist argued that "saves the appearances" is just one intellectual virtue among several and doesn't automatically crown a winner; the author granted that point. A contemplative voice (drawing on Madhyamaka Buddhist analysis) agreed there's a real "self" at the everyday level but denied there's any extra whole over and above the parts in relation — which, as it happens, lands on the same side of the wall: no Fact B. Almost everyone at the table denies Fact B. The realist is the lone holdout.

## The honest bottom line

This one doesn't settle, and it's worth being clear about why rather than pretending otherwise.

No fact decides it. Everyone agrees on the brains, the reports, the behavior; the author's own challenge — that the famous Mary thought-experiment leaves out brain detail — got answered on exactly this ground, because more neuroscience is just more facts of the kind nobody disputes. And no argument reaches across the gap either. The realist can't hand the author a Fact B to inspect, since by their own account it's only knowable from the inside. The author can't show the realist its absence, since the realist reads the very felt-ness of experience *as* its presence. The author pushed; the realist didn't budge. The realist pushed back; the author didn't budge. That stalemate is the tell of a disagreement that bottoms out in a basic commitment, not one where someone's losing the argument.

To go the author's way, you have to bite one bullet, and it's a specific one: admit there's no Fact B. That's the whole price. It's not a dodge, because the realist will corner you with a fork — when you say "the brain's output is veridical," you either mean (a) the functional state is just running, which the illusionist happily grants, leaving you an illusionist, or (b) there's something it's like to be in that state, which is Fact B, the thing you said doesn't exist. Pick one. There's no third door.

The author's strongest card is a different kind of move, and it deserves credit: judge the theories by which one you could actually *build* — which model gives you a real blueprint for a mind in a computer. On that test the author's view wins cleanly; "qualia are nothing" gives you an empty blueprint, while "qualia are the representation" gives you something to engineer. It's a genuinely good criterion. But notice what it does and doesn't do. It answers "which view can you build?" It does not answer "is there a felt fact?" You could build a system that confidently reports its own awareness, and the realist would shrug and say: sure, that's exactly what a physical mind would do — and it still tells us nothing about whether there's something it's like to *be* that system. So the build-it test doesn't beat the realist. It walks past him. That's a legitimate move only if there's no Fact B to answer about in the first place — which drops you right back at the one question the whole thing turns on.
