# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Reconstruction
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Reconstruct
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

---

## Standard form

**P1.** The agreed explanandum for any theory of phenomenal consciousness is the manifest datum that experience presents qualitative, felt character to introspection — that is, the fact that introspective reports consistently attribute felt, qualitative character to conscious states.

**P2.** A theory "saves the appearances" with respect to that explanandum if and only if it accounts for the explanandum without declaring the manifest datum illusory — i.e., without entailing that introspective reports of qualitative character are globally and systematically in error.

**P3.** Transparent representationalism accounts for the explanandum without entailing global introspective error: it identifies the felt, qualitative character reported by introspection with the representational content of experience directed at worldly properties, and treats introspective access to that content as broadly veridical.

**P4.** Illusionism (strong variety: Frankish) accounts for the explanandum only by entailing global introspective error: it holds that introspection systematically misrepresents functional/representational states as having phenomenal character they do not have.

**P5.** When two theories discharge the same explanandum, the theory that does so without a global error posit incurs lower theoretical debt on the conservatism dimension than the theory that requires one.

**P6.** A theory that incurs lower theoretical debt on the conservatism dimension, all else being equal, is the better model (on the axis of minimizing joint cost of saving appearances plus theoretical debt).

**P7.** All else is relevantly equal between transparent representationalism and illusionism on the other standard theory-choice dimensions (parsimony, empirical adequacy with respect to third-person facts, internal coherence).

**C.** Therefore, transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism.

---

## Validity

The inference is **valid** as reconstructed, subject to one qualification.

The argument has the structure of a comparative eliminative inference:

1. Both theories target the same explanandum. (P1)
2. "Better" is operationalized on a single axis: saves-appearances-at-lower-cost. (P2, P5, P6)
3. Theory A saves the appearances on that axis; Theory B does not — it incurs a specific extra cost. (P3, P4)
4. All other considerations are tied. (P7)
5. Therefore Theory A is better on the stated axis. (C follows from P3–P7 by conjunction and the definition of "better" at P6.)

The form is a variant of inference to the better explanation: given a shared explanandum and a shared axis of evaluation, the theory with lower cost wins. If all premises are granted, C follows deductively.

**The qualification.** P7 ("all else equal") is doing suppression work. The conclusion is comparative on the stated axis; it does not follow that transparency is better all-things-considered unless all other axes either tie or also favor transparency. As written, the argument is valid only on the restricted axis the thesis explicitly stakes (P6 pins this). The moment an opponent shifts to a different axis — ontological parsimony (illusionism posits no phenomenal properties), or hard-problem dissolution (illusionism makes the hard problem disappear by denying its explanandum) — the inference does not carry over. This is a scope issue, not a formal invalidity; the conclusion must be read with the axis restriction in force.

**No fallacy detected** in the P1–C chain as stated. The argument does not beg the question formally, but see the suppressed-premise flags below for a materially circular risk in P3.

---

## Where the weight sits

**P3 is the load-bearing premise.** The entire argument pivots on whether transparent representationalism genuinely saves the appearances — whether identifying phenomenal character with representational content actually accounts for what introspection reports, rather than silently replacing the explanandum with something else.

If P3 is denied — if it turns out that "veridical introspection of represented worldly properties" does not actually deliver the felt qualitative character that was the datum — then the core advantage collapses: transparency would be in the same position as illusionism, both failing to discharge the explanandum, with illusionism at least being candid about the failure. This is exactly the Chalmers/Goff line.

P4 is comparatively uncontroversial — strong illusionism stipulates the global error; denying P4 is just abandoning Frankish's position. P5 is a theory-choice principle most parties accept. P6 is true by the definition of "better" the thesis has explicitly pinned. P7 is the second-most contested premise (see below). But P3 is where the real contest is, because it is the premise that must do the positive work of establishing that the transparency maneuver is not a relocation or a redescription of the explanandum but a genuine explanation of it.

**P7 is the second site of weight.** The "all else equal" clause is plausible only if transparency and illusionism genuinely tie on parsimony and hard-problem dissolution. An illusionist will deny this: illusionism eliminates phenomenal properties from the ontology entirely, so it is the leaner theory on ontological parsimony, and it dissolves rather than merely answers the hard problem. If P7 fails, the argument can still establish that transparency wins on the conservatism axis, but cannot establish that transparency is the "better model" simpliciter — it would establish only a partial superiority on one axis while conceding inferiority on others. The thesis would need to argue that the conservatism axis is weightier, which introduces a further suppressed premise.

## Suppressed premises

**SP-A (The anti-relocation premise — the most dangerous gap, targeting P3).**

P3 assumes that identifying phenomenal character with representational content counts as genuinely accounting for felt qualitative character, not merely relocating or renaming the explanatory target. This requires the unstated premise:

> *SP-A: Representational content, as the transparency theorist characterizes it, is the kind of thing that can constitute or ground felt qualitative character — not merely co-vary with it or re-describe it.*

Without SP-A, P3 is at best asserting that transparency offers a candidate identification, not that the identification succeeds in accounting for the explanandum. The Chalmers/Goff objection is precisely that this premise is false: representing-that-the-tomato-is-red does not explain why there is something it is like to see red; the felt quality remains unexplained and is simply re-labeled as "the represented property." If SP-A is denied, P3 collapses, and with it the argument's core advantage.

**SP-B (The non-circular motivation premise — targeting P4's contrast).**

The thesis claims that illusionism "cannot non-circularly motivate" its global error posit. This is gestured at in the framing but does not appear as an explicit premise. The suppressed claim is:

> *SP-B: The only available motivation for positing global introspective error is the prior rejection of phenomenal realism — so the error posit presupposes what it is meant to explain.*

This is a powerful objection to illusionism if true, but it is not in the standard form as reconstructed. It would make P4 not merely descriptive (illusionism requires a global error) but additionally critical (illusionism cannot independently justify that error). The `hidden-premise-excavator` should develop this. If SP-B is true, P4's cost is understated — illusionism does not merely incur a conservatism penalty but incurs it with no non-circular budget.

**SP-C (The explanandum-stability premise — targeting P1 and P2 jointly).**

P1 specifies the explanandum as "introspective reports of qualitative character." P2 says saving the appearances means accounting for those reports without declaring them illusory. This pair presupposes:

> *SP-C: The explanandum is stable and theory-neutral — there really is a manifest datum of introspective reports of qualitative character that both theories must discharge, and neither theory is entitled to revise or dissolve that datum by fiat.*

Dennett denies SP-C. His heterophenomenological view is that "how things seem" is not a datum prior to theory; the "appearances" are themselves theory-laden constructs. If SP-C fails, P2 loses its grip: there is no fixed target to be "saved," and the conservatism-cost accounting breaks down entirely. The framer flags this as the Dennett vector. The `distinction-watchdog` should police whether council members exploit this to collapse the dispute rather than engage it.

**SP-D (The axis-weighting premise — targeting the move from partial to overall superiority).**

The thesis claims transparency is "better model" — a comparative all-in verdict — but the argument only establishes superiority on the conservatism axis. Moving from "wins on axis A" to "is the better model" requires:

> *SP-D: The conservatism axis (saving appearances at low cost) is the primary or decisive axis for theory-choice in this domain, such that winning on it suffices for an overall "better model" verdict even if the competing theory scores better on other axes.*

This premise is never stated. It is load-bearing: an illusionist who concedes conservatism inferiority but claims superiority on parsimony and hard-problem dissolution would insist that those axes are weightier. The argument as reconstructed does not establish SP-D; it assumes it via the operationalization of "better" in P6 combined with the all-else-equal stipulation in P7.

## Charitable variants

### Variant 1 (Strengthening P3 — the best case for transparency)

The strongest version of P3 runs: transparency does not merely identify phenomenal character with representational content and call the job done; it explains *why* introspection appears to report qualitative character by pointing to the structure of intentional directedness — experience is constitutively about its objects, so the "feel" of redness just is the experience's being directed at redness-out-there, with no residue. On this reading, the "appearance" of felt character is not left over after the representational story is told; it is identical to what the representational story describes. This is Tye's PANIC theory and Harman's transparency argument at their best. On this reading, the Chalmers/Goff relocation objection fails because there is no "felt residue" that has been relocated — the identity leaves nothing out.

This is the version the council should attack, not a weaker version that merely asserts identity without explaining why it is explanatorily satisfying.

### Variant 2 (Weakening to avoid the "saves" circularity worry)

If the objection is that "saving the appearances" of phenomenal character presupposes there is phenomenal character to be saved — thereby begging the question against illusionism — the thesis can be re-stated in observation-neutral terms:

> *Revised thesis: Transparent representationalism provides a more conservative account of the verbal and behavioral data about conscious experience — the things people say and do because of their experiences — because it posits fewer divergences between what subjects report and what their states actually are, and conservatism with respect to subject testimony is a standard theory-choice virtue.*

This retreats from "saving the phenomenal appearances" (which does carry phenomenal-realist baggage) to "saving the first-person testimony appearances" (which is theory-neutral: people report felt character, and we measure theoretical cost by how far a theory departs from those reports). On this variant, the argument does not presuppose that phenomenal character exists; it only notes that transparency posits fewer errors in first-person reports, which is an advantage under conservatism regardless of whether the reports are ultimately correct. This is the weakest version of the argument that still generates a genuine contrast with illusionism.

### Variant 3 (The Chalmers/Goff "relocation" objection as a charitable pressure test)

The framer asks that this variant be made explicit. Suppose the phenomenal realist argues:

> Neither transparency nor illusionism actually saves the phenomenal appearances; they differ only in how they fail. Illusionism denies felt character outright; transparency "saves" it by identifying it with something (representational content) that patently lacks the intrinsic qualitative character that was the datum. On a realist's lights, the argument's P3 is false: transparency does not save phenomenal character, it replaces it with representational content and relabels. The comparative claim is therefore that transparency loses less badly than illusionism — a much weaker thesis.

If this variant is correct, the correct reconstruction of the argument's actual epistemic position is:

> *P3* (realist-pressured): Transparent representationalism offers a candidate reduction of phenomenal character to representational content that, if successful, would save the appearances at lower cost than illusionism.
> *Additional premise needed*: The reduction succeeds (i.e., SP-A above).
> *C*: Therefore, if SP-A is true, transparency is better.

The thesis is then conditionally valid but not unconditionally so. This is the honest formulation if the argument is to survive the Chalmers/Goff attack: the argument holds only under the assumption that representationalist reduction is an adequate account of phenomenal character, which is precisely what the realist denies.

## Flags for downstream roles

- **Hidden-premise excavator:** Develop SP-A (anti-relocation) and SP-B (non-circular motivation). SP-A is where the whole crux likely relocates if the realist objection lands; SP-B is an independent critical edge against illusionism that may be the argument's sharpest tool but is currently buried.
- **Distinction-watchdog:** Police the slide between (i) "saves the phenomenal appearances" (presupposes phenomenal realism, per the circularity worry) and (ii) "saves the first-person testimony appearances" (theory-neutral). Also police the shift between the conservatism axis and the parsimony axis — these are being conflated in some presentations.
- **Self-application tester:** The thesis is a meta-theoretic claim about theory-choice virtues. Does the argument for it itself exhibit the virtues it prizes — conservatism about what we can establish, low theoretical debt? Or does it beg the question by assuming SP-A in the very act of formulating P3?
