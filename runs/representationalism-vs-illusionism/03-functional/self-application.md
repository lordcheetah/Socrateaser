# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Functional — self-application tester
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

---

SELF-APPLICATION: SURVIVES

## The test

The thesis is a meta-theoretic comparative claim: transparent representationalism is
the better model because it saves the appearances at lower cost, where "saving the
appearances" means accounting for the manifest datum of introspective reports of
qualitative character without declaring them globally erroneous. To apply this
claim, the asserter must: (a) identify the datum — the appearance of felt, qualitative
character — and (b) score two theories against it using a conservatism criterion.

Applied to itself: does the thesis, in the act of asserting its evaluative criterion,
presuppose what the criterion is meant to adjudicate — namely, that introspective
seemings constitute a real, stable datum?

Two angles were tested:

**Angle 1 — Epistemic bootstrapping (does judging "which model saves the appearance
better" presuppose access to the appearance that is the very thing in dispute?)**

Yes, it does — but this is symmetric. The thesis-asserter must consult introspective
evidence to locate and describe the explanandum. So must the illusionist, who must
equally appeal to how things introspectively seem in order to identify the "seeming"
they are explaining away. Every party to this debate, including Frankish, launches from
some level of introspective uptake. The epistemic dependence does not selectively
undercut the representationalist thesis; it is a shared precondition of the dispute.
No asymmetric self-defeat arises here.

**Angle 2 — Does the evaluative criterion beg the question against illusionism at the
level of its own application? (The sharper version.)**

The thesis's scoring criterion (P2: saving the appearances means not declaring them
illusory) presupposes that there are appearances — real, stable, theory-neutral
introspective data — to be saved. But this is exactly what SP-C (the explanandum-
stability premise) asserts, and what strong illusionism, at its most radical (the
Dennett vector), denies. If the illusionist is correct that the "appearance of
phenomenality" is itself the illusion to be dissolved rather than a pre-theoretical
datum, then the conservatism criterion has no grip: the scorecard the thesis uses to
declare itself the winner is one that, on the illusionist's own view, was built to
favor the representationalist outcome.

This is a structural tension, not a logical contradiction. The thesis does not
contradict itself when stated; it presupposes a disputed background condition (SP-C)
in framing its evaluation criterion. That is a question-begging risk — a form of
self-exemption from the dispute about what counts as data — but it falls short of
self-refutation for two reasons:

1. **The circularity is not vicious at the logical level.** The thesis says "theory A
   handles the agreed data better." Denying that there is agreed data is a denial of
   a premise (SP-C), not a demonstration that the thesis contradicts itself when
   applied to itself. Self-refutation requires that the thesis, when applied to its
   own case, produces a contradiction. Here it produces a challenged presupposition —
   a different, weaker failure mode that belongs to the council, not to the
   self-application test.

2. **The tension is symmetric in an important respect.** The illusionist's counter-
   criterion — that the better theory dissolves the hard problem and achieves
   ontological parsimony — equally presupposes that "dissolving the hard problem" is
   the decisive axis and that phenomenal properties are the kind of thing worth
   eliminating. Neither side can state its evaluative criterion from a view from
   nowhere. The representationalist's reliance on SP-C is not a special reflexive
   vulnerability; it is the ordinary theory-choice situation in which the choice of
   axes is itself part of the dispute.

## Result

SURVIVES: applying the thesis to itself yields no contradiction. The thesis is a
comparative claim about explanatory cost; applied to its own assertion, it does not
assert something that defeats its own grounds. The genuine reflexive tension —
that the conservatism criterion presupposes a stable datum (SP-C) whose stability
the opposing theory denies — is real but symmetric and sub-self-refutation in
severity. It is a question-begging risk the council should contest, not a structural
self-defeat detectable at the self-application level.

The reconstruction's own flag ("Does the argument beg the question by assuming SP-A
in the very act of formulating P3?") identifies a materially circular risk in P3 —
specifically, that "identifies phenomenal character with representational content and
treats introspection as veridical" quietly assumes SP-A (the anti-relocation premise),
which is the contested claim. This is a suppressed-premise problem, not a self-
application failure; it belongs to hidden-premise analysis, not here.

## If it fails: can it be repaired?

The thesis does not fail the self-application test, so no repair is strictly required.
However, the closest thing to a reflexive vulnerability — the SP-C presupposition that
there is a theory-neutral datum to be saved — has a principled, non-ad-hoc repair
already on offer in the reconstruction's Variant 2:

> Restate the datum as first-person verbal and behavioral reports (what subjects say
> and do) rather than "phenomenal appearances." Conservatism is then measured by
> divergence from subject testimony, which is theory-neutral: people report felt
> character, and we count how many of those reports each theory must declare erroneous.

This retreat does not immunize the thesis against all objections, but it removes the
SP-C circularity: the datum is no longer "there are phenomenal appearances" (contested)
but "there are first-person reports of felt character" (uncontested by all parties,
including Frankish). The criterion can then be applied without presupposing the answer
to the first-order question. The cost is that the thesis no longer claims to be saving
phenomenal appearances specifically — only to be more conservative about subject
testimony — which is a weaker but more defensible position.
