# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Functional — counterexample generator
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

---

COUNTEREXAMPLES: 7 strong | 2 weak | none decisive alone, but Cases 1 and 4 jointly narrow the thesis significantly

---

## Cases

---

### Case 1 — Block's Inverted Earth

- **Case:** Block (1990, "Inverted Earth," *Philosophical Perspectives* 4: 53-79) imagines a planet where everything is colored the complementary color of its Earth counterpart: the sky is yellow, grass is red, ripe tomatoes are green. A subject is fitted with color-inverting lenses before being transported there, so the lenses re-invert the environmental colors and her visual system receives the same proximal stimulations as on Earth. After long acclimatization, her inner state that used to be caused by red things is now reliably caused by green things — and by teleological/causal-covariation criteria, it now *represents* greenness. Yet, Block argues, her experience of looking at the Inverted-Earth sky still feels yellow to her. Same qualia, different representational content.

- **What the thesis predicts:** Transparent representationalism holds that phenomenal character just is (or is constituted by) the representational content directed at worldly properties, and that introspection of experience reveals those worldly properties veridically. If the subject's state now represents greenness, her experience of the sky should have green phenomenal character — it should feel green. If it still feels yellow, phenomenal character has come apart from representational content.

- **The tension:** The case appears to exhibit a dissociation between phenomenal character and representational content that the transparency thesis is committed to denying. If the dissociation is real, P3 fails: identifying felt character with represented content does not save the appearance that experience has *intrinsic* qualitative character distinguishable from what is currently represented. The thesis claims to save the appearances of phenomenal character at lower cost than illusionism; but if phenomenal character can remain fixed while content shifts, transparency has not explained the character — it has merely renamed it and left the residue unexplained. Illusionism, which denies there is a residual phenomenal fact to track, may be no worse off.

- **Force:** Narrowing. The case does not straightforwardly refute transparency; Tye and Lycan have contested whether the subject's state really does change its representational content after acclimatization, arguing that the relevant "wide" content is determined by the subject's original environmental history, so the state still represents redness even on Inverted Earth (the "long arm" reply). But if this reply is accepted, it comes at a cost: the defender must embrace a strongly externalist, historical account of content determination, which tightens the grip of the next counterexample (Case 4, Swampman).

- **Likely repair:** Accept historical/teleological wide representationalism: the state's content is fixed by its evolutionary and developmental history, so it still represents redness on Inverted Earth, and phenomenal character (being that represented property) remains yellow-ish redness. Cost: this move makes transparency hostage to a specific externalist semantics for mental content — a contested add-on premise — and immediately generates the Swampman vulnerability below.

### Case 2 — Block's Phenomenal Overflow (Sperling Paradigm)

- **Case:** Block (*Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 2007; "Consciousness, Accessibility, and the Mesh Between Psychology and Neuroscience," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 2007) argues from Sperling's classic partial-report experiments: subjects see a 3×4 grid of letters briefly flashed; they can accurately report any cued row, whichever row is indicated *after* offset — implying that all 12 letters were phenomenally conscious at the moment of display, even though only 3–4 could be accessed and reported. Phenomenal consciousness outstrips access consciousness: there is more in experience than is cognitively available.

- **What the thesis predicts:** Transparent representationalism, in its canonical formulations (Tye's PANIC theory; Dretske's "natural information" account), ties phenomenal character to poised, apt, and non-conceptual intentional content — content that is, crucially, *available* for the guidance of reasoning and action. On the standard formulation, only content that is suitably available (poised) generates phenomenal character. If phenomenal character overflows availability, the thesis's account of what makes content phenomenally present is incomplete at best and false at worst: there are phenomenally conscious states that are not suitably poised.

- **The tension:** If overflow is real, phenomenal character attaches to representational contents that are not globally broadcast — not the kind of content transparency's account specifies. The thesis's P3 says transparency "accounts for the explanandum without entailing global introspective error." But if it cannot account for overflow experiences (which subjects sincerely report as phenomenally present), it fails to save *those* appearances without either (a) denying the overflow — effectively a local error theory about those reports — or (b) revising the content account to drop the "poised" requirement, at which point it becomes unclear what constrains which representations generate phenomenal character.

- **Force:** Narrowing. The empirical status of overflow is genuinely contested; critics (Kouider, Prinz, Carruthers) argue Sperling does not show phenomenal consciousness beyond access. If the empirical dispute resolves against overflow, this case collapses. Its force is conditional on accepting Block's reading of the evidence. Still, even the possibility of overflow forces transparency to articulate what, exactly, constrains which representations are phenomenally present — a constraint the thesis's formulation in P3 currently underspecifies.

- **Likely repair:** Deny that Sperling subjects are phenomenally conscious of the uncued letters; what they have is a poised-but-not-yet-accessed iconic buffer that does not yet constitute phenomenal experience. Cost: this is itself a local introspective error posit — the subjects report seeing all the letters, and the transparency theorist must say they are mistaken about the extent of their own experience. That is a conservatism cost that partially erodes the thesis's claimed advantage over illusionism.

### Case 3 — Blurry Vision and After-Images (Transparency Under Internal Strain)

- **Case:** When one looks through a smudged lens or has a defocused eye, experience has a quality of blurriness. This blurriness does not seem to be attributed to the objects seen — the chair is not experienced as having blurry edges intrinsically; the blurriness appears to be a feature of how the chair is experienced. Similarly, an after-image of a bright light appears as a colored disc that one knows is not in the world; its reddish or greenish character does not represent any worldly property because the subject knows no such property is there. Both cases appear to be ones where introspected character does not track any currently represented worldly property.

- **What the thesis predicts:** Transparency holds that introspection reveals only the represented worldly properties. If blurriness is a phenomenal feature of experience, it must represent some worldly property (perhaps: that edge-locations are indeterminate, as Tye proposes). If after-image color represents anything, it must represent a property that was there — either a relict representation of the prior stimulus, or a mis-representation of the current visual field.

- **The tension:** The representationalist must either (a) explain blurriness and after-image color as genuine representations of worldly properties (Tye's move — blurriness represents indeterminate edges; after-image color represents properties of the prior stimulus via a persisting representation) or (b) concede that these are not transparent cases — that introspection here discloses features of experience that are not worldly-property representations, undermining the universality of transparency. Option (a) strains the notion of "represented worldly property" to accommodate internal states that present no worldly distal property at all (an after-image of green-after-red is not about the prior red thing in any direct sense). Option (b) concedes that introspection is not always transparent — which is the illusionist's foot in the door.

- **Force:** Narrowing. Not decisive — Tye's responses are developed and technically viable. But the case forces transparency to accept a progressively more elaborate account of what "worldly property" includes (indeterminate edge-locations, persistence of prior stimuli, etc.), which may begin to lose the simplicity advantage the thesis claims. Schroer (2007, "Seeing It All Clearly") and Peacocke have pressed this in detail.

- **Likely repair:** Accept that blurriness represents an indeterminate distal property (the edge really is where the blur locates it — the subject lacks information to locate it precisely); accept that after-images represent properties of the prior stimulus still active in the visual system. Cost: the "worldly property" scope must stretch to cover internal traces and indeterminate edge-locations, which the phenomenal realist will argue is a relocation of the phenomenal datum rather than an explanation of it.

### Case 4 — Swampman (Content Without History)

- **Case:** Davidson's Swampman: a lightning strike in a swamp happens to produce, atom-for-atom, a perfect physical duplicate of a human being. The duplicate has never interacted with the world; it has no causal history linking its internal states to environmental features. By teleological or causal-covariation representational content theories (Dretske 1981, 1988; Tye 1995), Swampman's internal states have no representational content — there is no history of co-variation and no biological teleology grounding "what they are for."

- **What the thesis predicts:** If phenomenal character is constituted by representational content (transparency), and Swampman has no representational content, then Swampman has no phenomenal character. When Swampman opens his eyes, there is nothing it is like to be him — no experience, no qualia. This is what the thesis commits the transparency theorist to.

- **The tension:** This is extremely hard to accept. Swampman is physically and functionally identical to a conscious person. Denying him experience seems to require that phenomenal character depends not on any current physical or functional properties but on historical facts about how those properties were acquired — a counterintuitive and arguably irrelevant condition on experience. Illusionism faces no such problem: it ties consciousness to current functional states, not historical ones, and Swampman's functional states exist. On this case, illusionism is arguably the more parsimonious account precisely because it does not require the historical grounding that transparency inherits from content externalism.

- **Force:** Narrowing, with some decisive bite against the thesis's P7 ("all else equal"). If transparency requires teleological content externalism to handle Inverted Earth (Case 1), and teleological content externalism generates implausible verdicts about Swampman, then the thesis's claim that transparency has lower theoretical debt than illusionism is complicated: transparency's debt includes externalist content semantics plus the cost of denying experience to structural duplicates. Illusionism, which operates on current functional organization, avoids both debts. P7 looks false, and the thesis loses its all-else-equal umbrella.

- **Likely repair:** Adopt a narrow-content or internalist version of representationalism (representations are constituted by current functional organization, not history); or bite the bullet and deny Swampman has experience. The internalist move costs the reply to Inverted Earth (Case 1): if content is narrow, the acclimatized subject on Inverted Earth has changed content, and phenomenal character should change with it — but Block's intuition is that it doesn't. The bullet-biting move concedes the counterintuitive result and must argue it is acceptable. Neither repair is free.

### Case 5 — Perceptual Presence and the Unseen Back of the Cup

- **Case:** When I see a coffee cup on my desk, I experience it as a three-dimensional object with a back, a bottom, and an inside that I cannot currently see. This "perceptual presence" of the unseen sides is a robust phenomenological datum (Husserl; O'Regan and Noë, 2001, "A sensorimotor account of vision and visual consciousness," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 24). The cup has a phenomenal presence as a full object, not as a half-disc. But my visual system is not representing the specific properties of the cup's unseen side — I have no representation of what color the inside is or whether there is a chip in the handle.

- **What the thesis predicts:** Transparency identifies phenomenal character with represented worldly properties. The perceptual presence of the cup's back should correspond to a representation of properties the back has. But I am not representing any specific property of the back — I lack the information. If phenomenal presence outruns specific property representation, transparency cannot account for it.

- **The tension:** The phenomenal character of "full objectual presence" is not captured by a list of represented surface properties. The experience as-of a solid three-dimensional object has a qualitative character — an anticipatory, embodied directedness — that exceeds what is representationally specified. If this character is real (and introspection strongly suggests it is), P3 is incomplete: the transparency account does not cover the full scope of what introspection reports as phenomenally present.

- **Force:** Weak to narrowing. Representationalists can respond that the represented content includes dispositional properties ("the cup is such that rotating it would reveal..."), or that the relevant content is the representation of a *kind* (coffee-cup) which includes the full-object structure. These are developed responses. The case has more bite against "strong" transparency — the claim that ALL phenomenal character is exhausted by currently represented surface properties — than against moderate versions.

- **Likely repair:** Move to "rich" representational content that includes dispositions, higher-order kinds, and anticipatory structure. Cost: the content posit becomes increasingly theoretical and less directly tied to what introspection reveals, which is exactly the Chalmers/Goff relocation concern.

### Case 6 — The Acquaintance Asymmetry (Pressure on Illusionism, Comparative Advantage for the Thesis)

- **Case:** Chalmers ("Debunking Arguments for Illusionism," *consc.net*, 2020) presses the acquaintance objection: when I have a pain or a visual experience, I seem to be directly acquainted with its phenomenal character in a way that is not mediated by inference. Illusionism says this acquaintance is itself the illusion — I merely functionally represent there being phenomenal character. But there is a disanalogy with ordinary perceptual illusions: in a visual illusion, there is a real perception (which is accurate) of a scene, and a false belief about the scene. In the illusionist case, the "illusion of phenomenal consciousness" would require a real experience that generates the false appearance of phenomenal character — but what is that experience? If it is itself phenomenal, illusionism has not explained it; if it is not, the "illusion" is a functional state that never seems like anything, in which case the "seeming of phenomenal character" has no actual seeming-base to explain.

- **What illusionism predicts:** That the acquaintance seeming is itself a functional/representational state that mis-attributes phenomenal character where there is none. Frankish: the illusion is not phenomenal but cognitive — a proto-representational system that generates phenomenal-character judgments without there being any phenomenal fact they accurately track.

- **The tension:** Frankish's reply works for third-person behavioral outputs but faces the "meta-problem" in first-person: the pressure is not just that we *say* there is phenomenal character but that it *seems that there is*, and "seeming" is itself a phenomenal-sounding locution. If the illusionist uses "seems" in a functional/behavioral sense, the challenge is to explain why that functional-seeming state feels the way it does (which reintroduces the explanandum). If they deny the functional-seeming state feels any way, they have conceded the hard problem for that state.

- **Force as a pressure on illusionism:** Narrowing to strong. This is the best case *in favor* of the thesis: it shows that illusionism incurs a non-trivial explanatory debt of its own — accounting for the phenomenal-seeming character of the very states it invokes to replace phenomenal consciousness — that it cannot easily discharge without circularity. This is the SP-B suppressed premise from the reconstruction, now cashed out as a concrete case. It does not prove transparency wins, but it contests P4's flatness: the illusionist's error posit is not just a conservatism cost but an explanatory regress.

- **Likely repair (illusionism's):** Frankish's reply: illusions need not be phenomenal illusions; the "seeming" is a cognitive/theoretical representation, not a phenomenal seeming. Cost: this requires a sharp distinction between cognitive-seeming and phenomenal-seeming that is itself contentious, and any argument that cognitive-seeming is not phenomenal-seeming relies on exactly the conceptual analysis whose verdict is the crux of the debate.

### Case 7 — The Explanatory Gap as a Counterexample to P3 (Chalmers/Goff Relocation Objection)

- **Case:** Chalmers (1996, *The Conscious Mind*; 2002, "Does Conceivability Entail Possibility?") and Goff (2019, *Galileo's Error*) argue that representing-that-x-is-red does not explain why there is something it is like to represent-that-x-is-red. Even if we accept that phenomenal character is constituted by representational content, the explanatory gap remains: we still cannot explain why *this* representational relation generates *this* felt quality. The transparency theorist has given us an identity — phenomenal character = represented worldly property — but not an explanation of why the identity holds or why the identity leaves the felt dimension present rather than absent.

- **What the thesis predicts:** P3 claims transparency "accounts for the explanandum without entailing global introspective error." If the Chalmers/Goff objection is correct, transparency does not genuinely account for the explanandum — it relabels it. The felt character is not explained but renamed as "the represented property," and the explanatory work remains undone.

- **The tension:** If P3 fails (transparency does not save the appearances but merely relocates them), the thesis's comparative claim collapses. Both theories fail to discharge the explanandum; they differ only in honesty about the failure. The Chalmers/Goff position is that illusionism, by denying the explanandum, is at least intellectually honest about what it cannot explain, whereas transparency pretends to explain by identification while leaving the felt residue untouched. The thesis would then be comparing two equal failures, and P5–C would not carry.

- **Force:** Narrowing, possibly decisive against the thesis's *strongest* form. The force is conditional on the Chalmers/Goff intuition that representational identity does not explain felt character — which is exactly what the hard problem asserts and which illusionists deny by dissolving the explanandum. This is not a clean knockdown because it requires accepting phenomenal realism to make it bite; someone who already accepts transparency's reduction will not find the explanatory gap compelling. However, it directly targets SP-A (the anti-relocation premise), which the reconstruction flags as the thesis's most dangerous gap. If SP-A is false, the argument is conditionally valid but not unconditionally so, and the thesis weakens to "transparency wins *if* representational reduction succeeds" — a much narrower claim.

- **Likely repair:** Argue that representational identity is explanatorily satisfying because there is no explanatory residue once the identity is accepted; the felt character just *is* the represented worldly property, so asking "why does representing redness feel red?" is like asking "why does water make things wet?" — a pseudo-question. This is the standard type-identity move. Cost: this requires the identity to be necessary (not contingent), and Chalmers' conceivability argument against type-identity is designed precisely to resist this. The repair is available but itself requires substantive philosophical defense not built into the thesis as stated.

## Summary Assessment

The two strongest cases are **Case 1 (Inverted Earth)** and **Case 4 (Swampman)**: they work in tandem, forcing transparency either to accept historical externalism (to handle Case 1) or to deny phenomenal character to functional duplicates (Case 4). Neither alone is decisive, but together they reveal that P7 ("all else equal") is underdefended: transparency inherits the costs of a specific, contested content semantics that illusionism avoids. **Case 7 (Explanatory Gap)** is the most philosophically fundamental but depends on phenomenal realist premises that the thesis's own terms are designed to remain neutral about.

The case that most straightforwardly *favors* the thesis is **Case 6 (Acquaintance Asymmetry)**: it shows that illusionism's apparent advantage (explaining away phenomenal character) generates an explanatory regress about the "seeming" that anchors the illusion, which is itself a non-trivial theoretical debt the thesis can claim for the conservatism ledger.

No single case here is a decisive refutation of the thesis as carefully stated. The thesis's qualified formulation — "better on the axis of saving appearances at lower cost" — has room to absorb most cases by adding qualifications. The joint cost of those qualifications is what the synthesizer should weigh.
