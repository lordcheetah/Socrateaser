# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Functional — hidden-premise excavator
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

## Suppressed premises

---

### SP-A — The constitution premise (anti-relocation)
**Stated form:** Representational content, as the transparency theorist characterizes it, is the kind of thing that can *constitute* or *ground* felt qualitative character — not merely co-vary with it, re-describe it, or be nomically correlated with it.

**Where it's needed:** P3 asserts that transparency "identifies the felt, qualitative character reported by introspection with the representational content of experience" and thereby accounts for the explanandum. Without SP-A, this identification is merely a naming move: the argument would be claiming that giving something two names constitutes an explanation of it. The inference from "transparency identifies character with content" to "transparency accounts for character" goes through only if identity of this kind is constitutively explanatory — which is exactly what the hard-problem tradition denies. Without SP-A, P3 reduces to: transparency *asserts* that content = character, not that it *explains* why character is present.

**How contestable:** Hidden crux candidate. This is the most contested premise in the neighborhood of consciousness studies. The entire explanatory-gap literature (Levine 1983, Chalmers 1996) is precisely an argument that no representational or functional identification satisfies it. The phenomenal realist denies SP-A explicitly; the illusionist implicitly agrees it cannot be satisfied (which is why they think eliminativism is cheaper). Even many sympathetic to representationalism (e.g., Ned Block) think SP-A is unearned. It is the most contestable premise in the argument and the one most completely absent from the standard form.

**Who would deny it:** Chalmers/Goff-style phenomenal realists (the identification leaves the felt residue unexplained — "Mary still learns something new"); Ned Block (phenomenal consciousness outruns representational content — the access/phenomenal distinction); higher-order theorists (content of what order? first-order representation doesn't explain why there is something it is like); illusionists who agree the identification fails but draw the eliminativist conclusion rather than the realist one.

**Prime crux candidate — yes.** SP-A is the single premise where the transparency theorist, the phenomenal realist, and the illusionist all part ways in different directions simultaneously. If SP-A is true, the transparency argument wins. If SP-A is false-because-impossible, the phenomenal realist's objection lands. If SP-A is false-but-reachable-by-elimination, the illusionist's revisionary move gains traction. The entire verdict space branches here.

### SP-B — The non-circular motivation premise
**Stated form:** The only available motivation for positing global introspective error (as illusionism requires) is the prior, independent rejection of phenomenal realism — so the error posit presupposes what it is meant to explain away, making the illusionist's central move circular.

**Where it's needed:** The thesis claims illusionism "cannot non-circularly motivate" its global error posit. This claim is invoked in the framing and implicit in the contrast with transparency's conservatism, but it is not stated as a premise in the standard form. The inference that illusionism incurs *unjustified* theoretical debt — rather than merely *high* theoretical debt — requires SP-B. Without it, P4 is descriptive only (illusionism requires global error) and P5 treats both theories as incurring legitimate costs that can be compared. With SP-B, the argument becomes asymmetric: illusionism's cost is not merely high but self-undermining, because the motivation for it circles back through the very rejection of phenomenal properties that it is supposed to establish.

**How contestable:** Live/disputed. Frankish would deny SP-B directly: illusionism is motivated by the general explanatory success of physicalism, by the theoretical parsimony of eliminating a philosophically problematic ontological category, and by the specific failure of all attempts to naturalistically account for thick qualia — none of which presuppose the prior elimination of phenomenal properties. The illusionist can argue that the motivation is inference-to-the-best-explanation from the combination of physicalist background commitments plus the explanatory intractability of phenomenal realism — a motivation that is not circular but abductive. SP-B thus embeds a contestable claim about the order of justification in the illusionist's dialectic.

**Who would deny it:** Frankish and Dennett; any philosopher who thinks parsimony-driven eliminativism is independently motivated without presupposing the falsity of what it eliminates (compare: eliminative materialism about folk-psychological states is not usually charged with circularity just because it rejects folk ontology).

### SP-C — The explanandum-stability premise
**Stated form:** The explanandum — the manifest datum that introspection reports qualitative, felt character — is a stable, theory-neutral fixed target that both theories must discharge; neither theory is entitled to revise, dissolve, or re-describe that datum as part of its theoretical move.

**Where it's needed:** P1 specifies the explanandum, and P2 operationalizes "saving the appearances" relative to it. The entire conservatism-cost accounting in P5 and P6 presupposes that the target does not shift when a theory is applied to it. If a theory can legitimately revise the explanandum — say, by arguing that what looks like a datum of qualitative character is actually a theory-laden construct — then the cost accounting breaks down: there is no fixed benchmark against which to measure conservatism. Without SP-C, an illusionist can respond to P4 by saying "the 'error' I posit is not an error relative to a fixed datum; I am arguing that there was never a datum of phenomenal character in the first place, only a theoretical projection we made prior to better understanding."

**How contestable:** Live/disputed. Dennett explicitly denies SP-C via heterophenomenology: there is no phenomenally characterized datum prior to theory; "how things seem" is already theoretically mediated. He would say the argument's P1 begs the question by treating the deliverances of introspection as data independent of the theoretical frameworks that interpret them. Frankish's illusionism also partially denies SP-C: the "appearance" of qualitative character is itself the illusion being explained, not a neutral input to be preserved.

**Who would deny it:** Dennett (heterophenomenology — first-person reports are the data, not their interpreted content); Frankish (the appearance of phenomenality is itself the explanandum, not a fixed starting point); pragmatists in the Dewey/Rorty tradition who deny that there is raw phenomenal given independent of language and practice.

### SP-D — The axis-weighting premise
**Stated form:** The conservatism axis (saving appearances at lowest theoretical cost) is the primary or decisive axis for theory-choice in the domain of consciousness theorizing — such that winning on it suffices for an overall "better model" verdict even if the competing theory performs better on other recognized axes (ontological parsimony, hard-problem dissolution, unification with physics).

**Where it's needed:** The argument establishes that transparency outperforms illusionism on the conservatism axis (via P3–P5). The conclusion C says transparency is the "better model." Moving from axis-specific superiority to overall superiority requires that the conservatism axis either (a) outweighs all others or (b) that all other axes tie (P7). P7 is explicitly contested by illusionists who claim superiority on parsimony and hard-problem dissolution. So even granting P7's denial, the argument can rescue C only by invoking SP-D — that the conservatism axis is *decisive* even when not the only axis in play. SP-D is never stated; without it, winning on conservatism only establishes that transparency is "the more conservative theory," not "the better model."

**How contestable:** Live/disputed. Different philosophical traditions weight theory-choice virtues differently. Naturalistically-minded philosophers often weight parsimony and unification with the physical sciences heavily; phenomenologically-minded philosophers weight conservatism about the appearances. The thesis's own operationalization of "better" attempts to settle this by fiat (P6 pins the axis), but whether that operationalization is itself the right one for consciousness theorizing is exactly what Frankish and Dennett contest. Granting SP-D effectively means granting that the framing of the debate (conservatism-first) is the correct framing — which is a substantive philosophical commitment, not a neutral starting point.

**Who would deny it:** Frankish (ontological parsimony and elimination of the hard problem are the decisive axes; conservatism that preserves a philosophical mistake is a vice, not a virtue); Dennett (the conservatism axis presupposes a mistaken epistemology of first-person evidence); eliminativist naturalists generally (consilience with physical theory outweighs conservatism about folk-psychological appearances).

### SP-E — The veridicality-uniformity premise
**Stated form:** Introspection is broadly veridical across the full range of phenomenal reports — not merely veridical about some features of experience (e.g., intentional content) while unreliable about others (e.g., the intrinsic "what-it-is-like" quality). The reliability is uniform enough that "saves the appearances" can be applied to introspection as a whole.

**Where it's needed:** P3 says transparency "treats introspective access to [representational] content as broadly veridical." The "broadly veridical" formulation is crucial: it needs to cover enough of the phenomenal domain to justify the "saves the appearances" verdict. But there is a selective-reliability gap: transparency might be veridical about the *intentional content* of experience (what the experience represents) while remaining silent or even wrong about the *phenomenal character* of that experience (what it is like to undergo it). If introspection is veridical only about the former, transparency "saves" only part of the appearance — the part that concerns what the experience is about, not the part that concerns how it feels. Without SP-E, P3's "veridical" applies only to the intentional component, and the argument cannot claim to have saved the phenomenal appearances.

**How contestable:** Live/disputed. This is the Ned Block objection made structural: Block's phenomenal/access consciousness distinction implies that veridical access to access-consciousness (roughly, intentional/representational content) does not entail veridical access to phenomenal consciousness. A "higher phenomenology" realist would say transparency proves veridicality about one kind of state by analysis, then silently applies it to a different kind. The selectivity would vitiate P3's force.

**Who would deny it:** Block (P-consciousness outruns A-consciousness; veridicality about representational content is veridicality about A-consciousness, not P-consciousness); Levine (there is an explanatory gap between the represented property and the felt quality that veridicality about representation does not bridge); phenomenologists in the Husserlian tradition who distinguish noetic and noematic aspects of intentionality and hold that introspective access to the phenomenal character of the noetic act is categorically different from access to represented content.

### SP-F — The non-equivalence premise
**Stated form:** Transparent representationalism and illusionism are genuinely rival theories — they make different predictions or issue different verdicts about at least one fact — rather than being notational variants or two descriptions of the same theoretical posture at different levels.

**Where it's needed:** The entire argument is comparative: theory A beats theory B. A comparative argument presupposes real rivalry. But both theories are physicalist, both deny thick qualia, both agree on all third-person neural facts, and both issue identical verdicts on behavioral evidence. The rivalry is internal to the interpretation of first-person introspective reports. Without SP-F, the argument might be comparing two ways of describing the same metaphysical landscape — in which case neither "beats" the other and the comparative structure collapses. This is related to the pseudo-dispute risk the framer identifies, but as a premise it goes further: even granting that the dispute is verbal at the surface, SP-F asserts it is real underneath.

**How contestable:** Granted by most parties in the dispute — the framer already shows there is a genuine non-verbal residue (is introspection veridical or not?). SP-F is effectively conceded by everyone with a stake in the debate and is unlikely to be the site of productive disagreement. Marking it nonetheless because the "two descriptions" challenge is a live move in deflationary philosophy of mind.

**Who would deny it:** Eliminative deflationists who hold that both theories' "explanatory" languages are equally post-hoc redescriptions of sub-personal processing; pragmatists who hold that the choice is purely verbal because no practical difference hangs on it.

### SP-G — The introspection-report identity premise
**Stated form:** What introspective reports report — the datum that must be saved — is phenomenal character as it presents itself in experience, not merely the verbal behavior and functional states that accompany such reports. The "appearances" to be saved are the appearances *of experience*, not merely the appearances of *talk about experience*.

**Where it's needed:** P1 and P2 fix the explanandum as "introspective reports of qualitative character." There are two readings of "introspective reports": (i) reports as evidence of an underlying phenomenal state, where the datum is the state itself; (ii) reports as the datum, where what must be explained is the verbal/behavioral output, not an underlying feel. If reading (ii) is correct, illusionism does save the appearances: it explains why people produce the verbal report "I feel a vivid red" without attributing phenomenal character to any state. On reading (i), illusionism fails to save the appearances because the datum is the felt character, not the report. Without SP-G specifying that reading (i) is the intended one, the argument's P1–P2 are ambiguous and the "saving" criterion can be satisfied by either theory.

**How contestable:** Live/disputed. Dennett explicitly opts for reading (ii): the datum just is the verbal/behavioral output, and there is no further fact about phenomenal character to be saved beyond explaining the output. His heterophenomenology is precisely a methodology that takes reports as the data without committing to what (if anything) they are reports of. If SP-G is denied, the conservatism cost advantage assigned to transparency in P3 evaporates: transparency and illusionism tie on saving the verbal/behavioral appearances, differing only on whether they posit a further phenomenal fact — and the illusionist denies that further fact is real.

**Who would deny it:** Dennett; Ryle-style behaviorists and their descendants; functionalists who think the explanandum is fully captured by the functional role of the state, not its phenomenal character.

### SP-H — The no-theoretical-peers premise
**Stated form:** Among theories of consciousness that are both physicalist and committed to saving the (first-person) appearances, transparent representationalism and illusionism are the primary competitors — there is no third theory that achieves what transparency claims to achieve (veridical introspection, no phenomenal property over and above representational content) without the costs transparency incurs.

**Where it's needed:** The argument's comparative logic ("A is better than B") does not establish that A is the best available option unless the space of competitors is fully surveyed. If a third theory — say, a sophisticated higher-order representationalism, a relational/enactivist account, or a biological naturalism (Searle) — achieves lower joint cost than transparency, the conclusion C does not follow; at best, C is "transparency beats illusionism," not "transparency is a better model" simpliciter. SP-H is the assumption that the comparison is exhaustive for the relevant theoretical space.

**How contestable:** Granted for the purposes of the binary comparison the thesis explicitly sets up; the thesis is phrased as a two-theory comparative. However, phenomenal realists and higher-order theorists would contest SP-H implicitly by arguing that the thesis has structured the choice to exclude the options that avoid the dilemma entirely. Marking as obvious/granted within the thesis's scope, but live/disputed as a framing choice that could be contested from outside.

**Who would deny it:** Higher-order representationalists (Rosenthal — consciousness requires the right kind of higher-order representation, which changes the "savings" calculation); enactivists/embodied cognition theorists (Noë, Thompson — consciousness is constituted by sensorimotor engagement, not internal representation at all, so "transparent representationalism" misdescribes the phenomenon from the start); Searle's biological naturalism (which claims to save the phenomenal appearances without either transparency's intentional identity or illusionism's error theory, via causal emergence at the biological level).

### SP-I — The conservatism-as-epistemic-virtue premise
**Stated form:** Conservatism with respect to first-person introspective reports is a genuine epistemic virtue in consciousness theorizing — not merely a pragmatic default or a sociological constraint — such that a theory that violates conservatism incurs a *real* explanatory debt, not merely a departure from folk intuition that is acceptable collateral damage in mature theorizing.

**Where it's needed:** P5 says that a theory that saves the appearances without a global error posit "incurs lower theoretical debt on the conservatism dimension." This is only a *debt* — something that counts against a theory — if conservatism in this domain is an epistemic virtue. If it is merely a pragmatic preference (we start with folk intuitions and revise as theory matures), then illusionism's departure from it is simply the cost of theoretical progress, and it carries no more weight than, say, the "theoretical debt" of abandoning caloric theory of heat. Without SP-I, the conservatism axis loses its normative force: illusionism can simply say "yes, we depart from appearances — that is what mature science does," and the conservatism premium transparency earns is worthless.

**How contestable:** Live/disputed, and philosophically deep. The status of first-person evidence is itself contested in philosophy of mind. Churchland-style eliminativists hold that folk-psychological intuitions have exactly the epistemic standing of pre-scientific intuitions about heat and combustion — they should be revised by third-person scientific success, not preserved as constraints. Frankish explicitly makes this move: "saving the appearances" of phenomenality is preserving a mistake, not a virtue. The normative weight of conservatism is thus not a neutral background condition but a substantive commitment that the anti-eliminativist must defend. SP-I is less visible than SP-A but is arguably the meta-level premise that underlies the entire conservatism-cost framework.

**Who would deny it:** Eliminativists of all stripes (Churchland, Frankish, Dennett in his more revisionary moments); strong naturalists who hold that mature physical theory is the ultimate arbiter of what is real and that phenomenal intuitions carry no special evidential weight; pragmatists who hold that the only virtue of conservatism is predictive convenience, not metaphysical insight.

**Prime hidden-crux candidate: SP-A**, because it is the premise that (a) the stated argument most silently depends on, (b) is the most philosophically contested in consciousness studies, and (c) splits the verdict space in multiple directions simultaneously — phenomenal realists deny it from one flank, illusionists deny it from another, and transparency theorists must affirm it but have given no accepted argument for it. The argument is conditionally valid but becomes unconditionally valid only if SP-A is granted; and granting SP-A is precisely what every serious opponent refuses to do.

**Secondary hidden-crux candidate: SP-I**, because it operates one level up: it is the premise that gives the conservatism cost its normative force. If SP-I is false, the entire axis on which the thesis stakes its claim is not a genuine epistemic axis but a preference, and the argument collapses before SP-A is ever reached. SP-I is less visible precisely because it is so foundational — it is the condition under which the argument's framing of "cost" is meaningful at all.
