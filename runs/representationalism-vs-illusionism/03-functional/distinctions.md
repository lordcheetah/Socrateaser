# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Functional — distinction watchdog
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Functional pass
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.

DISTINCTION: CONCERNS — 9 issues

## Equivocation

**Term: "qualia" / "phenomenal character"**

The framer correctly pins this as the pseudo-dispute landmine and defines a thin, neutral sense for the thesis. But the council does not hold the pin.

- **Phenomenologist (P3 defense):** Uses "phenomenal character" to mean world-directed presentedness — the felt quality *of* intentional directedness at the tomato. This is a thick-enough reading to ground veridicality claims but thin enough to survive anti-qualia commitments.
- **Illusionist:** Uses "phenomenal character" to mean what Frankish calls a quasi-phenomenal property misrepresented by introspection as having phenomenal status — the term picks out the target of the introspective *error*, which is a representational state, not a felt property. That sense is categorically different from the phenomenologist's.
- **Realist:** Uses "phenomenal character" / "felt quality" in the thick, fully non-relational, intrinsically qualitative sense — the one both transparency theorists and illusionists officially deny. The realist's case for denying SP-A depends on this thick reading being correct, but if it is correct the thesis's neutral framing of the explanandum (P1) has already been replaced.
- **Rationalist:** Uses "felt character" to mean whatever is picked out by the predicate "what it is like" — a deliberately non-committal sense that is supposed to be thinner than the realist's but still thick enough to generate Levine's gap. This is a third sense: not the thin-neutral (framer), not the world-directed (phenomenologist), not the quasi-phenomenal misrepresentation target (illusionist).

**Effect on validity:** The P3 vs. SP-A dispute requires a single, stable referent for "phenomenal character" — what exactly is it that transparency claims to account for? When the phenomenologist says "no residue left after the intentional story," the realist says "the residue is the felt quality," and the illusionist says "the apparent residue is the misrepresentation," they are not disagreeing about one thing. They are each using "phenomenal character" for a different object. Arguments citing P3 as TRUE (phenomenologist) and P3 as FALSE (realist, rationalist) are not engaging the same premise. The comparative verdict turns entirely on which sense of the term is operative. The argument does not collapse — the crux is real — but it cannot be resolved until the disputants stipulate a single sense.

→ **Does not survive disambiguation as stated.** The argument must first adjudicate which sense of "phenomenal character" is the target before the P3 contest can be resolved.

**Term: "saves the appearances" / "saves the phenomena"**

Pinned in the thesis to mean: accounts for the manifest datum without declaring it illusory (P2).

- **Thesis / reconstructor:** Uses "saves the appearances" in a *conservatism* sense — do not posit global introspective error. This is the theory-choice virtue reading.
- **Illusionist:** Challenges the term at a different level — argues that "appearances" are not a theory-neutral datum (echoing Dennett's SP-C), so "saving" them is not automatically a virtue. Uses "saves the appearances" in scare quotes that mean *merely preserving the error*.
- **Realist:** Uses "saves the appearances" in a *phenomenal-realist* sense — to save the appearances is to keep felt qualitative character in your ontology. On this reading transparency does not save the appearances at all, because its reduction eliminates the felt quality even as it claims to identify it.
- **Phenomenologist:** Uses "saves the appearances" in the intentionalist sense — the appearance is world-directedness, and transparency saves it by taking world-directedness at face value. This is closest to the thesis's intended sense but slides between "saves the phenomenal appearances" (which the realist says requires phenomenal properties) and "saves the first-person testimony appearances" (the reconstructor's weaker Variant 2).

**Effect on validity:** P2 defines the term, but P5 and P6 then deploy it in a way that only works if the "appearance" in question is theory-neutral testimony data. The illusionist denies it is, the realist denies it is the right kind of appearance to save. P5 therefore uses the term in the theory-choice-virtue sense while its contesters use it in incompatible senses. The inference from P5 to C trades on the ambiguity.

→ **Argument survives if the thesis-as-pinned sense (conservatism-about-first-person-testimony) is held fixed** — but several council moves collapse precisely because they silently switch to the phenomenal-realist sense or the theory-laden-datum sense. Flag wherever a council member treats "saves the appearances" as equivalent to "keeps phenomenal properties in the ontology" — that is a sense-shift that inflates what P3 must do.

**Term: "veridical" / "broadly veridical" (introspection)**

- **Thesis (P3):** Veridical means *truth-tracking about representational content directed at worldly properties* — introspection correctly delivers the intentional objects of experience.
- **Phenomenologist:** Uses veridical in exactly this sense; world-directedness is what introspection delivers, and that is correct.
- **Realist:** Uses veridical to mean *truth-tracking about the felt qualitative character itself* — and then argues transparency cannot be veridical in this sense because its reductive base (content) lacks the relevant property. This is a different sense: veridical-about-the-character vs. veridical-about-the-content.
- **Rationalist:** Uses veridical in the second sense as well — asks whether introspection veridically delivers the felt aspect, and argues the gap shows it cannot.
- **Illusionist:** Uses veridical in neither sense — treats any claim of veridicality as circular because the datum being tracked is itself the product of a misrepresentation.

**Effect on validity:** P3 claims transparency achieves broad veridicality. Whether P3 is true depends on which sense is operative. In the first sense (veridical-about-content), P3 may be true by stipulation — Tye's PANIC theory just defines phenomenal character as a certain kind of representational content, and then introspection of that content is veridical. In the second sense (veridical-about-the-felt-character), P3 requires SP-A to be true first. The rationalist and realist attack P3 in the second sense; the phenomenologist defends it in the first. They are not in direct disagreement — they are at cross-purposes about the sense of "veridical."

→ **The argument survives only if P3 is read in the first sense consistently.** The council's central P3 debate is partly a product of this equivocation. Flag explicitly.

**Term: "transparent" / "transparency" / "diaphanousness"**

- **Phenomenologist:** Uses transparency as a *first-person description* — what careful attention discloses. Harman's "look at the tree" passage is phenomenological reportage.
- **Thesis:** Uses transparency as a *metaphysical/constitutive claim* — phenomenal character IS representational content (strong identity form). The framer flags this distinction between weak transparency ("hard to find inner qualities") and strong transparency ("introspection is veridical and delivers only worldly properties").
- **Realist (attack round):** Accepts the phenomenological description of diaphanousness ("the only qualitative character on offer is the world-as-presented" — phenomenologist's own words) and argues it does *not* entail the constitution claim (SP-A). The inference from "objects of introspective attention are worldly" to "phenomenal character is exhausted by worldly properties" is, in Block's phrase, a non sequitur.

**Effect on validity:** This is the single sharpest equivocation in the argument. "Transparency" operates as a phenomenological datum in the setup (the diaphanousness observation grounds the framing) and then as a metaphysical identity claim in P3. The phenomenologist partially acknowledges the gap ("SP-A delivered, not assumed — because phenomenologically, that is the only character there ever was") but this is precisely the contested inference. If "transparent" means only the phenomenological description, it does not support the metaphysical identity in P3. If it means the metaphysical identity, it is not a datum but a contested thesis.

→ **The argument risks trading on the phenomenological datum to establish the metaphysical conclusion.** This is genuine equivocation affecting P3's support. The phenomenologist's draft defends P3 by collapsing the two senses — treating the absence of an inner screen as establishing that character IS content — which is exactly the non-sequitur the realist names.

**Term: "illusion" / "systematic error" / "misrepresentation" (illusionism's error posit)**

- **Thesis (P4):** Means global introspective error — introspection attributes felt qualitative character where none exists.
- **Illusionist:** Uses "illusion" in Frankish's technical sense — a higher-order misrepresentation by the introspective system, where quasi-phenomenal properties (real physical properties) are misrepresented as phenomenal (which they are not). This is a precisely specified error with a determinate target (quasi-phenomenal ≠ phenomenal).
- **Contemplative (attack round):** Uses "illusion" to mean the entire structure of apparent/misappeared experience, arguing that the illusionist's "illusion" reifies both the misrepresentation and a stable subject who is taken in — neither of which, on the contemplative's view, exists.
- **Naturalist:** Uses "systematic error" to mean the same kind of debunking that works for folk psychology, vital spirits, and phlogiston — a normal scientific move, not a special philosophical posit.

**Effect on validity:** P4 uses "systematic error" in a way that Frankish's actual position specifies more precisely (quasi-phenomenal properties misrepresented) than the premise captures. The thesis's conservatism-cost charge against P4 depends on the error being unmotivated (SP-B). Whether SP-B succeeds depends on whether the error has an independent motivation — and the illusionist argues the hard problem provides one. None of this creates a validity-breaking equivocation in the P1–C chain as stated, but the contemplative's dissolution of "the dupe who is deceived" is worth flagging as a distinct sense of "illusion" that the argument does not address.

→ **Manageable.** P4 is not internally equivocal; it is contested on its truth, not on its sense.

## Category bleed

**Crossing 1 (the central structural bleed): "explanatory gap" sliding between epistemic and metaphysical**

The reconstructor explicitly flags this as the distinction-watchdog's primary concern, and the council exploits it heavily.

- **Levine's original formulation (epistemic):** The gap "doesn't demonstrate a gap in nature, but a gap in our understanding of nature." Levine grants the materialist may be right and still insists the gap is real — as a fact about our concepts and their failure to achieve intelligible co-extension. This is an epistemic claim: a fact about what we can understand.
- **Rationalist's use:** Opens by citing Levine in the epistemic register — "a gap in intelligibility, knowable a priori by reflection on what the two concepts respectively involve" — and then uses the persistence of the conceivable question ("why does *this* content feel like *that*?") to argue that SP-A is "not a priori secured." This is a legitimate conceptual/epistemic use: the gap shows a constitution claim is not analytically established.
- **Rationalist's slide:** The same draft then concludes that "a premise that is neither a priori true nor empirically settleable cannot license the comparative verdict P6 demands." This is a *normative epistemic* claim about what licenses a verdict — it has moved from "there is a gap in our concepts" (epistemic) to "therefore the constitution claim fails as a premise" (meta-theoretic). These are different claims: a gap in intelligibility does not entail the identity is false, only that it is not analytically secured. Levine says as much ("the materialist response is right in the end").
- **Realist's use:** Deploys the gap as evidence that phenomenal properties are *real and irreducible* — a metaphysical conclusion. "The explanation of functions does not suffice for the explanation of experience" becomes, in the realist's hands, "therefore there are phenomenal facts that content cannot constitute." This is a full crossing from epistemic to metaphysical: the gap (a fact about our concepts) is used to establish that the world has a certain structure.
- **Naturalist's correction:** Correctly identifies the crossing — "The gap is an epistemic fact about us, our concepts and modes of presentation, not a gap in nature" — and uses this to push back on the rationalist's demand that constitution be a priori secured.

**Standard of evidence the epistemic claim was given:** Conceptual analysis, a priori reflection on whether the two concepts include each other.

**Standard the metaphysical conclusion now needs:** Empirical or independent metaphysical argument for why a conceivability gap tracks a real gap in nature (this is the Chalmers-vs-Yablo dispute, not resolved in this run).

→ **Flag:** The realist crossing is the most serious. The rationalist crossing is gentler but still present — the jump from "SP-A is not analytically secured" to "SP-A is false (or insufficient to license P3)" moves from an epistemic fact to a logical verdict without the bridge. Levine himself does not make that jump.

**Crossing 2: Phenomenological diaphanousness deployed as metaphysical constitution**

- **Established as:** Phenomenological — Harman's transparency observation is a first-person description of what careful attention discloses. The phenomenologist presents it explicitly as description before theory.
- **Used as:** Metaphysical — the phenomenologist concludes from the absence of an inner screen that "representational content *constitutes* the felt character because, phenomenologically, that is the only character there ever was." This is a constitutive metaphysical claim (identity, exhaustion of phenomenal character by intentional content).

**The bridge the argument needs but does not supply:** A principle connecting "no inner screen found in careful description" to "there is no felt quality distinct from intentional content." The phenomenologist asserts this connection ("SP-A delivered, not assumed") but does not argue for it. The realist correctly identifies the gap: "It establishes that the *objects* of introspective attention are worldly properties. It does *not* establish that the qualitative character of my experience is *exhausted by* those worldly properties — that inference is a non sequitur."

**Standard given:** Phenomenological (lived description of world-directedness).
**Standard needed:** Metaphysical constitution claim (identity between phenomenal character and representational content, or exhaustion of the former by the latter).

→ **Flag.** This is the single most consequential category bleed in the argument. P3, as the load-bearing premise, is defended by moving from a phenomenological datum (transparency as description) to a metaphysical conclusion (content constitutes character). The crossing is where SP-A is supposed to be established — and it is not.

**Crossing 3: Epistemic conservatism deployed as a metaphysical claim about introspective reliability**

- **P5 established as:** A *meta-theoretic norm* — a principle of theory choice, holding that fewer error posits incur lower theoretical debt on the conservatism dimension.
- **Deployed as:** A *claim about introspective reliability* — that introspection is in fact broadly truth-tracking, which is a substantive empirical/metaphysical claim about a faculty's accuracy.

The thesis's argument slides between these. P5 is a theory-choice virtue (normative/meta-theoretic) and does not establish that introspection is reliable. P3 asserts reliability. But the support for P3 — the phenomenological transparency observation — at most shows that introspection does not deliver an inner screen; it does not establish that what introspection does deliver (world-directedness, representational content) accurately reflects the nature of experience rather than the nature of the faculty's outputs.

The illusionist exploits this: "conservatism about introspection is a vice because introspection has no privileged authority about its own nature" — which is an argument that the meta-theoretic norm (P5) is inapplicable here because the relevant empirical condition (faculty reliability) is not met.

→ **Flag.** P5's meta-theoretic status is used to make it look like a pure theory-choice axiom, but its application to this case requires an empirical premise about introspective reliability that the argument smuggles in rather than establishes.

**Crossing 4: "Better model" sliding between meta-theoretic and all-things-considered metaphysical verdict**

- **Established in the argument (P6, C):** Comparative on the conservatism axis only — "better" is explicitly pinned to one dimension (P6, SP-D as the unstated axis-weighting premise).
- **Used by council members:** The naturalist, illusionist, and realist all treat "better model" as an all-things-considered verdict and attack accordingly (parsimony, hard-problem dissolution, ontological economy). The thesis's own axis restriction is explicitly flagged by the reconstructor but the council largely ignores it.

This is not an equivocation in the thesis — the framer and reconstructor pin the sense carefully. But it is a systematic failure by the council to track the restriction. The naturalist's case, for example, is almost entirely valid *against a different thesis* (the claim that transparency is the all-things-considered better model). Against the actual thesis, the naturalist can at most show P7 is false — that other axes do not tie — which is a legitimate attack but requires conceding that on the stated axis transparency wins.

→ **Flag as council-level category bleed, not thesis-level.** Multiple council members collapse the meta-theoretic comparative claim (better on axis A) into an all-things-considered metaphysical verdict, which makes their verdicts sound more decisive than the argument's scope warrants.

**Crossing 5: The illusionist's "conservatism is a vice" move — normative claim without normative grounding**

- The illusionist argues that "convicting introspection of error" is not a cost but a virtue — "the price of honesty." This is a *normative* claim about theory-choice: conservatism about this particular datum should be assigned negative weight.
- **What the argument needs:** A principle establishing that honesty-about-error outweighs conservatism specifically in the domain of introspective self-report. The illusionist supplies only an analogy (perception errors, color constancy) and the rhetorical reframing that transparency is "residual Cartesianism wearing a naturalist hat."
- **What category the claim is:** Normative/meta-theoretic — a claim about which theory-choice virtues should dominate in this domain.
- **What evidence is given:** Analogical (perceptual illusions) plus the independent motivation of the hard problem. These are empirical/theoretical supports for a normative conclusion, and the bridge is not argued.

→ **Flag.** The illusionist's most rhetorically forceful move — "conservatism is a vice" — is a normative claim deployed without a normative argument. It is supported by analogies that do not establish the relevant normative principle.

**Crossing 6: The contemplative's conventional/ultimate two-truths distinction — metaphysical framework imported without argument**

- **The contemplative's move:** Argues that the thesis runs the "ultimate" register (positing a real datum and a real fact about whether introspection gets it right) while the ultimate level contains no fixed explanandum.
- **Category of the claim:** Metaphysical — the claim that the felt datum and the introspector have no own-being (svabhāva) is a metaphysical thesis about the structure of reality.
- **How it is supported:** By reference to the two-truths doctrine (Madhyamaka) and the Abhidharma analysis of experience as a flowing continuum. These are appeals to a metaphysical framework.
- **What this means for the argument:** The contemplative's ILL-POSED verdict is not a *functional* verdict of the kind the distinction-watchdog adjudicates; it is a first-order metaphysical intervention that denies the argument's explanandum from outside the argument's framework. It is a legitimate philosophical position but it does not bear on whether the terms within the argument equivocate or whether the argument's internal category transitions are sound. The council seat exploits the contemplative's move to dissolve the dispute rather than adjudicate it, which is a meta-level intervention dressed as a functional one.

→ **Flag.** The ILL-POSED verdict imports a full metaphysical framework (two-truths, emptiness of svabhāva) to attack P1's explanandum-stability claim (SP-C). This is a legitimate philosophical objection, but it occupies a different register from the meta-theoretic comparative claim the thesis stakes. The contemplative is not showing the argument is ill-formed on its own terms; it is showing the argument's framework presupposes substantive metaphysical commitments. That is a metaphysical objection, not a logical one, and should not be read as establishing that the thesis is formally ill-posed.

**Near-ally collapse check (the framer's pre-flagged concern)**

The framer warns that transparency and illusionism both deny intrinsic qualia, creating the risk of a near-ally collapse where the council appears to fight while agreeing on the substantive metaphysics.

**Verdict: Partial collapse detected, but the genuine residue survives.**

- **Where they agree:** The phenomenologist (defending transparency) explicitly agrees with the illusionist that there are no thick, intrinsic, non-relational qualia. Both deny the "inner screen." The naturalist ally to illusionism also agrees with the phenomenologist that "introspecting qualia" is comparable to "seeing celestial spheres." At the level of thick-qualia denial, these three seats are indeed allies.
- **Where the collapse is resisted:** The phenomenologist insists on *introspective veridicality about world-directedness* — a claim the illusionist explicitly denies. The illusionist says introspection misrepresents functional states as phenomenal; the phenomenologist says introspection correctly delivers world-directed presentedness. These have opposite truth-conditions, as the caster's brief requires. The genuine residue (veridical vs. erroneous introspection about whatever is there) does survive.
- **Where collapse is real:** The naturalist and illusionist converge not just on the anti-qualia verdict but on the evaluative claim that conservatism is less important than parsimony and fecundity. The naturalist even concedes the illusionist's case adds up to the same verdict while suggesting the illusionist should frame the win differently. This is not an equivocation but it is the council alignment the framer warned about.
- **The phenomenologist/illusionist near-ally risk in the attack round:** The phenomenologist accuses the illusionist of "convicting an introspection nobody actually runs" — that the illusionist's error theory targets a Cartesian reconstruction of the report, not the report itself. The illusionist does not directly respond, but this is the sharpest internal challenge to the near-ally charge: if the illusionist's error posit has no target (because no one actually introspects thick qualia), then the illusionist and transparency theorist are not rivals about introspection's accuracy — they are rivals about whether there was ever an error-susceptible datum in the first place. That is the near-ally collapse in its most precise form, and it is not resolved in the council drafts.

→ **Flag.** The near-ally collapse is real at the level of thick-qualia denial and at the level of theory-choice priorities (naturalist/illusionist convergence). The residue that resists collapse is the veridicality question, but the phenomenologist's attack round raises the possibility that even this residue depends on a contested setup (the illusionist needs a target for the error that careful phenomenology may never deliver). This is not a redundant finding — it is the crux the synthesizer should develop.

## Clean joints

The following terms and transitions were checked and found stable:

- **"explanandum" (P1):** Used consistently throughout to mean the manifest datum that introspective reports attribute felt qualitative character to conscious states. No equivocation detected within the argument's own premises, even though the datum's status is contested (SP-C). The term is stable; the dispute is about whether it picks out anything real, not about what it picks out.

- **"global error" / "global introspective error" (P4):** Used consistently to mean systematic across-the-board misattribution. The illusionist's more precise "quasi-phenomenal misrepresentation" is a specification within the same sense, not a different sense. No equivocation.

- **"conservatism" as a theory-choice virtue (P5):** Used in the standard Quinean sense throughout — prefer the theory that departs less from existing accepted claims. The naturalist challenges its weight, not its meaning. No equivocation on this term; the dispute is normative, not semantic.

- **"parsimony" / "ontological economy":** Used consistently across all seats in the standard ontological sense — prefer the theory that posits fewer kinds of entity. The dispute about which theory is more parsimonious is substantive (is "veridically introspected representational content with felt character" one posit or two?), but the term itself does not shift sense.

- **"P3 is load-bearing":** The reconstructor's diagnosis is confirmed from multiple directions. Every council member treats P3 as the premise to attack or defend. The convergence is genuine, not apparent.

- **"SP-A" (the anti-relocation premise):** Used consistently as the claim that representational content can constitute phenomenal character (not merely co-vary with or re-describe it). This is the reconstructor's formulation and all council members use it in this sense when they name it. No equivocation.

- **"the hard problem" (as a phenomenon):** Used consistently to mean the explanatory gap between functional/physical description and phenomenal character. No equivocation, though its status (epistemic vs. metaphysical) is itself contested — flagged above under Crossing 1 as a category bleed, not an equivocation on the term.

- **"meta-theoretic" / "theory-choice":** Used consistently to mark the second-order, comparative-virtue level of the dispute. The naturalist, reconstructor, and framer all distinguish this level from the first-order metaphysical level. The council's category bleeds (Crossings 1, 2) cross this line but the distinction itself is not equivocated upon.

- **"SP-D" (axis-weighting):** Correctly identified by naturalist, illusionist, and reconstructor as the suppressed premise that moves from "wins on conservatism" to "is the better model." All uses of this term are consistent with the reconstructor's formulation. The dispute about whether SP-D is true is substantive, but the term is stable.

## Refinement 1

DISTINCTION: CONCERNS — 4 new issues; substantive-vs-terminological adjudication below

### New author distinctions: stability audit

**1. END-RESULT vs. CONSTRUCTION of introspection**

The author draws this distinction, the computational-functionalist specialist develops it via Graziano's Attention Schema Theory, and the illusionist's refinement section explicitly accepts it as Graziano's own framing. The realist's refinement also accepts the distinction ("I grant this freely") while arguing it does not touch the phenomenal-realist claim.

Stability verdict: **Stable and doing real work.** The distinction tracks a genuine difference between (a) what the introspective system reports as its output — "I am aware of a red apple" — and (b) the neural/computational story of how that report is generated. The distinction was already implicit in Dennett's heterophenomenology but is here sharpened into an asymmetric reliability claim: reliable about output, unreliable about construction. The term "end-result" is used consistently by the computational-functionalist and implicitly by the illusionist. No equivocation detected.

However, a category bleed requires flagging. The end-result/construction distinction is offered as an **empirical** claim about differential reliability (which parts of introspection track accurately). But it is deployed in the refinement dispute as a **conceptual** resolution of the veridical-vs.-error contest. That move is too fast: showing that introspection is reliable *about output* and unreliable *about construction* does not, by itself, settle whether the output-level report ("I am aware, and the content is red") is phenomenal-realist (there is something it is like) or illusionist (the output is a functional state misrepresented as phenomenal). The distinction filters where error lives, but it does not fix which camp owns the output-level fact. The computational-functionalist elides this by treating the output as "thin" by stipulation; the realist accepts the construction/output split and then re-inserts the phenomenal given at the output level. Both are consistent uses of the same distinction — they just disagree on what the output level contains, which is exactly the original dispute one level down.

**2. THIN vs. THICK qualia**

The author explicitly adopts thin qualia (qualia as representations, nothing intrinsic over and above them). The framer pre-pinned this distinction at stage 0. The computational-functionalist uses it as the central distinction throughout. The illusionist's refinement section confirms that Frankish's illusionism targets precisely thick qualia (intrinsic, ineffable, classical), and that thin qualia — functional representations that do real work — are affirmed by illusionism, not denied.

Stability verdict: **Stable and doing real work, but requires one precision fix.** The thin/thick split is the clearest structural clarification in the refinement round. Every camp can state its position in these terms: realists affirm thick qualia, everyone else denies them. The precision fix: "thin qualia" as used by the author and the computational-functionalist means *the representation itself, nothing more* — which is what the illusionist also means by "what is real" after the thick property is subtracted. But the transparency-representationalist (Tye, Dretske, Harman as characterized in P3) uses "phenomenal character" in a sense that sits *between* thin and thick: they deny intrinsic non-relational qualia (so not thick) but still claim introspective veridicality about the felt character as such (so not merely a functional representation in the Graziano-register). This middle position is what P3 is committed to, and the thin/thick dichotomy, as the author uses it, does not have a slot for it. If "thin" just means "the representation," then P3 as stated is false — because P3 claims introspection is *veridically tracking felt qualitative character*, which is a stronger claim than "introspection reads off the representation." The author's thin qualia resolve neatly into illusionism, not into transparency's P3.

**3. "Veridical" recast as USEFUL CORRELATION (not 1:1 correspondence)**

The computational-functionalist introduces this recast explicitly: a representation need not map isomorphically onto a worldly property to be veridical; it need only "correlate usefully for the self." The framer had already flagged the multiple senses of "veridical" as an equivocation risk. This recast is an attempt to deflate the veridicality claim in P3 enough to survive illusionist pressure.

Stability verdict: **Introduces a new equivocation rather than resolving the old one.** "Useful correlation" is an epistemic/functional notion — accuracy as pragmatic fit for the system. "Veridical" in the thesis's P3 means truth-tracking about representational content (the sense flagged as Equivocation 3 in the original report). These are different standards:

- *Truth-tracking veridical*: introspection correctly identifies the intentional object of the experience (what the experience is *of*).
- *Useful-correlation veridical*: the representation reliably guides adaptive behavior for this body in this environment.

A state can be useful-correlation-veridical while being truth-tracking-false (inverted qualia are a canonical case: the representation reliably guides behavior while the felt character may be inverted). The recast therefore does not save P3 in its thesis-standard sense; it substitutes a weaker, different standard and then treats P3 as vindicated. That is a category bleed: a normative/functional standard (useful fit) is deployed where an epistemic standard (truth-tracking about the nature of the state) was required. The recast should be flagged, not endorsed, as a clarification.

**4. "FELT BEING REAL" vs. "SIMULATING FELT BEING REAL"**

The author reports no perceptible difference between these. The illusionist's refinement section accepts this: "There is no difference — and saying so is precisely my position." The illusionist's argument is that denying the felt-real/simulating-felt-real distinction just *is* the denial of thick-qualia Fact B, and that denial is the load-bearing premise of illusionism.

Stability verdict: **The distinction is stable in meaning but the author's use of it is self-undermining for the thesis.** Here is the precise verdict: the author's claim that felt-being-real and simulating-felt-being-real are indistinguishable is not a conceptual confusion — it is a substantive metaphysical commitment. Specifically, it is the commitment that there is no "Fact B" (an actual phenomenal property over and above the representing). That commitment is exactly what separates the illusionist from the phenomenal realist. The author, by endorsing the indistinguishability, has taken the illusionist's side on the one wall in the debate. This is not a terminological matter. The phenomenal realist's entire case (Goff, Block, Chalmers) is that the distinction *is* real — that there is a difference between representing-as-phenomenal and being-phenomenal, and that the felt quality is on the being-side, not merely the representing-side. By saying there is no such difference, the author affirms illusionism's central metaphysical claim.

**5. The rationalist's charge: "QUALIA IS THE REPRESENTATION" equivocates NECESSITY for CONSTITUTION**

The rationalist's refinement section addresses this directly under point (b). The argument: "Qualia can't be none" establishes a *necessity* claim — a mind with no felt character would not be a functioning mind. But necessity of co-presence does not entail constitution. The rationalist's TV analogy makes this precise: the signal is necessary for the display, and they are perfectly correlated, but the signal does not *constitute* the displayed light — they are different things standing in a reliable causal/functional relation. Identification of the display with the signal would be false.

Stability verdict: **The rationalist's charge is well-formed and the equivocation is real.** The move from "qualia can't be none" (necessity) to "qualia IS the representation" (constitution/identity) is invalid as stated. Necessity of co-presence is one kind of claim; identity or constitution is another. The following are all compatible with necessity and fail to establish constitution:

- *Supervenience*: wherever there is representation, there is felt character, but felt character is a further fact.
- *Correlation*: the two always go together without either constituting the other.
- *Grounding*: representation grounds felt character without being identical to it.

The author's phrasing "qualia IS the representation" is therefore an identity claim that the necessity argument does not support. However — and this is the crucial watchdog precision — the rationalist's charge that this equivocates *necessity for constitution* is correct only if we grant that necessity and constitution are distinct. The illusionist's refinement section argues that once thick Fact B is denied, there is nothing for "constitution" to mean over and above "is reliably present with" — and then necessity and constitution collapse. Whether that collapse is coherent is itself the dispute. So: the rationalist's charge is a genuine equivocation flag *conditional on thick qualia being in play*; if the author is already operating with thin qualia only, the equivocation dissolves because "constitution" means something weaker.

### Is the gap between the author's view and strong illusionism substantive or terminological?

**Adjudication: Predominantly terminological, with one narrow substantive residue.**

The illusionist's refinement section argues that the author has independently reconstructed the Graziano/Frankish position and then declined the word "illusion." This watchdog can confirm the mapping is accurate across the four commitments the illusionist tabulates: thin qualia, output-reliable/construction-unreliable introspection, Graziano-register machinery, and "nothing left over beyond the representation." All four are illusionist commitments and all four are author commitments.

The word "illusion" is the surface site of disagreement. Graziano himself objects to the word while accepting the substance, and Frankish retains Graziano on the illusionist roster. This is a confirmed intramural terminological dispute within the illusionist camp.

The one candidate for a substantive residue is the author's claim that "the output — I am aware of the red apple — is veridical." The illusionist says introspection's output-level report is a misrepresentation: it represents the state as having phenomenal character it does not have. The author says the output report is correct because there *is* awareness and the content *is* thus-and-so. The question is whether "phenomenal character" and "awareness + content" are the same target.

Here is the precise verdict. If "phenomenal character" means thick qualia (Fact B, intrinsic felt quality over and above the representing), then:
- The author denies thick qualia.
- The illusionist denies thick qualia.
- Both agree the introspective attribution of thick qualia is an error.
- The residue is terminological.

If "phenomenal character" means the thin output-level fact (there is awareness, and it has such-and-such content), then:
- The author says introspection is veridical about this.
- The illusionist says this too: the functional state is real, the report that it is running is correct.
- Again, both agree.
- Again, the residue is terminological.

The only place where the gap could be substantive is if the author means to claim something *between* thin and thick — something that deserves the label "phenomenal" but is not the classical thick quale. The Graziano schema is exactly this: the attention schema represents awareness as having a property (non-physical, inner, immediate) that it does not have — and *that misrepresentation* is what gets called an illusion by Frankish, while Graziano resists the word because the schema and what it models are both real. If the author is claiming that the schema's output (representing-as-having-awareness) is veridical about *the schema's content* rather than veridical about a phenomenal property, then the author and the illusionist agree again — this just is Graziano's position, and it is illusionism on Frankish's taxonomy.

**Conclusion:** The gap is terminological with respect to the metaphysics, and substantive only with respect to the rhetorical frame: "illusion" connotes global deception, which the author (and Graziano) resist because the functional states and the awareness-output are real. Frankish's usage is technical and narrower than the connotation — the misrepresentation is specifically the attribution of the thick phenomenal property, not the denial of functional consciousness. Once the author understands "illusion" in Frankish's technical sense (misrepresentation of thick property only), the disagreement collapses. The thesis, as originally formulated — that transparency is *better* than illusionism — cannot be supported by the author's own stated commitments, because those commitments are illusionist commitments that deny P3.

### Plain-language glossary of the 5-6 load-bearing terms (per-camp)

Below is the glossary the author asked for. Each entry gives the term and then states, in plain language, what each camp means by it.

---

**QUALIA** (also called "phenomenal character," "felt quality," "what it is like")

- *Transparency representationalism (the thesis's view):* The feel of an experience — the redness you experience when you see a tomato. The thesis claims this feel is just what it's like to represent the tomato's redness; no extra ingredient is needed. You see redness *out there*, and that seeing is the feel. Nothing is left over inside.
- *Illusionism (Frankish/Graziano):* The functional brain state that represents "I am aware of red" is real. But the *special inner glow* — the intrinsic, private, ineffable feel — that introspection says accompanies it does not exist. What feels like an inner glow is just a simplified, schematic report the brain makes about its own activity. The brain claims to have magic inner feels; it does not; the claiming is the "illusion." What does exist: the representation, the awareness, the content. What does not exist: any extra property *on top of* the representation.
- *Phenomenal realism (Chalmers/Goff):* The felt quality is a real feature of the world — not reducible to how the brain processes information, not identical to any representational content. When you see red, there is a definite, irreducible what-it's-likeness to that seeing. This cannot be explained away as "just representation." It is the one thing we know most directly, from the inside.
- *Rationalist (Levine/Kripke):* Whatever "what it is like" picks out, it cannot be fully explained by any account of how the brain processes information — including representational accounts. There is a gap between the processing story and the felt story that no identity claim closes.

---

**INTROSPECTION** (as in: is it reliable?)

- *Transparency representationalism:* When you attend carefully to your experience, you find the world — the redness out there, not an inner mental patch. Introspection is broadly reliable: it correctly tells you that your experience is about the world, and about what properties of the world it is about.
- *Illusionism:* Introspection is reliable about *what* you are aware of (the red apple, not the blue cube) and about *that* you are aware. It is unreliable about *how* awareness is built — it tells you awareness is a mysterious inner glow; it is actually a representational process. The self-model is like a dashboard gauge: accurate about the reading, inaccurate about the engine.
- *Phenomenal realism:* Introspection is the one faculty that gives direct access to phenomenal properties. You cannot be wrong that there is *something it is like* to be in your current state, even if you can be wrong about many details of its construction.
- *Rationalist:* Introspection may be reliable in the output sense, but it cannot tell us whether felt character is identical to or merely correlated with representational content — that question outruns introspection's authority.

---

**VERIDICAL** (as in: is introspection veridical?)

- *Transparency representationalism (P3):* Introspection is veridical in that it correctly delivers the intentional object — what the experience is about (the redness of the tomato, the location of the pain). Felt character just is that intentional directedness, correctly tracked.
- *Illusionism:* The output report ("I am aware of red") is veridical — the system really is in that state. The construction report ("this awareness has an ineffable inner glow") is not veridical. So: partially veridical, partially not, but the partially-not is systematic and specific.
- *Computational-functionalist gloss (Graziano):* Veridical means "useful correlation for this body in this environment," not "mirrors the world 1:1." A representation that reliably guides action is veridical enough; it need not reproduce the intrinsic nature of its target.
- *Phenomenal realism:* Introspection is veridical about the existence and felt character of experience — you cannot be wrong that you are having an experience with *some* felt quality, even if you can be wrong about many details.
- *Rationalist:* Veridical requires that what introspection delivers (felt character) is genuinely what the brain state is — i.e., that the identity claim (felt character = representational content) is true. But that identity is not established, so veridicality of the thick sort cannot be assumed.

---

**ILLUSION** (as in: illusionism says introspection is an illusion)

- *Frankish (illusionism):* A highly specific technical term. "Illusion" means: introspection misrepresents your brain states as having a particular property (thick phenomenal quality — private, ineffable, intrinsically qualitative) that they do not have. The brain states themselves are real. The awareness is real. The content is real. The *extra property* (the one that would make the hard problem hard) is the thing falsely attributed. Frankish does not mean "experience doesn't exist" — he means "experience lacks the magical ingredient introspection says it has."
- *Graziano (AST):* Dislikes the word "illusion" for exactly this reason: it sounds like experience doesn't exist, which is false. The model and what it models are both real. What is false is the model's claim that awareness is non-physical and intrinsically qualitative. Graziano's preferred frame: the self-model is a simplified representation that is accurate about awareness-as-output and inaccurate about awareness-as-construction.
- *Author:* Resists "illusion" for Graziano's reason. Concurs with the substance: there is nothing left over beyond the representation, so the thick phenomenal property is not real. Disagrees only that calling this an "illusion" is the right frame.
- *Phenomenal realism:* "Illusion" is what the illusionist calls the most fundamental datum we have — the felt quality itself. This makes the error theory self-undermining: to *have an illusion of phenomenality* is already to have a phenomenal experience (of the illusion). The illusion must itself be phenomenal, so denying phenomenality with an illusion argument bites its own tail.

---

**THE EXPLANATORY GAP** (also called "the hard problem")

- *Transparency representationalism:* Not a real gap in nature — a gap in our description, which closes when we correctly identify felt character with representational content. Once you see that the feel of red just is representing redness, there is nothing left to explain.
- *Illusionism:* Not a gap in nature — a gap manufactured by introspection's false self-report. The hard problem is hard because introspection tells us there is a non-physical inner feel that needs to be fitted into the physical world. Strip the false report and the hard problem becomes the tractable "illusion problem": explain why the brain represents itself as having a non-physical property.
- *Phenomenal realism:* A real gap in nature. Even after every fact about how the brain represents and processes information is filled in, the question "why is there something it is like to be in that state?" remains unanswered — because that question is about a *different kind of fact* (qualitative, felt) than the facts neuroscience traffics in (structural, functional, relational).
- *Rationalist:* A real gap in our conceptual scheme, established by the fact that the question "why does this processing feel like anything?" remains intelligible no matter how much processing detail you add. Whether it is also a gap in nature is a further question; the conceptual gap alone is enough to defeat any identification claim that fails to close it.
- *Contemplative:* A manufactured gap — but not because one theory has the right answer. Both "the feel is real" and "the feel is an illusion" presuppose a fixed felt item and a stable introspector. Careful first-person attention finds neither standing still. The gap has no edges to be real or unreal.

### New clean joints (Refinement 1)

- **"END-RESULT" / "CONSTRUCTION" (Graziano-register):** Used consistently across the computational-functionalist, illusionist refinement, and realist refinement. All three agree on the distinction's meaning: end-result = the introspective output report; construction = the neural/computational process generating it. The dispute is about what the end-result report commits us to, not about the distinction's meaning.

- **"THIN qualia" vs. "THICK qualia":** Stabilized in the refinement round. All camps accept this dichotomy. The substantive dispute is entirely about whether thin qualia suffice for the phenomenal datum, not about what the dichotomy means.

- **"USEFUL CORRELATION" (computational-functionalist recast of veridical):** Newly introduced term; consistent in the computational-functionalist's use, but not adopted by other seats. Flagged above as introducing a new equivocation (functional/normative standard substituted for epistemic standard), not resolving the old one.
