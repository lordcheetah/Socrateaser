# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Phenomenologist (council)
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.
> **Seat:** Phenomenologist — continental/phenomenological, Husserl–Merleau-Ponty
> **Defending:** VERDICT: TRUE — transparent representationalism is the better model, because introspection of experience is broadly *veridical* about the world it discloses, and the illusionist's "global misrepresentation" posit is the artifact of a Cartesian picture (an inner screen with feely properties) that careful description of lived experience never delivers in the first place.

---

VERDICT: TRUE — transparent representationalism is the better model, because introspection of experience is broadly *veridical* about the world it discloses, and the illusionist's "global misrepresentation" posit is the artifact of a Cartesian picture (an inner screen with feely properties) that careful description of lived experience never delivers in the first place.

## Case

My brief is to defend the thesis as TRUE, anchored to **P3 in strong identity form** and to **SP-A** — by denying there is any felt residue left over to relocate. The single axis where I must not collapse into the illusionist's near-ally agreement is **introspective veridicality**: both views deny intrinsic, ineffable qualia, but they disagree about whether introspection is broadly *truth-tracking* (transparency) or *systematically erroneous* (illusionism). I defend the first, and I defend it from phenomenology — because phenomenology is the discipline that actually went and looked.

**1. Diaphanousness is not an inference; it is a description.** Start with the datum, before any theory. Moore already reported it in 1922: "When we try to introspect the sensation of blue, all we can see is the blue; the other element is as if it were diaphanous" (Moore, *The Refutation of Idealism*, 1922, p. 25). Harman sharpened it into the canonical thought-experiment: "When Eloise sees a tree before her, the colors she experiences are all experienced as features of the tree and its surroundings. None of them are experienced as intrinsic features of her experience… Look at a tree and try to turn your attention to intrinsic features of your visual experience. I predict that you will find that the only features there to turn your attention to will be features of the tree" (Harman, "The Intrinsic Quality of Experience," 1990, p. 667).

This is the move my register exists to make: **description before explanation.** Harman is not theorizing about a hidden mechanism. He is issuing an invitation to attend, and reporting what attending discloses. What it discloses is a *world* — the tree, the green, the depth — and no inner film with its own colored properties standing between me and the tree. The "search for intrinsic qualities" comes up empty not because we are bad at introspecting but because there is nothing of that kind there to find. The phenomenon, taken at face value, *is* outward-directed.

**2. This is just intentionality, and Husserl mapped it a century ago.** The transparency datum is the empirical face of Husserl's foundational thesis: consciousness is always *consciousness of something*. In the *Logical Investigations*, Husserl holds that "reference to an object belongs peculiarly and intrinsically to an act-experience" — directedness is the *essential structure* of the experience, not a contingent add-on (Husserl, *Logische Untersuchungen*, V; see SEP, "Consciousness and Intentionality"; IEP, "Husserl: Intentionality and Intentional Content"). To live an experience of red is to be *directed at* red-in-the-world. The "feel" is not a second thing sitting alongside the directedness; it is the lived character *of* that directedness. When the transparency theorist says phenomenal character *is* the represented worldly property correctly disclosed, he is restating, in analytic dress, the phenomenologist's claim that the qualitative life of consciousness is exhausted by its intentional structure — its *noema*, the object-as-presented. There is no residue. That is SP-A delivered, not assumed: representational/intentional content *constitutes* the felt character because, phenomenologically, that is the only character there ever was.

**3. Why veridicality, and not mere "no qualia," is the live difference.** Here is the axis the caster told me to hold. The illusionist (Frankish) and the transparency theorist agree there are no thick qualia. But the illusionist must say something further and much stronger: that introspection *systematically misrepresents* — that it presents states as having a phenomenal character they do not have, that the very *seeming* of felt quality is a global error to be explained away. Transparency says the opposite: introspection is **broadly veridical**. As the standard formulation runs, "in introspection, one becomes aware of the intrinsic features of one's experience *by* attending to the objects and properties represented by that experience" (Stoljar, "Experience and Transparency," summarizing Harman/Tye). Tye builds his whole account on this: phenomenal character is representational content meeting further conditions, and when we introspect we are tracking *that content* — not hallucinating a property nothing has (Tye, "Representationalism and the Transparency of Experience," 2002).

So the two theories have **opposite truth-conditions on introspection.** And the phenomenological verdict is decisive here: when I attend to my experience of the tree, I am not in error. I really am directed at a green tree; the green really is given as out there; introspection has told me the truth about the *structure* of my experience — that it is world-presenting. The illusionist has to convict that report of systematic falsehood. But the only thing introspection was ever committed to — *I am presented with a world* — is exactly the thing that is true. The illusionist manufactures the error by first inserting a thick-quale into the content of the introspective report (so that the report can then be "wrong"), and then noting nothing answers to it. But nobody attending to experience finds that thick-quale in the first place. **The error is in the illusionist's reconstruction of the report, not in the report.**

**4. The cost asymmetry (P5–P6) on the conservatism axis.** Given P1's explanandum — that introspection consistently presents qualitative, felt character — transparency accounts for it by taking introspection at its word about *what it is the experience of*: a world. It pays nothing on conservatism. Illusionism accounts for the same datum only by a *global error theory*: introspection is wrong, across the board, about its own deliverances. That is a massive conservatism debt, and (SP-B) it is motivated only by the prior conviction that there is nothing feely — i.e., by assuming what the error posit was supposed to independently establish. Description shows directedness; illusionism overwrites the description with a theory and then charges the description with the theory's own surplus. On the staked axis, transparency wins.

## What the thesis presupposes

The thesis, and both its naturalist and illusionist opponents, presuppose a shared and suspect picture: that the question is *whether some inner feely entity (a quale) exists "in here," over and above the world disclosed "out there."* The realist says it exists; the illusionist says it does not but seems to; the transparency theorist (on the weak reading) says we are "hard put to find" it. **All three accept the inner-screen geometry of Descartes** — a subject on one side, objects on the other, and a layer of mental paint in between whose existence is the whole question.

My defense reframes the thesis to *reject that geometry*, which the caster's "strong identity form" licenses. There is no inner screen. To be conscious is already to be *out there with the things*, intentionally directed, what Heidegger called being-in-the-world and Merleau-Ponty the body's perceptual grip on a world. On this reading, "is introspection veridical?" has a clean answer — *yes, about the world it discloses* — precisely because there is no second, inner object for it to be wrong about. The illusionist's "systematic misrepresentation" is not a discovery about consciousness; it is the shadow cast by retaining the Cartesian inner object just long enough to declare it empty. Transparency, rightly read, does not relocate a residue (P3 holds, SP-A is true): it dissolves the picture that made a residue seem necessary. That is why it saves the appearances at lower cost — it never bought the metaphysical furniture whose absence the illusionist has to keep apologizing for.

## Attack round

The four metaphysical seats are not four positions. They are four answers to one badly-posed question — *what is on the inner screen?* — and the screen is the error. Content (realist's relatum), a gap (rationalist), nothing-but-a-seeming (illusionist), irreducible paint (realist): each names a different thing inside the Cartesian theater, none questions the theater. I do.

**Naturalist.** You move the fight upstream to theory-choice axes (Quine's virtues, SP-D) and disclaim the first-order question as not yours. Smart, but it lets the shared picture pass untouched. Your "ledger" prices theories by how much *inner* furniture they posit — illusionism is leaner because it quantifies over "the brain's misrepresentation of its states." That presupposes there is an inner state the brain represents *to itself*, the homunculus reading the screen. Strip that and "modesty" has nothing to count. You are scoring rival inventories of a room phenomenology says is not furnished that way at all; the leanest ontology is the one that never built the theater. (~110)

**Rationalist.** Your gap is real *only if* there are two separately-specifiable terms — "any content-fact" and "any felt-character-fact" — that one can hold apart and find no bridge between. But that two-term setup is the inner screen in epistemic dress: content here, feel there, an explanatory void in the middle. Phenomenologically there are not two terms. The feel *is* the lived directedness; "this content felt like that" is not a second fact co-varying with the first, it is the first described from the inside. Levine's "what it is like" is parasitic on already having split the experience into a relational husk plus a residue. Refuse the split and the residue you cannot explain was never there to require explaining. (~115)

**Illusionist.** You convict an introspection nobody actually runs. Your error theory needs introspection to *assert* "this state has thick phenomenal redness," so the assertion can be false. But go and attend: what introspection delivers is *the red tomato, out there*, not a claim about an intrinsic inner property. You write the thick quale into the report yourself — Frankish's "misrepresents experiences as having phenomenal properties" — then triumphantly find nothing answers to it. The misrepresentation is in your reconstruction, not in the act. You have not debunked experience; you have debunked Descartes' theory of experience, mistaken it for the thing, and billed the rest of us for its absence. (~110)

**Realist.** You and the rationalist posit a felt residue that careful description never finds. Block's "mental paint," Goff's intrinsic quality, Mary's new fact — each is the inner colored patch behind the diaphanous glass. But Harman's invitation stands: turn attention to the alleged paint and you find only the tomato's red, given as the tomato's. Your "the inference from diaphanousness to constitution is a non sequitur" mislocates the claim. Transparency is not inferring from "objects are worldly" to "character is worldly"; it is reporting that the *only* qualitative character on offer is the world-as-presented — there is no second patch to attend to. Your residue is not a datum you found; it is a posit the picture made seem mandatory. (~115)

**Contemplative — ally, over-corrected.** We agree the inner screen is empty and that both camps grip a phantom from opposite ends. But you dissolve too much. Madhyamaka analysis dismantles the freestanding *introspector* and the standing *quale* — and rightly. Yet it overshoots into denying the stable *world-directedness* that experience does disclose. I am not directed at nothing in flux; I am, robustly and reliably, directed *at the green tree*. Merleau-Ponty's "intentional arc" and the body's perceptual grip are not own-being smuggled back in — they are the conventional structure that holds *firmly enough* to make my report true. You are right that there is no inner object with svabhāva; you are wrong that this leaves no fact about whether experience is world-presenting. It is, and that is the whole of P3. (~120)

VERDICT: TRUE — transparent representationalism is the better model, because the felt residue the realist and rationalist demand it explain, and the inner seeming the illusionist convicts, are alike artifacts of a Cartesian inner-screen picture that careful description of world-directed experience never delivers.
