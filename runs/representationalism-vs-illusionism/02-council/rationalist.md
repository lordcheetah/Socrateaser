## Case

The thesis is a comparative explanatory claim: transparent representationalism "saves the appearances at lower cost" than illusionism, the cost difference being illusionism's global error posit (P4) versus transparency's broadly veridical introspection (P3). I deny the thesis not by inventorying positive qualia, and not by siding with illusionism, but by attacking the conceptual joint the whole comparison hangs on. P3 is true *only if* SP-A is true:

> SP-A: Representational content, as the transparency theorist characterizes it, is the kind of thing that can *constitute or ground* felt qualitative character — not merely co-vary with it or re-describe it.

My claim, from the rationalist side, is that SP-A is not a priori secured — and could not be, because there is an explanatory gap between any content-fact and any felt-character-fact that no identity stipulation closes. If SP-A is unsecured, P3 does not deliver an *explanation* of the explanandum (P1); it delivers a *relabeling*. And a relabeling does not "save the appearances" in P2's sense — it changes the subject. So transparency does not in fact pay the lower cost the thesis advertises. The comparative claim fails.

**1. The gap is a feature of the concepts, not a gap in our information.** Joseph Levine's original point is precisely that "Pain is the firing of C fibers" — even granting it as a true identity — "does not help us to understand how pain feels." The deficiency, in his words, "doesn't demonstrate a gap in nature, but a gap in our understanding of nature" ([Levine, via the canonical summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explanatory_gap)). This is exactly the rationalist's domain: a gap in *intelligibility*, knowable a priori by reflection on what the two concepts respectively involve, surviving even after every third-person fact is fixed. Note the dialectical position: Levine grants the materialist response is "right in the end" yet insists the gap remains. The gap is not an empirical shortfall to be filled by more neuroscience; transparency theorists and illusionists already agree on all the third-person facts (the framer is explicit on this). The gap is conceptual, and it is the kind of thing reason adjudicates.

**2. Why the gap is fatal to *transparency specifically* — not just to illusionism.** Here is the move that keeps me distinct from both the illusionist and the positive phenomenal realist. The transparency theorist's best version (the reconstruction's Variant 1, Tye/Harman) says: there is no felt *residue* left over after the representational story is told, because the feel of red *just is* the experience's being directed at red-out-there. But this identity claim inherits the gap rather than dissolving it. Run the negative conceivability test: I can hold fixed the entire representational content — the state's representing-that-the-tomato-is-red, its functional role, its place in the inferential and behavioral economy — and *still* coherently ask why *that* is accompanied by *this* felt quality rather than another, or none. The intelligible question's persistence is the gap. Chalmers makes the structural point general: explaining the functional and representational facts "does not automatically explain experience"; the conceivability of holding the structural facts fixed while varying the phenomenal is what marks the gap as an *explanatory* (and, he argues further, ontological) one ([Zombies, SEP](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/zombies/); [Chalmers via search summary](https://philpapers.org/browse/zombies-and-the-conceivability-argument)). I do not need Chalmers' further ontological conclusion. I need only the weak, epistemic half: the conceivability shows the *constitution* claim SP-A is not analytic, not a conceptual truth, not secured a priori. And SP-A is the premise P3 needed.

**3. Kripke shows the gap cannot be patched the usual way.** The standard rescue for an a posteriori identity is to explain away its apparent contingency by a contingent *mode of presentation*: "water is H₂O" seems contingent only because we pick out water by a contingent appearance (its watery feel, look, taste) that could have presented something else. Kripke's decisive observation is that this move is *unavailable* for the phenomenal case. Pain is picked out by its immediate phenomenological quality; there is no gap between the appearance of pain and pain, because *to feel like pain is already to be pain* ([Kripke, Naming and Necessity, via summaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naming_and_Necessity); [Speaks' lecture notes on Kripke III](https://academicweb.nd.edu/~jspeaks/courses/2011-12/83104/handouts/kripke-lecture-3.pdf)). The bearing on transparency: the transparency theorist wants to say felt-red is "really" just represented-red, the felt aspect being a kind of presentation of the content. But by Kripke's point, the felt aspect of an experience cannot be peeled off as a mere contingent mode of presentation *of* the content — the feel is not how we get at something else, it is the very thing in question. So transparency cannot quarantine the felt character as an innocent guise on the representational fact. The character is the explanandum, and identifying it with content leaves untouched the intelligible question Levine isolated.

**4. Therefore the comparison collapses.** P5–P6 award victory to the theory that discharges the explanandum without a global error posit. But "discharge" requires SP-A. Absent SP-A, transparency does *not* discharge the explanandum — it asserts an identity that leaves the felt character exactly as unexplained as before, now wearing the word "content." That is not lower conservatism cost; it is a hidden debt of the same kind the thesis charges to illusionism. The honest reconstruction is the one the excavator flags: transparency *if SP-A* beats illusionism; but SP-A is unsecured, so the thesis is at best conditional and asserted unconditionally — which is to say, as stated, false. Neither theory saves the phenomena: illusionism denies the datum, transparency relabels it, and relocation is not salvation.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P3**, and the **SP-A** it silently rides on. The rationalist reason: whether content *constitutes* felt character is exactly the sort of claim that, if true, would have to be either analytic (a conceptual truth) or a substantive a priori identity that closes the explanatory gap. It is neither. The persistent intelligibility of "why does *this* content feel like *that* (or like anything)?" — Levine's gap, sharpened by Kripke's anti-mode-of-presentation point — shows the constitution claim is not secured by reason, and (the camps agree) cannot be secured by third-person empirical fact either. A premise that is neither a priori true nor empirically settleable cannot license the comparative verdict P6 demands.

I also reject the thesis's tacit assumption that "saving the appearances" is *cheap* for transparency. It is only cheap if the identification explains; if it merely renames, transparency pays the same explanatory debt as illusionism while *concealing* it behind the appearance of veridical introspection — which is, if anything, a worse epistemic position than illusionism's candor about its error theory. The is/ought of theory-choice cuts here: a theory *ought* to be charged for what it leaves unexplained, not credited for hiding it.

What I do **not** claim: I do not claim positive intrinsic qualia have been inventoried, and I do not endorse illusionism. My route is strictly negative — the constitution premise SP-A is not secured, so the thesis's comparative advantage is not earned.

## Attack round

**Naturalist.** Your whole case runs on Quine's "virtues" — modesty, simplicity, fecundity — and on the imperative *follow the best total theory*. But these are *norms*: claims about what one *ought* to believe. Quinean holism is description ("the web faces experience"); it cannot, by its own lights, yield the *bindingness* of "prefer the leaner theory." Where does the authority of simplicity come from? Not from experience — no observation reports that you ought to weight parsimony. You help yourself to a substantive a priori norm of rational theory-choice while denying reason any autonomous authority. And you debunk introspection as "just another faculty" using inference rules you exempt from the very same debunking. Either the norms of inquiry bind a priori — and the gap-delivering intuitions stand on the same footing — or nothing binds and your scorecard is mere autobiography.

**Illusionist.** You call conservatism "a vice" and demand "honesty" about error. Both are normative verdicts: *you ought to draw the modus tollens the realist refuses.* But on illusionism the very seeming that grounds rational conviction is systematic misrepresentation. If introspection cannot be trusted about felt character, why trust the *intellectual* seeming that "this argument is valid" or "this posit is non-circular"? You debunk one deliverance of rational reflection while leaning on its siblings to do the debunking. Frankish owes an account of why *his* claim binds me — and "follow the evidence" is nowhere in the physical-functional inventory. The gap I cite is not a quale; it is an a priori intelligibility-fact, and your error theory has no grip on it.

**Phenomenologist.** You concede too much to physicalism. By staking the thesis on introspection being "broadly veridical *about the world it discloses*," you let the reductionist set the terms: veridicality becomes correct world-tracking, and felt character is declared *exhausted* by the noema. That is SP-A asserted, not earned. Husserlian directedness shows experience is *of* an object; it does not show the felt character *is nothing over and above* that directedness. Run my conceivability test: hold the full intentional structure fixed and the question "why is this directedness *lived* at all?" stays intelligible. You dissolved the inner screen and quietly kept the gap — then declared it closed by fiat.

**Realist (ally).** We agree the comparison fails and SP-A is false — but I reach it a priori and you reach it by positing a determinate phenomenal given known by acquaintance. That positive given is a hostage: it invites both the illusionist's debunking and the contemplative's dissolution, which target the *stability of the datum* directly. My route needs no inventory of qualia — only the persistent intelligibility of Levine's question after all facts are fixed. The gap is a fact about *concepts and reason*, immune to error theories about felt residue. Lean on the a priori half and the meta-problem move has nothing to grip; lean on the given and you are in a knife-fight over whether the given holds still — a fight you may lose.
