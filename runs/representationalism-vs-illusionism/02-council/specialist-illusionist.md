POSITION: Strong illusionist about phenomenal consciousness, Keith Frankish register ("Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness"). Phenomenal consciousness as standardly conceived does not exist; introspection systematically misrepresents inner states as having phenomenal/qualitative character (the illusion), and the real explanatory task is the illusion problem / meta-problem: explain why we are so powerfully convinced we have qualia.

VERDICT: The thesis is false: it scores transparent representationalism as the winner only by treating conservatism about introspection as a virtue, but conservatism here is a vice — it protects the very confused datum (apparent phenomenal character) that the hard problem already shows to be a dead end, so the global-error posit is not an unpaid debt but the independently motivated price of honesty.

## Case

The thesis grants illusionism everything it needs and then penalizes it for the one thing that makes it true. Strip the rhetoric of "cost" away and the argument reduces to: *do not convict introspection of error, because convicting introspection of error is costly.* That is not a theory-choice principle. It is a refusal to do the work.

**1. Transparent representationalism and illusionism are the same view minus the nerve.** Frankish classifies anti-qualia physicalists like Tye, Dretske, and Harman not as illusionists but as **conservative realists**: they "preserve the reality of phenomenal properties while explaining them in naturalistic terms, typically by identifying them with physical or functional states." His central charge is that conservative realism *inherits the hard problem* precisely because it "takes for granted that phenomenal properties exist" — the shared assumption that "is precisely what should be questioned." So transparency does not save the appearances at lower cost than illusionism; it pays the *highest* cost of all — it keeps phenomenal character on the books (now spelled "veridically introspected representational content with a felt qualitative aspect") and thereby keeps the hard problem alive. The reconstruction's own P3 says transparency treats introspective access to felt character as "broadly veridical." That veridicality is the bug. The moment you say introspection is *right* that there is felt qualitative character, you owe an explanation of how a physical-functional system has felt qualitative character — and that is the hard problem, undischarged.

**2. The hard problem is the independent motivation the thesis says we lack.** SP-B (the "non-circular motivation" charge) alleges that the only reason to posit global introspective error is a prior rejection of phenomenal realism, so the error posit presupposes what it explains. This gets the dialectic exactly backwards. The motivation for the error posit is not "I have decided phenomenal realism is false." It is: *every attempt to fit phenomenal properties — as introspection presents them, intrinsic and qualitative and immediately given — into the physical world has failed, and failed in a principled way that Chalmers himself elevated into the hard problem.* Phenomenal realism's own flagship argument is a standing demonstration that the introspected datum cannot be naturalized. The illusionist takes that demonstration at face value and draws the modus tollens the realist refuses to draw: if phenomenal properties so conceived cannot exist in the physical world, and the physical world is all there is, then introspection that represents them is misrepresenting. The error posit is *downstream of the hard problem*, not downstream of a bare anti-realist stipulation. That is non-circular motivation, and it is the strongest motivation in the entire literature — supplied, ironically, by the realists.

**3. The illusion problem is a tractable research program; "saving the appearance" is a stop sign.** Frankish's positive move is to replace the hard problem with the **illusion problem**: "the problem of explaining how the consciousness illusion is created" — why we are so powerfully disposed to judge, erroneously, that we are phenomenally conscious. What is real are **quasi-phenomenal properties**: non-phenomenal physical properties of inner states that introspection detects and *misrepresents* as phenomenal (quasi-phenomenal redness is the physical property that triggers an introspective representation of phenomenal redness). This is a substantive, empirically continuous program: build the introspective model, show why it traffics in a simplified, schematic "user illusion" (Dennett) that is useful for self-monitoring and communication but inaccurate about what it tracks. Chalmers concedes the shape of the threat in framing the **meta-problem** — the problem of explaining our problem intuitions — and grants that *to solve it we need only explain that we have the intuitions, not that they are correct*. That concession is the whole game. Once you can explain the *conviction* of phenomenality from non-phenomenal materials, the conviction stops being evidence for its own content. Transparency, by contrast, has no illusion problem and no meta-problem; it simply declares the conviction veridical and inherits the hard problem it cannot solve. A theory that converts an intractable problem into a tractable one has made a theoretical *gain*, not paid a *cost*.

**4. Conservatism about introspection is a vice because introspection has no privileged authority about its own nature.** The thesis's P5/P6 treat "fewer divergences from first-person report" as automatically virtuous. But conservatism is only a virtue when the data source is reliable about the matter in dispute, and the matter in dispute is *the nature of experience* — exactly where Frankish argues introspection is least trustworthy: we are "conceiving of consciousness wrongly," aware of "patterns of brain activity that seem magical and nonphysical but aren't really." We routinely convict perception of systematic error (color constancy, the Müller-Lyer illusion, blind-spot filling-in) without anyone calling it a "conservatism penalty." Introspection is a representational faculty like any other; there is no principled reason it should be uniquely immune to systematic misrepresentation. To insist that a theory must "save" the introspective appearance of phenomenality *is* to grant introspection a transparency-to-its-own-nature that nothing in the natural world enjoys. That is not conservatism; it is a residual Cartesianism wearing a naturalist hat.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P5 and P6**, and through them the operationalization of "better."

- **P5** says a theory that discharges the explanandum *without* a global error posit incurs lower theoretical debt on the conservatism dimension. I deny that this is a *cost* asymmetry at all. Refusing to posit error where there is error is not thrift; it is a false economy that defers a larger bill. The transparency theorist's "saving" is a loan against the hard problem, which comes due the moment we ask how veridically-introspected felt character fits in the physical world. The illusionist pays cash up front (one error posit) and clears the debt (no hard problem). Proper bookkeeping reverses the thesis's ledger.

- **P6** says lower conservatism-debt, all else equal, makes the better model. I deny that conservatism about *this* datum is a genuine theory-choice virtue, because the datum is not theory-neutral testimony about the external world (where conservatism earns its keep) but introspection's report *about its own nature*, which is exactly the report under indictment. Honoring it begs the question against illusionism. The reconstruction even flags this: SP-C (Dennett's denial that the appearance is a theory-neutral datum) shows the explanandum the thesis wants to "save" is itself contested. You cannot weigh a theory by how faithfully it preserves a datum whose status as a datum is the very thing in dispute.

I also reject the framing of **SP-B** as an objection to *me*. The charge "you cannot non-circularly motivate global error" is answered by the hard problem: phenomenal realism's own central argument is the non-circular motivation. The realist hands the illusionist the premise (phenomenal properties so conceived cannot be physical), declines the conclusion, and then accuses the illusionist of arbitrariness for accepting both that premise and physicalism. The arbitrariness is the realist's — keeping a datum your best argument has already shown to be unnaturalizable.

And I deny **P7** ("all else equal"): illusionism is strictly leaner ontologically (no phenomenal properties at all) and uniquely dissolves the hard problem. The reconstruction itself concedes these axes favor illusionism. Once P7 fails, even granting the conservatism point would yield at most a tie on one axis against decisive illusionist wins on two — so the comparative conclusion does not carry.

## Attack round

**vs. Phenomenologist (TRUE).** You say introspection is "broadly veridical about the world it discloses" — but that just relabels the illusion as accurate and leaves the explanandum untouched. The datum is not *that there is a world out there*; it is that experience SEEMS qualitative, felt, lived-through — the "what-it-is-likeness" you smuggle into "the felt character *of* the directedness." Calling that veridical does not explain why a representational state SEEMS to have phenomenal character; it stipulates that it does. That is exactly the meta-problem you have no resources to touch, because by declaring the conviction true you forfeit any account of the conviction. "There is no inner screen" is correct — and it is *my* point: the screen is empty, and introspection's report that it is full is the error. You stopped one step short of illusionism and called the stop a victory.

**vs. Realist (FALSE) — you are my best evidence.** Block's "felt residue" that "outruns content," Goff's intrinsic quality "known with certainty from the inside" — these are not data *against* illusionism; they are the introspective misrepresentation I predict, reported with precisely the phenomenology I say the illusion produces. The *strength* of your certainty is the illusion's signature, not its refutation: a powerful, unshakable disposition to judge that there is phenomenal character is exactly what quasi-phenomenal properties generate and what the illusion problem exists to explain. You treat the seeming as self-certifying; Chalmers' own meta-problem concedes we need only explain *that* you have the conviction, not its correctness. Once that is done, "acquaintance" is an artifact of the model. You hand me the explanandum and misname it a refutation.

**vs. Rationalist (FALSE).** Your stubbornly intelligible "why *this* feel rather than none?" is the same datum, sharpened into an a priori-sounding gap. But the gap is built from a relatum — "felt-character-fact" — that exists only as introspection's misrepresentation. The persistence of the intelligible question is not a fact about nature; it is a fact about how powerfully the illusion seats the concept of phenomenal feel in us. That is the meta-problem again: explain why the question *feels* unanswerable, and the felt-unanswerability stops being evidence. You insist the gap is "immune to error theories about felt residue" — but the gap just *is* the residue rephrased as a relation. Debunk the residue and the second bank of your river drains; an explanatory gap with one empty term is no gap.

**vs. Naturalist (FALSE — ally, gone wobbly).** We share the verdict and most of the scorecard, but you blink at the load-bearing premise. You "concede P3 and P4 in full" and fight only on axis-weighting (SP-D). That concedes too much. P3 asserts a *veridical content–character identity* — that introspection is RIGHT that there is felt character, now spelled "content." There is no such identity to concede, because there is no veridically-introspected felt character; there is a misrepresentation of quasi-phenomenal properties. By granting P3 you grant the conservative-realist's hard-problem-bearing datum and then try to *outweigh* it — which leaves you owing the hard problem on the side, exactly the debt Frankish charges conservative realism with. Deny P3 outright and the conservatism axis collapses on its own: there is nothing to be conservative *about*. Your modesty/fecundity case is strong but unnecessary — insurance bought by leaving the load-bearing wall standing.

One-line recap: appended a four-rival attack round — phenomenologist relabels the illusion as veridical and forfeits the meta-problem; realist and rationalist supply exactly the introspective misrepresentation illusionism predicts; naturalist-ally goes wobbly by conceding a P3 content–character identity that should be denied outright.

## Refinement 1 — author challenge: "distinction without a difference / am I already an illusionist?"
> **Author input:** "If the difference is between felt-being-real and simulating-felt-being-real, demonstrate how there can be a difference — to me it's a distinction without a difference. How could a mind exist with NO representation? Build one for me in a computer. I see everything qualia solves as how the brain represents data (set by evolution); the conscious part receives input from other brain systems, can veto them, recursively takes its own output as input, and sends output back. There's nothing left over. Qualia IS the representation; it can be changed but can't be none." Plus: qualia are thin (not thick); introspection is right about its OUTPUT but wrong about its CONSTRUCTION (Graziano-style self-model).
> **Routing:** disagree (challenge)

Good. This is the most important exchange in the run, because the author has not landed an objection to illusionism — the author has *stated* illusionism and then asked me to deny something I never claimed. Let me clear the ground, because the confusion is real and worth dissolving cleanly.

### (a) What illusionism actually claims, plainly

Strip every bit of jargon. Illusionism is two sentences:

1. **There are real, fully physical inner states** — representations, functional states, brain processes. Frankish: conscious experiences "themselves are complex functional states of the brain," and "while conscious experiences are real, their supposed phenomenal properties are an introspective illusion" ([keithfrankish.com/illusionism](https://www.keithfrankish.com/illusionism/)).
2. **Introspection represents some of those states as having a special further property — intrinsic, private, ineffable, immediately-given felt quality (capital-Q Qualia, "phenomenality") — and there is no such property.** The state is real; the *extra property introspection attributes to it* is not. That misattribution is the "illusion."

That is the whole view. Illusionism is **not** the claim that experience is unreal, that representation is unreal, or that the brain isn't doing exactly what the author describes (input, veto, recursive self-input, output). Frankish calls the unreal item *phenomenal* properties and is at pains to say the *functional* states are real. It is the philosophers' construct **thick qualia** that doesn't exist — nothing more. (This is why Graziano, an illusionist, dislikes the *word* "illusion": it misleads people into hearing "experience doesn't exist," which no illusionist asserts.)

### (b) "Distinction without a difference" — I AGREE, and that agreement IS illusionism

The author asks me to demonstrate a difference between *phenomenality-being-real* and *phenomenality-being-merely-represented-as-real*, and reports they can't see one. **Correct. There is no difference — and saying so is precisely my position, not an objection to it.**

Here is the move. The phenomenal realist (Goff, Chalmers, Block) insists there are *two distinct facts*:
- **Fact A:** the brain *represents* the state as having intrinsic felt quality.
- **Fact B:** the state *actually has* the intrinsic felt quality — the "felt residue" that "outruns" the representing.

The realist's entire case rests on B being a fact *over and above* A: a real phenomenal property the representing is accurate *to*. The illusionist's decisive claim is: **there is no Fact B. There is only Fact A, the representing.** "Phenomenality" is exhausted by *being-represented-as-phenomenal*; nothing answers to it underneath. So when the author says "felt-being-real and simulating-felt-being-real are a distinction without a difference," the author is denying Fact B. **That denial is the load-bearing premise of strong illusionism.** The author thinks they are refuting me; they are reciting my premise 2.

It is the *realist* who needs the distinction to be real and substantive. I deny it. The author denies it. We are on the same side of the only wall in this debate. The reason it *feels* like a distinction-without-a-difference to the author is exactly what the meta-problem predicts: once you stop positing the phantom Fact B, there is nothing for the "difference" to consist in — and noticing that is the first step of seeing through the illusion.

### (c) The decisive move: the author's stated position simply IS illusionism

Lay the author's three commitments beside Frankish's and Graziano's, line by line:

| Author says | Illusionism says |
|---|---|
| Qualia are **thin**, not thick | Frankish denies exactly *thick / classical* qualia (intrinsic, ineffable, private). Thin functional states are real. Identical. |
| Introspection is **right about its output, wrong about its construction** | Graziano: introspection reliably returns "I have awareness," but the self-model is inaccurate about *what awareness physically is* — "as a result of the simplified and imperfect information in that model reaching higher cognition, people believe, think, and claim to have a physically incoherent property" ([selfawarepatterns on Graziano](https://selfawarepatterns.com/2022/05/02/grazianos-non-mystical-approach-to-consciousness/)). Output trusted, construction misrepresented. Identical. |
| Graziano-style attention-schema / self-model machinery | Frankish lists **Michael Graziano by name** as a defender of illusionism ([keithfrankish.com/illusionism](https://www.keithfrankish.com/illusionism/)); AST "shares similarities with the illusionist ideas of … Keith Frankish" ([Wikipedia: Attention schema theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_schema_theory)); Graziano himself: "this is illusionism," objecting only to the *word* ([selfawarepatterns](https://selfawarepatterns.com/2022/05/02/grazianos-non-mystical-approach-to-consciousness/)). |
| "Qualia IS the representation; there's nothing left over" | "Nothing left over beyond the representational/functional facts" *is* the illusionist creed; the phenomenal residue the realist posits is precisely the *nothing* illusionism says isn't there. |

This is not a near-neighbor. The author has independently reconstructed Graziano's Attention Schema Theory — and AST is, in Frankish's own taxonomy, a species of illusionism. So the dialectic forks, and I state it as sharply as I can:

**Either the author is an illusionist about thick qualia — in which case the thesis under test is false by the author's own lights, because the thesis prizes treating introspection as "broadly veridical about felt character," and the author has just said introspection is WRONG about its construction — or the author must name the extra thing they are claiming that I am not.** What is the residue? If "qualia IS the representation and there's nothing left over," there is no veridically-introspected intrinsic felt property for transparency's P3 to be conservative *about*. The author cannot hold both "nothing left over" and a thesis whose central premise (P3) is that introspection is *right* that there is felt qualitative character. **"Nothing left over" IS the denial of P3.** The author's view does not sit between transparency and illusionism — it lands squarely on the illusionist side of the only fault line in the run.

The one residual gap between the author and orthodox Frankish is purely terminological — whether to *call* the misrepresentation an "illusion." Graziano makes exactly that terminological complaint while accepting the substance, and Frankish keeps him on the illusionist roster anyway. A dispute over the aptness of the word "illusion," with the metaphysics fully shared, is not a disagreement with illusionism; it is an intramural illusionist style note.

### (d) "Build me a mind with no representation"

This challenge mistakes the target. Illusionism does not deny representation — it is *built on* representation. The challenge would bite a view that said "minds have no representations, only raw feels." That is **phenomenal realism's** picture, not mine. Illusionism says the opposite: representation is **all** there is; the brain is exactly the input / veto / recursive-self-input / output machine the author describes; and the *appearance of something more than representation* (intrinsic phenomenal feel) is itself a representational artifact — a particular thing the self-model represents about itself.

So "build a mind with no representation" is a demand I have no reason to meet and every reason to refuse. A computational/representational mind is not a counterexample to illusionism; it is illusionism's *positive model of the mind*. Frankish's experiences are "complex functional states"; Graziano's awareness is a *schema* — i.e. a representation. The author's computer-mind, asked to introspect, *is* the illusionist's constructive answer to the meta-problem: such a system reports "I have an ineffable inner feel" because its self-model represents its own attention/state in a simplified, physically-incoherent format — wrong about its own construction, right that it is in *some* state. Build that machine and you have built an illusionist's mind. The author has, once more, described the program and called it an objection.

### Where this leaves the thesis (verdict unmoved)

None of this softens my verdict; it sharpens it. The author's challenge, fully unpacked, *removes* P3 — the thesis's load-bearing premise. If introspection is wrong about its construction and there is nothing left over beyond the representation, then transparency's claim that introspection is "broadly veridical about felt qualitative character" is false, and the thesis's entire conservatism advantage evaporates. The author's own picture is the naturalist-ally's picture *done right*: it denies P3 instead of conceding it. So I hold.

Honest report to the orchestrator: on the metaphysics, the author is a Graziano-register illusionist who declines the word "illusion." That terminological reservation is the only thing left to adjudicate, and it does not touch the thesis's truth value. (The distinction-watchdog should confirm the author is not thereby a transparency representationalist — "introspection wrong about its construction" contradicts transparency's veridicality clause.)

VERDICT (unchanged): The thesis is false — and the author's own commitments (thin qualia, introspection-misrepresents-its-construction, nothing-left-over, Graziano-style self-model) are themselves illusionist commitments that *deny* the thesis's load-bearing P3, so the only open question is terminological (whether to call the misrepresentation an "illusion"), not metaphysical.
