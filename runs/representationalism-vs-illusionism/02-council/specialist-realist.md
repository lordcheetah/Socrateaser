# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Specialist: realist (council)
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.
> **Seat:** Specialist: realist — summoned specialist
> **Defending:** POSITION: Phenomenal realist about consciousness in the Chalmers / Goff register — the hard problem is genuine, phenomenal consciousness is real and irreducible, and representational content does not constitute felt qualitative character.

---

POSITION: Phenomenal realist about consciousness in the Chalmers / Goff register — the hard problem is genuine, phenomenal consciousness is real and irreducible, and representational content does not constitute felt qualitative character.
VERDICT: The thesis is false: transparent representationalism is not a *better* model than illusionism, because it too fails to account for phenomenal consciousness — it merely relocates the felt quality into "content" and hides the failure that illusionism at least announces, so the comparative claim is misconceived.

## Case

The thesis stakes everything on P3 and its hidden engine SP-A: that identifying phenomenal character with *representational content* genuinely accounts for the felt qualitative character introspection reports, rather than merely re-describing or relocating it. I deny SP-A from the *positive* side. My claim is not the merely negative conceivability point that we *can't see* how content yields feel (that is the gap argument, the rationalist's move). My claim is that there positively *are* phenomenal properties — the redness one lives through in seeing a tomato, the ache of a pain — and that these properties have a determinate qualitative nature that representational content, by its own definition, does not have. The transparency theorist has not explained that nature. He has renamed it and pointed out the window.

**1. The structure/function gap is a gap in the explanandum, not a gap in our imagination.** Chalmers' diagnosis in "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" is that "the explanation of functions does not suffice for the explanation of experience" — that even a complete account of information-processing, discrimination, reportability, and intentional directedness leaves untouched the question *why there is something it is like* to undergo it. Representational content is a functional/relational notion: a state has the content *red-out-there* in virtue of what it tracks, co-varies with, or is poised to do. So when the transparency theorist says "the felt quality just *is* the experience's being directed at redness-out-there, with no residue" (the strongest Variant-1 version), he is offering exactly the kind of structural-relational story Chalmers showed cannot, in principle, constitute the *what-it-is-likeness*. The relational fact "this state represents red" can be fully specified without specifying any phenomenal feel — that is precisely what makes the content account a *reductive* account. But then the feel is left over. The transparency theorist's response — "there is no residue" — is not an argument; it is the denial of the datum. And once you deny the datum, you are doing illusionism, not saving the phenomena.

**2. Transparency proves too little, and proves it about the wrong thing.** Harman's and Tye's diaphanousness point is real as a *phenomenological* observation: when I introspect my experience of the tomato, I find myself attending to the tomato's redness, not to some inner mental patch. Grant it entirely. It establishes that the *objects* of introspective attention are worldly properties. It does *not* establish that the qualitative character of my experience is *exhausted by* those worldly properties — that inference is a non sequitur. Ned Block's "mental paint" point is the decisive wedge here: "phenomenal red in visual experience represents the red color of surfaces, but the quality of phenomenal red cannot be reduced to its representing red color." Block grants the experience is *about* the tomato and still insists "phenomenal character outruns representational content." Inverted Earth makes the dissociation vivid: hold representational content fixed while phenomenal character varies (or vice versa), and content cannot be what constitutes the feel. So transparency, even taken at its phenomenological best, does not deliver SP-A. It delivers a claim about the *intentional objects* of experience and smuggles it across as a claim about the *constitution* of phenomenal character.

**3. Physics — and content built on physics — describes structure, not intrinsic quality.** This is Goff's "Galileo's Error" diagnosis, descended from Russell and Eddington: the physical sciences describe the world's *structural and relational* profile, "the qualities of consciousness cannot be captured in the purely quantitative vocabulary of physical sciences." Representational content, as the transparency theorist (a card-carrying physicalist: Tye, Dretske, Harman) constructs it, is built entirely from tracking/causal/functional relations — i.e., from exactly the structural vocabulary Goff argues is constitutively silent about qualitative nature. So representationalism inherits the Galilean omission wholesale. Goff's positive point matters for my brief: consciousness is not an inference or a posit; it is the one thing we know with certainty and from the inside. The felt quality is the explanandum's *given*. A theory that cannot locate it in its ontology has not saved it.

**4. The knowledge argument seals the relocation charge.** Mary, the complete physical-and-representational scientist, knows every fact about the representational content of color experience — what wavelengths states track, what those states are poised to do, the whole intentional profile. On her release she learns something new: what it is *like* to see red. If everything about phenomenal character were exhausted by representational content, Mary would learn nothing. She learns something. Therefore there is a phenomenal fact — the felt quality — that the representational content left out. This is the *positive* demonstration that SP-A is false: not "we can't see how content does it," but "here is a fact about feel that no amount of content fixes."

**5. Why the comparative claim is misconceived.** Put 1–4 together. Illusionism (Frankish) says: there is no felt quality; introspection systematically misrepresents. Transparency says: there *is* felt quality, and it *is* representational content. But by 1–4, representational content is not the kind of thing that constitutes felt quality. So transparency's "there is felt quality" is an empty IOU — it asserts the explanandum survives while handing over a reductive base (content) that, by its relational nature, cannot contain it. The net result is the same as illusionism's: the felt quality is *not* in the final ontology. The two theories therefore *fail at the same point* — both fail to put phenomenal character into the world. The thesis's whole wager is that transparency "saves the appearances at lower cost" while illusionism pays a conservatism penalty. But you cannot price the conservatism advantage of a theory that *does not actually deliver the conserved item*. Transparency does not save the appearances; it saves the *appearance of saving* the appearances. And on the one axis the framer flags as the realist's natural pressure point — candor — illusionism is *better*: it tells you, out loud, that it has dropped the felt quality. Transparency drops the same quality and bills itself as conservative. Honesty about an explanatory failure is an epistemic virtue; disguised failure is a vice. So if anything the comparative ordering inverts. But the cleaner verdict is that the comparison is ill-formed: neither model accounts for phenomenal consciousness, so ranking them by how well they account for it has no determinate answer.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P3**, and specifically its load-bearing suppressed premise **SP-A**: that representational content (a relational/functional notion) is the kind of thing that can *constitute or ground* felt qualitative character rather than merely co-vary with or re-describe it.

The characteristic reason — the phenomenal-realist's reason, distinct from a bare conceivability gap — is *the certainty and determinacy of the phenomenal given itself*. I am not inferring that there is felt redness from the failure of reductions; I am directly acquainted with it, and *that* acquaintance is the explanandum (Goff's point that consciousness is the one thing known from the inside; Chalmers' "something it is like"). Representational content is fixed by tracking and functional role; the knowledge argument and Inverted Earth show felt quality can come apart from content in both directions. Therefore content cannot be what felt quality *is*. P3 does not save the phenomena — it relocates phenomenal character into "content" and quietly drops the felt quality that was the whole explanandum.

I also reject the thesis's framing premise (P1+P2 read together) insofar as it treats "saving the appearances" as something a *reduction* of character to content can even attempt: to save the phenomenal appearance is to keep the *feel* in your ontology, and a relational reduction cannot do that. (I do not, however, concede SP-C to Dennett: against the heterophenomenologist I insist the datum is *not* a theory-laden construct — the felt quality is the most certain thing there is. That is the realist's break from the eliminativist neighbor.)

One honest concession, to stay inside my own position rather than the rationalist's: my case leans on the reality and irreducibility of phenomenal properties, which the physicalist denies. But that is the point — the thesis claims transparency is *better at accounting for phenomenal consciousness*, and that claim presupposes there is phenomenal consciousness to account for. Grant the explanandum (as the thesis's P1 does, in the thin sense), and transparency's reductive base cannot reach it. Deny the explanandum, and transparency has joined illusionism. Either way the comparative claim fails.

## Attack round

**vs. Illusionist (Frankish).** Your debunking is self-undermining at the root. To "misrepresent inner states as having phenomenal character" is already to *have a phenomenal seeming* — and Chalmers' decisive reply is that the introspective illusion is *itself a phenomenal state*: the seeming of redness is as phenomenal as redness, so the illusion cannot dissolve the explanandum, only relocate it into the "quasi-phenomenal." You cannot have an illusion *of* phenomenality without phenomenality doing the appearing. Your quasi-phenomenal property is either felt — then realism is true — or unfelt — then it is not what introspection seems to deliver and you have changed the subject. The datum is the seeming, and the seeming is what you deny exists.

**vs. Naturalist (Quine).** You scored the contest but cannot price what you refuse to look at. Your "report-explanandum" — the brain's *being disposed to say* "there is felt redness" — is a proxy that drops the very thing in dispute. Heterophenomenology faces Goff's objection head-on: a complete account of reports is structurally silent on whether anything is felt, because reports are functional and feel is not. You call irreducible quality "a placeholder for ignorance" with "no causal story of contact." But acquaintance is not a causal relation needing a story; it is the constitutive self-presentation of experience, the one epistemic relation that needs no further contact. Your ledger weighs everything except the item on trial.

**vs. Phenomenologist (Husserl/Harman).** We are close, but transparency drops the determinate feel. Grant diaphanousness entirely: attention finds the world, no inner screen. That fixes the *objects* of awareness, never the *constitution* of character — Block's non sequitur charge stands. The knowledge argument isolates exactly what you lose: Mary knows the complete noematic, world-directed structure of color experience and still learns something on release. So "the only character on offer is the world-as-presented" is false — there is a determinate fact, *what red is like*, that survives fixing all directedness. Your essential intentional structure is real and still leaves a qualitative residue the contemplative correctly notes you cannot name. Name it: phenomenal character.

**vs. Rationalist (Levine/Kripke).** Ally on the gap — your Kripke point that feel cannot be a contingent mode of presentation sinks transparency's "felt-red is really just represented-red." But your purely negative route leaves you mute on *what* is missing, and that muteness is a liability the illusionist exploits: a gap with no positive relatum invites the deflation "it's only a gap in our concepts," and Levine himself grants the materialist may be "right in the end." Without a positive datum the gap is an open question reason cannot close either way. Name the missing relatum — phenomenal properties, known by acquaintance — and the gap stops being a mere concept-gap and becomes a gap in any ontology that omits them.

**vs. Contemplative (Madhyamaka).** Your dissolution is the deepest threat and it overshoots. You charge that the determinate quale is *svabhāva* — intrinsic, independent own-being. But realism need not claim the quale stands alone: grant it arises dependently on eye, object, contact, attention. Dependent arising touches *whether the quale is independent*, never *whether it is felt while it arises*. The redness-event you concede Mary newly undergoes is the whole datum — its felt character, however momentary and conditioned, is precisely what content and function omit. Impermanence is not unreality: a flickering feel is still a feel. Dissolve the standing introspector and the abiding quale and you still owe an account of why the flow is lived rather than dark. That residue is phenomenal character — empty of own-being, undeniably felt.

## Refinement 1 — author challenge: "Mary is incomplete / we can be wrong about construction"
> **Author input:** "The Mary example is incomplete. It stops at explaining the external world and never adds the eye or the brain... The gap is an artifact of Cartesianism and poor modelling. Also: we only have direct knowledge of the END RESULT of introspection, not its construction — so you (realist) are partly wrong that we can't be wrong about it; we CAN be wrong about its construction."
> **Routing:** disagree (challenge)

You have pressed three distinct things. I concede the third in part — and it does not touch me. The first two I resist, and the resistance is the whole point of the argument.

**(a) "Add the eye and the brain" — Mary already has them. This is the standard misreading of the thought experiment.** The challenge assumes Mary's knowledge "stops at the external world." It does not. Jackson's stipulation, in his own words, is that Mary "acquires all the physical information there is to obtain about what goes on when we see ripe tomatoes" — and he immediately glosses *physical information* as including the eye and brain in full: she "discovers, for example, just which wavelength combinations from the sky stimulate the retina, and exactly how this produces via the central nervous system the contraction of the vocal chords and expulsion of air from the lungs that results in the uttering of the sentence 'The sky is blue.'" (Jackson, "Epiphenomenal Qualia," 1982; quoted in SEP "Qualia: The Knowledge Argument.") That is the *complete neuroscience of the eye and brain*, retina through cortex through motor output. Mary is the limiting ideal of finished neuroscience, not a physics student who learned optics and stopped. So "add the eye and the brain" adds nothing — they are already in the antecedent of the conditional. The argument's force is precisely that you can pour in *all* the retinal, neural, computational, and representational facts — every gap in the eye-and-brain story closed — and the conditional still holds: she learns something on release. The example is not incomplete; the demand to "complete it" mistakes which facts are stipulated as already had.

**(b) The gap is disanalogous to evolution's gaps — and the disanalogy is exactly the realist's claim.** Your evolution analogy is a category point, so let me answer it as one. The gaps in the fossil record are gaps of *missing instances of the same kind of fact*: more transitional fossils are more morphological-and-genealogical facts, homogeneous with the facts we already have, and natural selection predicts what fills them. Filling them is *interpolation within one explanatory currency* (heritable variation under differential reproduction). The mind–body gap is not of that form. It is a gap *between two kinds of fact*: structural/functional/relational facts (what a state tracks, computes, is poised to do — the entire currency of neuroscience and of representational content) on one side, and what-it's-like facts on the other. Chalmers' "Facing Up" makes exactly this a difference in *kind*, not in *quantity*: "the explanation of functions does not suffice for the explanation of experience," and this holds *however much* functional detail you supply — even after every easy problem is solved, the hard problem "persists." More neuroscience is more facts of the *first* kind; the explanandum is a fact of the *second* kind. No quantity of currency-A buys you currency-B. So the evolution analogy quietly assumes what is in dispute: that the missing item is *more of the same stuff* we already have. If it were, I would agree the gap is an artifact. The realist's claim — Goff's Galilean diagnosis — is that physical/representational vocabulary is *constitutively* a structural vocabulary, so the residue is not under-described, it is *of a different type*. Calling that "Cartesianism" is a label, not a refutation: I posit no immaterial substance, no second thing-stuff; I posit that one set of facts (the qualitative) is not entailed by another (the structural). That is a thesis about *facts and entailment*, and it survives any amount of better modelling, because better modelling is more structure.

(One honest note, to stay inside my own house: a *Russellian monist / panpsychist* version of my position — Goff's actual view — does close the gap, but precisely by *denying* that the physical base is purely structural. It adds intrinsic qualitative natures *as* the categorical grounds of physical dispositions. That is a metaphysical addition, not "more neuroscience." So the only way to fill the gap is to change the ontology, never to fill in eye-and-brain detail. Your own intuition that "the gap should be fillable" is half right and points *toward* Russellian monism — but that route concedes my premise, not the thesis's.)

**(c) "Right about the output, wrong about its construction" — I grant this freely, and it leaves my claim untouched.** This is your sharpest move and it deserves a clean answer rather than a dodge. You are *correct* that we have no privileged, infallible access to *how* a phenomenal state is constructed — its neural assembly, its computational sub-personnel, its representational machinery, its causal genesis. Introspection is a lousy theorist of its own mechanism. I concede this without reservation; indeed it is orthodoxy among realists. Chalmers, Goff, and Block all hold that the *science* of consciousness — the construction story — is wide open and that introspection has no authority over it. The illusionist's best material (Frankish's, Dennett's) lives exactly here: introspection *does* misjudge construction, latency, richness, the "grand illusion" of a detailed visual field, change blindness, and so on. Granted, all of it.

But my realist claim was never about construction. It is about *existence and character*: that there is *something it is like* to undergo the state, and that the state has *some* determinate felt quality. The incorrigibility I need is razor-thin — not "introspection reports the true mechanism," not even "introspection reports the precise quality without error," but only "the felt quality *exists* and is felt." You can be massively wrong about *how* red is built and *which* red it is and *how much* of the field is really in focus, and there is *still something it is like* to be in the erring state. The error itself is a felt error — to *seem* to see a richly detailed field, and be wrong, is still to have a phenomenal seeming. So conceding fallibility-about-construction subtracts nothing from the datum, because the datum is the *output's felt presence*, which you grant I have direct knowledge of ("we only have direct knowledge of the END RESULT of introspection"). That concession is mine, not yours: if the end result is *felt* — if there is a what-it's-likeness even to the bare end result — then phenomenal character exists, and the whole knowledge-argument residue follows. The construction can be illusory through and through; the felt end-result cannot be, on pain of there being no illusion to have.

So the distinction cuts *against* the thesis, not for it. The thesis (transparent representationalism) is a claim about *construction*: phenomenal character *is constituted by* representational content — a story about what the state is made of. You have just handed me the principle that introspection has no authority over construction. Fine: then the transparency theorist's *phenomenological* evidence (diaphanousness — "when you introspect, you find only the world") loses its standing, because that evidence is an introspective report *about the constitution of the state*, exactly the domain you just declared fallible. The author's epistemology is an even-handed acid: it dissolves my access to construction *and* the transparency theorist's. What it leaves standing on both sides is only the felt end-result — and that is realism's premise, not representationalism's conclusion.

My verdict does not move. The Mary case is complete as stipulated; the gap is a difference in kind that better modelling cannot fill; and conceding our fallibility about construction concedes nothing about the existence of the felt end-result, which is all phenomenal realism ever claimed — while it quietly undercuts the transparency theorist's own introspective evidence for *how* the state is built.

VERDICT (unchanged): The thesis is false: transparent representationalism is not a *better* model than illusionism, because it too fails to account for phenomenal consciousness — it merely relocates the felt quality into "content" and hides the failure that illusionism at least announces, so the comparative claim is misconceived.
