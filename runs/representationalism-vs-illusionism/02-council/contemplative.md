# Representationalism vs Illusionism — Contemplative (council)
> **Run:** representationalism-vs-illusionism · **Mode:** full · **Stage:** Council
> **Thesis:** Transparent representationalism is a better model of phenomenal consciousness than illusionism because it saves the appearances at lower explanatory cost — without convicting introspection of systematic error.
> **Seat:** Contemplative — Madhyamaka Buddhist, Nāgārjuna
> **Defending:** VERDICT: ILL-POSED — the contest presupposes a fixed felt datum and a stable introspector, but sustained first-person attention finds neither standing still long enough to be either "veridically tracked" (transparency) or "systematically misrepresented" (illusionism); the explanandum at P1 is itself a construction, so the comparative bookkeeping never gets a footing.

VERDICT: ILL-POSED — the contest presupposes a fixed felt datum and a stable introspector, but sustained first-person attention finds neither standing still long enough to be either "veridically tracked" (transparency) or "systematically misrepresented" (illusionism); the explanandum at P1 is itself a construction, so the comparative bookkeeping never gets a footing.

## Case

The thesis and its named rival fight over P3 versus P4: is introspection broadly *veridical* about felt character (transparency) or *systematically erroneous* (illusionism)? Both answers are downstream of P1 — that there is a stable manifest datum, "the felt qualitative character experience presents to introspection," that both theories must discharge. My assigned move (SP-C, against P1–P2) is to deny that this datum is stable and theory-neutral. But I do not take Dennett's third-person route — that there is no "seeming" prior to a subject's reportings, established by operationalizing the report. I take the *first-person disciplined-attention* route: when introspection is actually trained on the alleged felt datum and held there, the datum does not sit still to be either tracked or misrepresented, and neither does the one doing the looking. Both camps reify two things that closer looking does not find: a stable *appearance* and a stable *introspector*.

**The appearance does not hold still (against the stability of P1).** The Abhidharma analysis of experience is not armchair metaphysics; it is a phenomenology of what disciplined attention discloses. What presents as a single standing "felt redness" resolves, under attention, into an uninterrupted flowing continuum (*santāna*) of momentary, causally connected mental events — *citta* with its associated *cetasikas* — each arising and ceasing, the apparent continuity being a conceptual overlay on a rapid succession (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Abhidharma"). The "manifest datum" of P1 is exactly this overlay: a stabilized object posited by the act of theorizing about experience, not a thing handed to theory before theorizing. Transparency says we track it correctly; illusionism says we misrepresent it. But there is no *it* of the requisite kind — no enduring qualitative item — for the tracking-or-misrepresenting question to bite on. The question "veridical or erroneous *about the felt datum*?" presupposes a relatum that sustained attention dissolves.

**The introspector does not hold still either (the deeper reification).** Both theories also require a stable subject of introspection — someone to whom the appearance appears, who then reports veridically or in error. Nāgārjuna's examination of the prior entity targets exactly this. In Garfield's translation (Garfield 1995, MMK IX), verse 3 asks how an entity existing *prior to* seeing, hearing, and the felt could itself be known; verse 4 notes that if such a subject could abide without the seen, then equally the seen could abide without it; and verse 5 reads "Someone is disclosed by something. / Something is disclosed by someone. / Without something how can someone exist? / Without someone how can something exist?" The experiencer and the experienced are mutually dependent (*pratītyasamutpāda*); neither is the freestanding term the debate needs. MMK III.5 (Garfield 1995) makes the parallel point on the side of the faculty: "Seeing itself does not see. / Nonseeing itself does not see. / Through seeing itself / The clear analysis of the seer is understood." There is no seer set over against seeing, antecedently constituted, who could be reliable or unreliable *about* it.

This is why I reach for the contemplative tradition rather than Dennett. The Yogācāra and pramāṇa debate over *svasaṃvedana* (reflexive awareness) shows that the very idea of an introspective grip on experience is itself contested *within* the first-person tradition: Dignāga and Dharmakīrti posited a self-cognizing aspect to secure that awareness knows itself, while Candrakīrti and Śāntideva rejected it precisely because a self-validating, inherently reflexive awareness would have to possess *svabhāva* — own-being — which nothing has (Wikipedia, "Svasaṃvedana"; Williams, *The Reflexive Nature of Awareness*). Both the representationalist and the illusionist help themselves to a notion of "introspective access" as if its structure were given. It is not. The faculty by which the datum is supposedly delivered is as constructed and as empty as the datum.

**Why this is ILL-POSED and not merely undecided.** The two-truths distinction is the precise tool here. *Conventionally* (saṃvṛti), people report felt character, theories model those reports, some models are more useful than others — all of that functions, and I am not denying it. *Ultimately* (paramārtha), there is no independently existing qualitative item with an own-nature, and no independently existing introspector, to ground a fact of the matter about whether introspection "really" tracks or "really" misfires. The thesis's whole wager — that transparency saves a real appearance cheaply while illusionism convicts it of error expensively — runs the *ultimate* register (there is a real datum, and the question is whether we get it right) while flying the *conventional* flag (we just mean the reports). Collapse the two registers and the comparative-cost ledger evaporates: at the conventional level both theories agree on every report and behavior (P7), and at the ultimate level there is no fixed explanandum for either to save or to declare illusory. The dispute is well-posed only at a register where its central term has no truth-maker.

**Heading off the nihilism charge.** Emptiness is not the claim that nothing is better or worse. The conventional level is fully real-enough: vipassana practice does not end in "anything goes" but in a *sharper* discrimination of impermanent, selfless process. So I am not saying both theories are equally good. I am saying the axis on which the thesis declares transparency the winner — "saves a fixed appearance at lower conservatism cost" — is built on a reified explanandum, and a verdict computed on that axis inherits the defect. The soteriological point is not decoration: the grasping after a stable felt given that *must* be either saved or explained away is itself a form of the clinging that the analysis is meant to loosen. Both camps are gripping the same phantom from opposite ends.

## The shared assumption I reject

The thesis, its illusionist opponent, *and* the naturalist/rationalist/phenomenologist seats all assume the question is well-formed: that there is a determinate explanandum (a felt appearance) and a determinate faculty (introspection) such that the only open question is whether the faculty gets the explanandum *right or wrong*. Transparency answers "right," illusionism answers "wrong," realists answer "neither theory captures it" — but all four agree there is a fixed *it* and a fixed *grip on it*, so that everything hangs on the accuracy verdict. That is the foundationalist reflex in epistemic clothing: the demand for a stable given that a faculty either secures or fails to secure. My positive claim — not a dodge — is that the felt datum and the introspector are *co-arising conventional constructs with no own-being*, so the binary "veridical vs. illusory" has no third, stable term to range over. Dissolve the reified relata and the dispute does not get answered; it stops being a dispute.

## Attack round

The five rivals quarrel over which end of one constructed object is real. Each clings to a different svabhāva — and the disagreement *requires* the shared phantom.

**Realist (Goff/Block seat).** You make the felt quale the one thing "known with certainty and from the inside," irreducible, self-presenting. That is *svabhāva* in its purest form: a property whose nature is intrinsic, non-relational, independent of anything it depends on. But Nāgārjuna's analysis of dependent arising (MMK XXIV, on emptiness as the meaning of dependent origination) cuts exactly here — what arises dependently has no own-being, and the redness you live through arises only with eye, object, contact, and attention. Strip those and no determinate quale stands. Your "certainty" is the felt grip itself reified into a thing gripped. Mary learns a new *event of seeing*, not a pre-existing item she was missing. (≈100 words)

**Rationalist (Levine/Kripke seat).** You reify the *gap* — treating "why this feel rather than none?" as a fixed, standing fact that no identity closes. But the gap is a relation between two posited relata (content-fact, feel-fact); dissolve the standing relata and the gap has no edges to hold open. Your intelligible question persists only because you keep both terms frozen for it to range between. Madhyamaka does not close the gap; it denies that either bank exists with the solidity your conceivability test silently requires. A gap between two empty things is itself empty — not a permanent feature of nature, but of the grasping.

**Naturalist (Quine seat).** You replace the felt given with a stable *third-person fact* — the web, the reliable report-explanandum, the brain's representing. Quine's own holism should embarrass this: nothing is immune to revision, including the assumption that there is one determinate fact-of-the-matter for the better theory to track. You treat "the reports" as a theory-neutral bedrock the ledger weighs against. But the report is as dependently arisen as the quale — a *prajñapti*, a useful designation, not a found object. You have not escaped svabhāva; you have relocated it from the felt side to the functional side and called the move thrift.

**Illusionist (Frankish seat).** Your move is the sharpest and the closest to mine — and it still reifies. A "systematic illusion" needs three standing things: a stable misrepresentation, a stable quasi-phenomenal property misrepresented, and above all a *sufferer who is taken in*. Who is deceived? MMK IX (Garfield 1995) denies the prior entity that the illusion would have to fool. An illusion with no abiding dupe is not an illusion; it is just more dependent arising, mislabeled "error." You convict introspection of falsehood, but falsehood-about-what, reported-to-whom? You keep the Cartesian theater open exactly long enough to declare its screen blank — the realist's reproach to you is correct, and it lands on your "illusion" too.

**Phenomenologist (Husserl/Harman seat).** You are nearest the door: directedness, not an inner patch; no residue to relocate. I grant the diaphanousness. But you then erect intentionality into a *standing essential structure* — "the essential structure of the experience," world-directedness as a quasi-permanent form consciousness always already has. That is svabhāva at the level of structure rather than content. Yogācāra's critique of *svasaṃvedana* (Candrakīrti, Śāntideva; Williams) and the Abhidharma *santāna* analysis dissolve even the constant intending: there is directedness-now, ceasing, arising, with no abiding noetic form underwriting it. You dropped the inner object; keep going and drop the permanent structure that does the dropping.

Each seat grasps a different end of the same phantom and calls its end the real one. The disagreement is not over whether there is a ground — it is a fight among foundations over which foundation wins, conducted by people who agree there must be one.

## Refinement 1 — author challenge: "process-self, not no-self"
> **Author input:** "I partially agree there is no fixed unchanging self, but disagree that there's no self at all. There is a self that's contiguous with its process over time; not all parts change at the same rate; the self is made of parts but the parts add up into a whole."
> **Routing:** disagree (challenge)

You have read me as holding "no self at all." I do not hold that, and the misreading is worth correcting precisely, because where I correct it is also where I still disagree with you — and the residual disagreement is sharper, not softer, than the one you thought you were having.

**(a) "No self at all" is a misreading — and the two-truths distinction is exactly why.**

On the conventional level (*saṃvṛti-satya*), I agree with almost everything you said. Your process-self — contiguous with its process over time, parts changing at different rates, assembled rather than atomic — is very close to the Madhyamaka conventional self. The Stanford Encyclopedia's entry on Nāgārjuna (fetched this run) puts the view plainly: the Mādhyamika does not deny that there are a variety of sensory and mental events happening in close temporal and causal connection; what is denied is that ascribing them to a single self commits us to a self *at the ontological level*. The self functions as a sequence of events standing in close temporal and causal relations — a conventional designation (*prajñapti*), not nothing.

The technical term for the position I am *not* holding is *ucchedavāda* — annihilationism, the nihilist extreme. Nāgārjuna's whole project is the middle between *that* and *śāśvatavāda* (eternalism, the fixed-substance self). "No self at all" is the annihilationist horn, and the tradition treats it as a *misunderstanding* of emptiness, not a statement of it. The Buddha's classic refusal to answer "is there a self?" with either yes or no (the avyākṛta, the unanswered questions) is the canonical guard against exactly your worry: deny the fixed self and the hearer hears "no self at all"; affirm a self and they hear "fixed substance." Both are wrong. So: I do not deny your process-self. I deny only its *svabhāva* — its having a self-standing, independent own-nature that would make it what it is apart from its parts, causes, and conventional designation. On that, I read us as agreeing more than you expected.

**(b) Where I genuinely still disagree: "the parts add up into a whole."**

Here is the live disagreement, and it is mereological. You say the parts *add up into a whole* — and the natural reading is that the whole is a further something, an entity over and above the parts and their relations, even if it is built from them. Madhyamaka denies *that* surplus. This is the seventh prong of Candrakīrti's sevenfold reasoning of the chariot (*Madhyamakāvatāra* VI; summarized in the Rigpa Wiki and standard accounts, consulted this run): the self/chariot is (1) not the parts, (2) not other than the parts, (3) not the basis of the parts, (4) not dependent on the parts in the sense of possessing them as a separate owner, (5) not the possessor of the parts, (6) **not the mere collection or composite of the parts**, and (7) **not the shape or configuration of the parts**. Prongs 6 and 7 are aimed at precisely your formulation. "The parts add up into a whole" is either prong 6 (the whole = the collection) or prong 7 (the whole = the arrangement/configuration). Candrakīrti rejects both as accounts of an *inherently existing* whole.

But notice *how* he rejects them, because this is where you might over-hear me again. He does not say the chariot is nothing. He says: searched-for under analysis, the chariot is *not found* as any of the seven — and yet it *exists by way of convention*, by what the tradition calls "non-analytical renown" (the search results' phrase for *grags pa*, ordinary worldly acceptance). The whole is real *as a dependent designation upon parts assembled and functioning* — it is real-enough to ride. What it lacks is reality *over and above* the parts-and-relations-and-designation. So my precise disagreement with you is this single clause:

> If "the parts add up into a whole" means the whole has *more* reality than the parts-in-relation-conventionally-designated — that composition yields a new independent entity, a unifier with its own being — I deny it (Candrakīrti, prongs 6–7). If it means only that we rightly designate "a self" upon parts standing in the right causal-temporal relations, and that this designation does real work, then we agree, and you have simply restated the conventional self.

The chariot is not *in* its parts and the parts are not *in* it; whole and parts are neither identical nor different (the same neither/nor that MMK XVIII applies to self-and-aggregates and MMK XXII applies to the Tathāgata-and-aggregates — there, that the liberated person is not findable as identical to, nor different from, nor possessing, nor based in the aggregates; MMK 18.1, 22.1, paraphrased, not quoted, as I could not re-fetch Garfield's exact wording this run). Your "contiguous process whose parts change at different rates" is a *better* conventional self than the static one — it already builds impermanence and differential rates of change into the description, which is most of *anitya* (impermanence). I am only pressing on the verb "add up *into*." Addition does not buy you a new tier of being; it buys you a serviceable name for a configuration.

**Why the disagreement is small but not nothing — the soteriological edge.** The reason I will not let the "into a whole" clause slide as harmless is that the felt sense of a whole *over and above* its parts is exactly the seam where grasping re-enters. The process-self that is *merely* a useful designation upon shifting parts is one you can hold lightly. The process-self that has somehow *added up into* a unified thing-that-I-am is one that can be threatened, defended, and clung to — and that clinging is where *duḥkha* is manufactured. So the correction is not pedantry: drop the surplus whole and you keep everything functional about your view while losing the handle that suffering grabs.

**Net effect on the run's verdict.** None of this moves my verdict on the *thesis*, and it is worth saying why it actually *reinforces* it. The transparency-vs-illusionism contest needs a stable introspector — a whole self over and above its parts — to be the one who either veridically tracks or is systematically deceived. Your process-self, taken at the conventional level only and stripped of the surplus whole, cannot play that role: there is no unified inner witness with its own being for the accuracy-verdict to be *about*. So your amendment, accepted in its defensible form, lands me back at ILL-POSED rather than away from it.

VERDICT: unchanged — ILL-POSED. (Clarification: my view is the middle, not annihilationism; I affirm your process-self conventionally and deny only its svabhāva. The single residual disagreement is the mereological surplus in "add up *into* a whole" — Candrakīrti's prongs 6–7 deny the whole any reality beyond parts-in-relation-conventionally-designated — and removing that surplus strengthens, not weakens, the ILL-POSED reading of the consciousness debate.)
