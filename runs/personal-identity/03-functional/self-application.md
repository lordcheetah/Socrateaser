SELF-APPLICATION: N/A — thesis is not self-referential

## The test

The thesis is a metaphysical criterion: personal identity over time holds because,
and only because, two person-stages are linked by overlapping chains of
psychological continuity (memory, intention, character). Applied to itself, the
question is whether this criterion ranges over its own content — whether asserting
it generates a contradiction of the kinds that sink "all truths are relative" or
verificationism.

**Step 1 — Direct application.** Is the thesis a claim about all criteria of
identity, including itself? No. It is a first-order criterion about what makes
person-stages numerically identical. It does not claim to be the criterion for
criteria, nor does it say anything about what makes propositions true, meaningful,
or assertible. There is no direct loop.

**Step 2 — The reflexive angle the prompt identifies.** The theorist who holds the
psychological-continuity criterion at T2 must themselves persist from T1 (when
they first grasped it) to T2 (when they assert it). By the thesis's own account,
that persistence holds in virtue of psychological continuity — specifically, memory
of having reasoned through the criterion, the retained intention to assert it, the
continuing character of the theorist as a philosophical reasoner. Applying the
criterion to its own asserter yields: the theorist persists via the very mechanism
the thesis theorizes. This is not a contradiction. It is simply the thesis
applying normally to a case that happens to be the asserter.

**Step 3 — Check for failure types.**

- *Self-refutation:* The thesis does not say "psychological continuity is
  impossible" or "memory cannot constitute identity." Applying it to the asserter
  confirms rather than contradicts it.
- *Self-exemption:* The thesis makes no claim that exempts itself from its own
  scope. Asserting it does not require a non-psychological form of persistence.
- *Pragmatic self-defeat:* Believing the thesis does not remove the grounds for
  believing it. The thesis does not undermine psychological continuity — it
  valorizes it. A theorist who holds it is, on the thesis's own account, the same
  person who worked it out, provided continuity holds.
- *Performative contradiction:* No presupposition of asserting the thesis
  contradicts it.

**Step 4 — The one genuine reflexive cost (not a refutation).** There is a
second-order epistemological note worth recording, though it is not a logical
self-refutation: if memory is partially constitutive of the identity of the
theorist who holds the criterion, then the theorist's memory-based access to the
reasoning that generated the criterion cannot serve as fully independent evidence
for the criterion. The evidential base (memory) and the explanandum (what memory
constitutes) are not cleanly separable. This is a real epistemological circularity
— not unlike the circularity that attends any attempt to justify perception using
perceptual reports — but it is a feature of the criterion's *justification*, not
its logical *content*. It does not make the thesis self-refuting; it makes the
thesis's epistemic grounding less than fully external, which is a concern for
another role (hidden-premise-excavator) rather than for self-application.

## Result

N/A. The thesis makes no claim that ranges over itself. It is a first-order
metaphysical criterion, not a claim about all claims or all criteria. The reflexive
angle (the asserter's own persistence depends on psychological continuity) applies
the thesis to a case that happens to be the asserter, but the result is
confirmation, not contradiction. None of the four failure types — self-refutation,
self-exemption, pragmatic self-defeat, performative contradiction — is triggered.

## If it fails: can it be repaired?

Not applicable. The thesis survives as N/A, not as a failure requiring repair.
The epistemological circularity noted in Step 4 is real but is not a
self-application failure; it belongs to a different diagnostic role.
