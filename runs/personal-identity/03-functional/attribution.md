### 1. Naturalist — Olson's three-premise thinking-animal argument

- **Voice → who:** Naturalist attributed to Eric Olson, "An Argument for Animalism"
- **The claim:** Three-premise argument: (P1) a human animal is presently sitting in your chair; (P2) that animal is thinking; (P3) you are the thinking being in your chair; therefore you are the animal. Named "too many thinkers" as the problem forced on those who reject P3.
- **What the source says:** SEP *Animalism* (Fall 2025) states the argument in precisely those three premises with near-identical wording: "Presently sitting in your chair is a human animal / The human animal sitting in your chair is thinking / You are the thinking being sitting in your chair." It also confirms the "too many thinkers problem" label (attributed to Parfit 2012:7 in the SEP). The Olson PhilPapers record links to the paper.
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE
- **Note:** None.

The White Rose eprints PDF of the paper was not machine-readable; the argument is attested in every secondary source checked.
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE
- **Note:** Primary text of "An Argument for Animalism" was not machine-readable at the White Rose eprint URL; accuracy rests on consistent secondary attestation plus SEP. Core position is not in doubt.

### 6. Rationalist — Kant, Critique of Pure Reason B131, unity of apperception

- **Voice → who:** Rationalist quoted Kant: "The *I think* must be able to accompany all my representations" from B131, described as the "original synthetic unity of apperception."
- **What the source says:** Multiple sources confirm the exact wording. The IEP *Kant's Philosophy of Mind* and a web search on B131 return: "The I think must be able to accompany all my representations; for otherwise something would be represented in me that could not be thought at all." This is the standard translation and is confirmed at B131. The rationalist's further reading — that this furnishes the identity of the self over time as a psychological-rational unity — is an interpretive extension, but the quotation itself and its locus are accurate.
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE
- **Note:** The rationalist treats the apperception passage as yielding a persistence condition over time. This is a contested interpretive move (Kant's point is transcendental, not about psychological persistence in the empirical sense), but it is a recognizable reading of Kant and the attribution of the quoted passage to B131 is correct.

## Summary of issues

**Issue 1 (minor — source inaccessibility):** Olson's "An Argument for Animalism" was not machine-readable at the White Rose eprint URL. The fetus argument and thinking-animal argument attributions are accurate by strong secondary attestation (SEP, multiple scholarship sources), but the primary-text PDF could not be rendered to check exact wording. No fabrication is suspected; this is a retrieval limitation.

**Issue 2 (moderate — unverifiable section numbers):** The specialist-parfitian's section number citations for *Reasons and Persons* (§78–§81, §87, §89–§90, §95) could not be verified against the primary text or any machine-readable source listing the section numbers. The content attributed to each section is substantively correct, and at least one secondary source independently confirms §90 for the branching argument. The "double success" quote and its argumentative context are confirmed genuine. The section numbers are plausible but UNVERIFIABLE from available open-web sources.

**Issue 3 (minor — interpretive extension):** The rationalist reads Kant's B131 unity of apperception as providing a *persistence condition over time* for the self. Kant's original point is a synchronic condition on the unity of experience in a single subject, not a diachronic persistence condition. The rationalist's application is a recognizable Kantian-inspired argument in the personal identity literature, but it runs beyond what B131 itself states. The quotation is accurate; the inference drawn from it is an interpretive extension that Kant scholars dispute.
