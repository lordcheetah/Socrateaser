## The question, plainly

Are you the same person you were at five years old because of an unbroken chain of
memories, intentions, and character traits, rather than because you're the same
living body? The thesis says yes: psychology is what makes you *you* over time, and
biology has nothing to do with it.

## Why it's not obvious

The psychology side has a strong pull. Picture a brain transplant: your brain goes
into a new body. Almost everyone's gut says *you* went where your brain went, where
your memories and plans and personality went, not where the old leftover body
stayed. The mere meat doesn't seem to be the thing we care about. When we worry
about surviving, we're worrying about whether our inner life continues, not whether
some particular cells keep metabolizing.

But the other side has a fact the psychology view has to swallow. You were once an
early fetus with no mind at all, no memories, no intentions, nothing psychological
going on. That fetus didn't die when consciousness switched on; it grew into you.
So if you and that fetus are one and the same thing, then something persisted across
that stretch with zero psychological continuity, which makes psychology look like it
*can't* be the whole story. There's a sharper version too. Right now there's a human
animal sitting in your chair, breathing, digesting, thinking. If "the person" were a
separate thing from "the animal," defined by psychology while the animal is defined
by biology, you'd have two thinkers competing for the same thoughts. One is plenty.

So it's not a contest between a smart view and a silly one. Both sides are holding a
case the other has to explain away.

## Where it really turns

It all comes down to whether you think personal survival is even the kind of thing
that has to be *strict identity* in the first place.

Here's what that piece of jargon means. "Numerical identity" is the relation a thing
has only to itself: one thing, not two, and it's all-or-nothing. You either are or
aren't the very same entity as that fetus; there's no being 80% the same person.
The thesis assumes that *this* relation, this one-to-one, yes-or-no fact, is the
prize everyone is fighting over, and it claims psychology is what fills the role.

Now the case that breaks the assumption open. Imagine a teleporter that scans you,
destroys the original, and builds an exact copy on Mars with all your memories
intact. Did you survive? Push it harder: imagine the machine builds *two* copies,
on Mars and Venus, each with your full psychology. Philosophers call this **fission**
(splitting). Both copies remember your childhood, intend to finish your projects,
have your temperament. The psychological link, what Parfit named **Relation R**, the
overlapping chains of memory, intention, and character, holds perfectly between you
and *each* copy. But strict identity can't work that way. You can't be numerically
identical to two different people, because then they'd have to be identical to each
other, and they're plainly not, they're on different planets having different lunches.

That's the hinge. Relation R, the thing we actually care about when we care about
surviving, can run one-to-many. Strict identity can only run one-to-one. So they
can't be the same thing. Which forces the question the whole dispute turns on: when
you signed up to explain "personal identity," did you sign up to explain the strict
one-to-one fact, or did you sign up to explain what matters in survival? If those
two come apart in the fission case, you have to pick which one the thesis was ever
about, and the thesis stands or falls on that pick.

## Where serious people land

A few different camps, and what each one asks you to accept.

The **psychological-identity defender** (the Lockean view, after John Locke, who said
a person is something that "can consider itself as itself") says: yes, identity is
strict, and psychology fills the role. The person and the animal are two distinct
things sharing one skull. What it asks you to accept: that there really are two
things sitting in your chair, a person and an animal, not one.

The **animalist** (Eric Olson, Peter van Inwagen) says: identity is strict, fine, but
*biology* fills the role, because you simply *are* the human animal. That's why the
fetus was you. What it asks you to accept: that there's exactly one thinker here, the
organism, and the "person" as a separate object is a fiction we talk ourselves into
from the inside.

The **Parfitian** (after Derek Parfit) accepts that psychology, Relation R, is what
matters, but uses fission to conclude that R can't be identity, so "personal identity
is not what matters." What it asks you to accept: that there's no deep further fact
about whether you survived as *this one person*, and in branching cases the identity
question just has no answer.

The **soul theorist** (Richard Swinburne) says neither psychology nor biology
constitutes you; both are just evidence. The duplication that wrecks the body wrecks
the memory-chain too, since both can be copied, so what's left must be a
non-copyable immaterial soul. What it asks you to accept: that there's a further,
unanalyzable fact about which soul is which, one we can never actually check.

And the **phenomenologist** refuses the whole setup. The lived self is given as a
single flowing stream, not as a stack of time-slices waiting to be glued back
together; asking "what relation re-binds the slices" mangles the thing it claims to
describe. What it asks you to accept: that how the self shows up in experience gets
to overrule how metaphysics carves it.

## The honest bottom line

This one doesn't get settled by discovering a fact, and it doesn't get settled by a
knock-down argument either. Every camp agrees on every physical detail about fetuses,
brains, and teleporter duplicates. What they disagree about is which relation gets to
*count* as "what we are," and whether the prize itself, strict one-to-one identity,
was ever the right prize.

The fission case is where it gets decided, and fission doesn't hand down a verdict.
It hands you a forced choice with no free option, where each camp pays a price it was
already willing to pay:

- Keep psychology as sufficient, drop strict one-to-one identity, and you land on
  "identity isn't what matters" (the Parfitian).
- Keep strict identity, drop psychology as sufficient, and biology decides which
  animal is which (the animalist).
- Keep both, and relocate yourself into something that can't be duplicated: a soul
  (Swinburne).
- Or deny the case even makes sense, because the stream was never sliced to begin
  with (the phenomenologist).

No fact in the dispute and no argument inside it can force the choice among those,
because the choice just *is* deciding what "personal identity" is going to mean. So
here's the plain version of where it bottoms out: grant that strict numerical
identity is the thing to be explained, and you get a real, fact-constrained fight
among the Lockean, the animalist, and the soul theorist, one that could in principle
have a right answer. Decline to grant it, and the thesis was malformed from the
start, true in spirit (psychology, not biology, is what we care about) and false in
its letter (the claim that psychology is what makes you the strictly *same* thing).

Which is the genuinely uncomfortable result. You don't get told who won. You get
shown that the answer depends on a decision about words that no evidence can make for
you, and that's worth more than a tidy verdict that papers over exactly the spot
where thoughtful people actually part ways.
