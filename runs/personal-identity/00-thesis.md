# Personal Identity Over Time — Thesis & framing
> **Run:** personal-identity · **Mode:** light · **Stage:** Frame
> **Thesis:** A person persists over time because, and only because, of psychological continuity (overlapping memory, intention, character) — not biological continuity.

---

## Thesis
A person at a later time is the same person as someone earlier because, and only because, the two are linked by continuity of psychology (overlapping chains of memory, intention, and character), not by continuity of the same biological organism.

## Classification
- **Type:** metaphysical (with a conceptual sub-question that the dispute keeps trying to collapse into)
- **Why this type:** The question is about numerical identity over time — what relation must hold between an earlier and a later thing for them to be *one and the same* entity. That is a claim about persistence conditions, which is a metaphysical matter, not an empirical or normative one. No survey of facts settles it: both sides agree on every physical fact about a fetus, an adult, and a brain transplant case, yet disagree about which thing survives which event. There is a conceptual layer underneath — whether "person" and "human animal" pick out the same thing or two coincident things — and the most powerful objection to the thesis (Olson's "thinking animal") turns on that conceptual move. But the thesis itself stakes a metaphysical claim about what *we* essentially are and what it takes for us to persist, so it is metaphysical first. Naming this matters: the council must not be allowed to "refute" the thesis by citing neuroscience (an empirical move that answers a different question) or by appealing to what would be *good* for us to count as survival (a normative move). The argument is settled by analysis of the identity relation and by which intuitions about cases we are willing to give up.

## Key terms to pin
- **person:** Used here in the Lockean sense — a thinking, self-conscious thing whose persistence is fixed by psychological relations. The rival sense (animalism) holds that "person" is a phase sortal or functional kind, while the thing that *we fundamentally are* is a human animal whose persistence is biological; on that reading the thesis is a category error, because it answers "what persists?" with a property (personhood) rather than the underlying substance.
- **psychological continuity:** Overlapping chains of direct psychological connections (memory, intention, belief, character), in Parfit's sense — *not* requiring the persistence of a single substrate, and explicitly allowing that the chain could in principle be carried by a new brain or a duplicate. The rival sense would smuggle in *same-brain* continuity, which quietly concedes a physical criterion and collapses the dispute.
- **continuity of the biological organism:** The persistence of one living human animal — the same self-maintaining metabolic process. The rival sense narrows "organism" to "brain," which is a third position (the brain criterion / somatic-vs-cerebral split) and would change the dispute by making the contest one of *which organ* rather than *psychology vs. biology*.
- **"because, and only because":** The thesis claims psychological continuity is both necessary and sufficient. This is the load-bearing modal commitment. A weaker thesis ("psychology usually tracks identity") is not contestable in the same way and must not be substituted in.

## Is it contestable?
Strongly. The thesis is denied by **animalism** — Eric Olson and Peter van Inwagen most prominently. Olson's "thinking animal" / fetus argument is a direct refutation attempt: I was once an early fetus with no psychology, and that fetus did not cease to exist when a mind switched on, so psychological continuity cannot be necessary for my persistence; and the human animal sitting here thinks all my thoughts, so if a *person* defined by psychology were a distinct coincident thing, there would be two thinkers ("too many thinkers"). Bernard Williams's "future great pain" cases pressure the psychological criterion from the intuitive side. The thesis is also denied "from below" by **biological/physical reductionists** about the body and by **soul/substance dualists** (the thing that persists is an immaterial substance, not a psychological chain). It is even partly denied by Parfit himself, whose work the psychological view leans on: he concluded that *identity is not what matters*, which threatens the "only because" clause from a third direction. This is a live, century-spanning dispute with no consensus winner. It is not a bad seed.

## Pseudo-dispute check
The disagreement does not dissolve under a distinction — it is *generated* by one, and survives it. The tempting dissolution is: "psychology and biology normally travel together, so the dispute is idle." But the whole tradition is built on cases that pry them apart — brain transplants, teleporters, fission, the early fetus, the persistent vegetative state, gradual replacement. In every such case the two criteria deliver *opposite verdicts about who exists*, so there is a real fact in dispute, not two words for one phenomenon.

There is one genuine equivocation the council must police, and surfacing it is part of framing rather than pre-solving: the word "person" itself. If "person" means *self-conscious thinker* and "human being" means *animal*, an animalist can grant that personhood tracks psychology (a person-stage is psychologically individuated) while denying that *we* are essentially persons — we are animals that are persons for a phase. On that reading psychologist and animalist are answering two different questions ("what makes a person-stage the same person?" vs. "what makes *one of us* persist?") and the fight is partly verbal. But it does not fully dissolve, because the thesis explicitly claims that *what we fundamentally are* is psychologically individuated — it stakes the strong reading. So the real, non-verbal residue is: **are we essentially persons (psychological things) or essentially animals (biological things)?** That is a substantive metaphysical disagreement about our fundamental kind, and the distinction-watchdog should be told to keep "person-stage identity" from being passed off as a win for the strong thesis.

## Suggested specialists to summon
- An **Olson-style animalist** — defends that we are essentially human animals and that psychological continuity is neither necessary nor sufficient (the thinking-animal and fetus arguments). This is the thesis's sharpest named opponent and the council needs it explicitly.
- A **Parfitian reductionist** — accepts psychological continuity as what matters in survival but denies the "only because identity" framing, arguing that identity itself is not what matters and that fission shows the relation can hold one-to-many. This pressures the thesis from *inside* its own tradition, which is more interesting than pure opposition.
- (Optional) A **substance/soul theorist** (Swinburne-style) — denies both psychological and biological criteria in favor of a persisting immaterial self; worth summoning only if the council needs the third corner of the verdict space the fixed seats don't already cover.
