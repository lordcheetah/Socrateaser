## Standard form

**The thesis has two directions because "because, and only because" asserts both necessity and sufficiency. They are reconstructed separately, then joined.**

---

### Direction 1 — Sufficiency: psychological continuity is enough for identity

P1. Personal identity over time consists in whatever relation must hold between an earlier person-stage and a later person-stage for them to be stages of one and the same person.

P2. Two person-stages are one and the same person if and only if the relation that constitutes personal identity holds between them.

P3. Overlapping chains of direct psychological connections — memory, intention, belief, and character — are sufficient to constitute the identity-making relation between two person-stages.

P4. No additional condition (in particular, continuity of the same biological organism) is required beyond P3.

C1. Therefore: psychological continuity alone is sufficient for personal identity over time.

---

### Direction 2 — Necessity: psychological continuity is required for identity

P5. A person-stage at time T2 is the same person as a person-stage at T1 only if the identity-making relation holds between them.

P6. Biological-organism continuity — the persistence of the same self-maintaining metabolic process — is not itself sufficient to constitute the identity-making relation (i.e., a body can persist while the person does not, and a person can persist without the same body).

P7. The only relation that is sufficient to constitute the identity-making relation is psychological continuity as defined in P3.

C2. Therefore: psychological continuity is necessary for personal identity over time.

---

### Conjunction — the thesis

From C1 and C2:

C.  A person at a later time is the same person as someone earlier because, and only because, the two are linked by psychological continuity; not by continuity of the same biological organism.

---

## Validity

**C1 (sufficiency direction)** follows from P1 through P4 by a simple substitution of identicals: if personal identity consists in the identity-making relation (P1–P2), and if psychological continuity meets that relation without remainder (P3–P4), then psychological continuity is sufficient. The inference is valid.

**C2 (necessity direction)** follows from P5 through P7 by elimination: if identity requires the identity-making relation (P5), and if biology alone does not constitute it (P6), and if psychology is the only candidate that does (P7), then psychology is necessary. The inference structure is valid — it is an eliminative argument.

**However, P7 is not derived; it is asserted.** The argument does not show that psychology is the only candidate; it rules out one rival (biology) and asserts uniqueness. This conceals a suppressed premise:

> SP. For any proposed identity-making relation R, if R is not psychological continuity and not biological-organism continuity, then R is not a genuine candidate. (This excludes soul/substance criteria, brain-based somatic criteria, and four-dimensionalist stage-theoretic reformulations without argument.)

The argument is therefore *valid given SP*, but SP is doing work that is not surfaced. Flag for hidden-premise-excavator.

**The conjunction** (the full thesis C) is valid given C1 and C2 together; no additional inference is needed.

---

## Where the weight sits

**P4 carries the sufficiency direction** and **P7 (plus SP) carries the necessity direction.** But they are asymmetric in vulnerability:

P4 — that no biological condition is *required in addition* to psychology — is the premise the fission case attacks most directly. In a fission scenario (a person's brain is split; each half is transplanted into a separate body), each resulting person-stage stands in psychological continuity with the original. P4 says that is sufficient for identity. But both resulting stages cannot be numerically identical to the original and to each other (identity is transitive and one-to-one). So either psychology is sufficient but the original person dies (Parfit's move), or sufficiency fails (the animalist's move). P4 does not survive the fission case unmodified.

P6 — that biological continuity is not sufficient — is the premise the fetus/early-embryo argument attacks. If I was once an early fetus with no psychological states, and the fetus did not cease to exist when psychology switched on, then psychological continuity cannot be *necessary* (Direction 2 fails). This also pressures P3's necessity role indirectly.

**The single sentence that carries the most weight across both directions is P3/P4 together: that psychological continuity, and nothing additional, constitutes the identity-making relation.** If an opponent can show one case where psychological continuity holds and identity does not (fission undermines sufficiency) or one case where identity holds and psychological continuity does not (the early fetus undermines necessity), the thesis in its "because, and only because" form collapses. The biconditional is maximally exposed to counterexample — which is what makes the thesis genuinely contestable.

---

## Charitable variants

### Variant A — Weakened sufficiency (Parfit's own retreat)
The most obvious reconstruction of P4 is too strong: psychological continuity is sufficient for *what matters* in survival, not for numerical identity. Under this reading the thesis becomes:

> Psychological continuity is the relation that matters for prudential concern, anticipation, and practical planning — regardless of whether it constitutes strict numerical identity.

This escapes the fission objection (both branches matter; neither is "the same person," but that is fine because identity was never what mattered). It also escapes Olson's thinking-animal objection. But it changes the thesis: "what matters" is not the same as "numerical identity," so this variant concedes the framer's strong claim while preserving its practical import. It is a genuine variant, not a rescue of the same thesis.

### Variant B — Psychological continuity as constitutive within a kind
Rather than claiming psychology is the only criterion, the argument could be restricted:

> For things of the kind *person* (as opposed to things of the kind *human animal*), psychological continuity is the constitutive identity criterion.

This is the neo-Lockean move: persons and human animals are distinct coincident kinds with distinct persistence conditions. The thesis is then not in competition with animalism about what biological organisms require; it makes a kind-relative claim. This sidesteps the thinking-animal objection by accepting that there are two coincident thinkers (the person and the animal), at the cost of accepting a controversial coincidence ontology. The variant is stronger against Olson but weaker against van Inwagen, who denies coincident entities.

### Variant C — Psychological continuity as defeasible default
The "because, and only because" formulation could be read as a ceteris-paribus law rather than a strict biconditional:

> Under normal conditions, psychological continuity is both necessary and sufficient; in bizarre edge cases (fission, teleportation, upload) the concept of personal identity breaks down rather than delivering a determinate verdict.

This is closest to Johnston's or Shoemaker's positions. It preserves the thesis's practical force while treating fission as a concept-exhausting case rather than a counterexample. The cost is that the "only because" clause loses its modal force and the thesis becomes difficult to falsify.

**Strongest reconstruction overall:** Variant B — the kind-relative reading — is the most defensible formulation that still asserts something genuinely controversial. It makes the biconditional precise (it applies within a kind), grounds it in a Lockean sortal ontology, and is falsifiable by the thinking-animal argument rather than trivially collapsible by fission. The council should attack this version, not the naive reading.
