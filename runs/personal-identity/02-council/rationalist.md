VERDICT: TRUE — the self is essentially a self-conscious rational unity whose persistence is constituted by psychological continuity, while the human animal that biologically persists is a distinct, coincident thing; psychology is therefore necessary and sufficient for *our* identity, strictly numerically construed.

## Case

I defend the thesis in its kind-relative form (Variant B): for things of the kind *person* — and we are essentially persons — psychological continuity is the constitutive criterion of strict numerical identity. The human animal is a coincident, biologically-persisting thing of a *different* kind. This is not the Parfitian retreat. I am not saying "identity is not what matters." I am saying psychological continuity *is* identity, for us.

**1. The concept "person" already builds in self-conscious rational unity.** Locke fixed the term that the whole dispute inherits: a person is "a thinking, intelligent being, that has reason and reflection, and can consider itself as itself, the same thinking thing in different times and places" (*Essay* II.xxvii.9). The crucial words are *consider itself as itself*. A person is not merely a thing that thinks; it is a thing that represents *itself* as the subject of its thoughts across time. Locke's further claim — that "person" is a *forensic* term, a term of accountability — is not a side remark but a structural one: only a being that can own its past as *its own* can be praised, blamed, or held to a promise. What does that owning if not consciousness? "As far as this consciousness can be extended backwards to any past action or thought, so far reaches the identity of that person." The persistence condition falls directly out of what the thing *is*.

**2. The transcendental argument: a self just is the unity of apperception.** Kant gives the deepest version of why the self is not a biological substance among others. "The *I think* must be able to accompany all my representations" (*Critique of Pure Reason*, B131). This is the original synthetic unity of apperception — the condition under which any manifold of experience can be *mine* at all. The self, for Kant, is not an object encountered in experience but the formal unity that makes a unified experience possible. Now run this transcendentally: the identity of *this* subject over time is nothing other than the standing possibility that these earlier and later representations belong to one "I think." That is a *psychological-rational* unity, not a metabolic one. The animalist asks for the biological mechanism that grounds my persistence; this misunderstands the kind of necessity at issue. The unity of the self is a condition on the possibility of there being a thinker who could even pose the identity question — it is presupposed by inquiry, not discovered within it.

**3. Shoemaker closes the loop materialistically — no dualism required.** One need not be a substance dualist to hold this. Shoemaker's functionalism *entails* the psychological criterion: it belongs to the nature of a mental state that it have its characteristic causes and effects *in the being that is in that state*. Mental states are individuated by their causal-functional roles, and those roles trace psychological continuity. So whatever genuinely thinks must have *psychological* persistence conditions. The thinking thing — the person — persists by psychological continuity as a matter of what it is to be a bearer of functionally-individuated states. The biological animal, by contrast, has *biological* persistence conditions. These are simply two different kinds with two different criteria, coincident in the same matter.

**4. This dissolves the "too many thinkers" objection rather than fleeing it.** The animalist (Olson) presses: the animal thinks all your thoughts, so if the person were a distinct thing there would be two thinkers. The neo-Lockean answer, following Shoemaker, is that *strictly* only the person thinks — because thinking requires psychological persistence conditions, which the animal *qua* animal lacks. The animal "thinks" only derivatively, by constituting the thinker, as a statue "weighs" what its bronze weighs. There is one episode of thinking, two coincident things standing in different relations to it. Coincidence ontology has a cost, but it is a *theoretical* cost — paid in metaphysical inventory — not a refutation.

**5. Why necessity holds (the fetus objection answered).** Olson says: I was once an early fetus with no psychology; the fetus did not cease to exist when the mind switched on; so psychology is not necessary for *my* persistence. The kind-relative reply: strictly, the early fetus is not numerically identical to *me, the person*. The person came into existence when the rational, self-conscious unity did. The organism that was the fetus persists and now coincides with me. "I was once a fetus" is true in the loose, organism-tracking sense in which "this statue was once a lump" is true — not in the strict sense that fixes *personal* identity. To insist that the fetus-sense is the only legitimate sense of "I" is simply to assume animalism, which is the point in dispute.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P6/P7 as the animalist reads them** — that biological-organism continuity could be the identity-making relation *for us*. The rationalist reason: identity conditions follow from a thing's *kind*, and our kind is fixed by what we essentially are, which is given a priori by the concept of a self-conscious rational subject, not read off the biology. The animalist's move — "the thinking thing is the animal, and animals persist biologically" — smuggles in a substance metaphysics that the transcendental analysis of the self forecloses: it treats the "I" as one more object in nature with natural persistence conditions, when the "I" is the formal condition under which objects are given at all (Kant, B131–139). You cannot derive the persistence conditions of the *subject* of experience from the persistence conditions of an *object* of experience without category error.

I also hold firm against the Parfitian seat: I deny that fission shows "identity is not what matters." Fission is a case where the *person*-kind's identity conditions are not satisfied (one-to-one fails), so strictly the original person does not survive — full stop. That is a determinate verdict, not a concession that identity is unimportant. The biconditional survives because in non-branching cases — the only cases where a unique successor stands in the apperceptive unity — psychological continuity is exactly necessary and sufficient.
