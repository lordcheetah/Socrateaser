VERDICT: FALSE — even if we grant the thesis its whole framework (that *something* persists and there is a unique identity-maker), psychological continuity is the *worst* candidate for the role, not the privileged one, because memory and character are the most rapidly arising-and-ceasing, most clung-to contents of experience; P7 inverts the actual order of impermanence.

## Case

I am assigned P7 — the eliminative claim that *the only* relation sufficient to constitute the identity-making relation is psychological continuity (memory, intention, belief, character). I attack it on its own ground. Suppose, for argument, that the thesis is right that *something* persists and that there is a single relation that makes it persist. Even granting all of that, psychology is the last place to locate it. The contemplative tradition I speak from spent two and a half millennia looking exactly where the thesis points — at the mental stream — and reported the opposite finding: that stream is the most volatile, least stable, most deceptive thing on offer.

**1. The canonical text says the mind is a *worse* identity-bearer than the body.** In the Assutavā Sutta (Saṃyutta Nikāya 12.61), the Buddha makes a comparison that lands directly on P7. In Ṭhānissaro Bhikkhu's translation, fetched this run, the body "composed of the four great elements is seen standing for a year, two years, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, a hundred years or more. But what's called 'mind,' 'intellect,' or 'consciousness' by day and by night arises as one thing and ceases as another." And then the striking line: "It would be better for the uninstructed run-of-the-mill person to hold to the body composed of the four great elements, rather than the mind, as the self. Why is that? Because this body... is seen standing for a year..." Note what this is *not*: it is not the Buddha endorsing the body as the self either (both are not-self). It is a *ranking of candidates*. And on that ranking, the psychological criterion is strictly inferior to the biological one. The thesis's P7 chooses precisely the candidate the tradition singles out as the most fleeting. P6 may be true that biology is not the identity-maker — but it does not follow, and the Assutavā Sutta explicitly denies, that *therefore* psychology is the better candidate. Psychology is the worse one.

**2. Memory, intention, character are the clung-to aggregates — flux mistaken for ground.** The thesis builds identity out of memory, intention, belief, and character. In the analysis of the five aggregates (khandhā), these are exactly *vedanā* (feeling), *saññā* (perception/recognition, which underwrites memory), *saṅkhārā* (volitional formations — intention and character-dispositions), and *viññāṇa* (consciousness). The Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta (SN 22.59) takes each in turn and concludes that each must be regarded — in Ñāṇamoli Thera's rendering — as "This is not mine, this is not I, this is not my self," precisely *because* each is impermanent (anicca) and therefore unfit to be a self. The thesis takes the four contents the tradition names as the paradigm of impermanence and clinging, and nominates *them* as what makes me one and the same across time. That is not a neutral metaphysical choice; from the contemplative side it is the signature error — *upādāna*, grasping the flux as a ground. The more volatile the content, the more we cling to it, and clinging is what manufactures the felt sense of a continuous "me."

**3. The chariot cuts against P7 as hard as against the body.** The Milindapañha's chariot dialogue (Nāgasena to King Milinda) is usually read as deflating the *bodily* self: no "chariot" is found over and above pole, axle, wheels, frame. But the same analysis applies without remainder to the psychological chain. "Memory," "character," "intention" are designations (paññatti) for assembled, dependently-arisen parts; no continuous psychological *substance* underwrites the chain any more than a continuous chariot-substance underwrites the parts. So if the chariot argument refutes the biological self, it equally refutes the psychological self. The thesis cannot borrow the deflationary force of "no substance underlies the body" while exempting "memory and character" from the same solvent. There is no more a self to be found in the overlapping memory-chain than in the metabolizing organism.

**4. The reconstruction's own pressure points confirm the diagnosis.** The reconstructor flags that fission breaks sufficiency and the early fetus breaks necessity. Both failures have the same root, and the contemplative tradition predicts it: psychological continuity is a *relation among momentary events*, not the persistence of a thing, so it splits (fission), starts late (the fetus had no psychology), and admits of degrees — exactly the behavior of an impermanent, composite, dependently-originated process rather than an identity-maker. Nāgārjuna's point in the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (the chapter examining the self and the aggregates, MMK 18) is that a self is neither identical with the aggregates nor different from them; a fortiori it is not to be located in the most labile subset of them. The thesis errs not by being too austere but by being insufficiently austere: it dissolves the substantial body-self and then re-inflates a substantial psyche-self in its place.

So: deny P7. The eliminative move "not biology, therefore psychology" fails twice. It fails as an inference (P6 does not yield P7). And it fails on the merits, because of all the candidates surveyed, memory-and-character is the one the tradition identifies as *most* impermanent and *most* clung-to — the worst possible identity-bearer.

## The shared assumption I reject

The thesis, and the whole dispute as framed, assumes there is a fact of the matter about *the* relation that makes a later person numerically one-and-the-same as an earlier one, and that the contest is merely over *which* relation fills that single role (psychology vs. biology vs. brain vs. soul). I reject the premise that the role is occupied at all. On the two-truths distinction: *conventionally*, persons persist, promises bind across time, the woman who borrowed the book is the one who owes it back, and ordinary psychological continuity does real work — this is true and not to be denied. *Ultimately*, there is no svabhāva, no self-existent identity-relation, doing the underwriting; "person" is a serviceable designation (paññatti) for a dependently-arisen, continually-replaced stream, and the demand for a unique strict identity-maker is the demand for a ground that was never there. The thesis is false in its strong "because, and *only because*" form not because some rival criterion wins, but because the biconditional asks the conventional stream to behave like an ultimate essence. My specific assignment is narrower and I commit to it on its own terms: even *inside* the foundationalist game the thesis is playing, psychology loses, because it bets identity on the most impermanent contents there are.
