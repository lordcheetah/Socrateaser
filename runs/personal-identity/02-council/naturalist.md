VERDICT: FALSE — psychological continuity is neither necessary nor sufficient for our persistence, because what we fundamentally are is a human animal whose identity is fixed biologically, and the psychological "person" is at best one thinker too many.

## Case

I am assigned the animalist line, and I hold it without hedging: the thesis is false because it mistakes a property we have for a phase (being a self-conscious person) for the thing we fundamentally are (a living human animal). The naturalist demand here is simple — point to the actual object in the world that persists, and give its persistence conditions. There is exactly one biological organism sitting in this chair, born of a pregnancy, metabolizing, and one day dying. The thesis asks me to believe that the entity that *I* am is something else: a chain of memories and intentions that merely rides on that organism and could in principle leave it. That is one entity too many, and the world contains no causal slot for it.

**The fetus argument (against P6's necessity role and P7).** Olson's central observation is that each of us was once a fetus, and the early fetus had no psychology at all — no memory, no intention, no character, nothing for a "chain of direct psychological connections" (the reconstruction's P3) to consist of. Yet that fetus did not perish and get replaced by a numerically distinct thing when a mind switched on; it grew a mind. As Olson puts it, our having started out as a fetus is *incompatible* with the psychological-continuity approach: the persistence condition for beings like us is life, not psychology ([Olson, "An Argument for Animalism"](https://philpapers.org/rec/OLSAAF); [SEP, *Animalism*](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2025/entries/animalism/)). The inference is clean and it kills the necessity direction (C2) directly:

1. I was once an early fetus (I point to ultrasound photos and call them "me," and I mean it literally, not metaphorically).
2. The early fetus had no psychological continuity with my present states.
3. So there is a stage in my history not linked to my present by psychological continuity.
4. Therefore psychological continuity is *not necessary* for my persisting from then to now.

This is exactly the move the reconstruction itself flags as the weight-bearing attack on P6 (line 63) and the suppressed-necessity role of P7. The psychological theorist's only escapes are bad: deny that the fetus was ever me (then I never began at conception or implantation — when, exactly, did "I" pop into being, with no biological event marking it?), or retreat to "what matters" rather than identity (Variant A) — which concedes the metaphysical thesis outright. The thesis as stated, the strict biconditional, does not survive premise 4.

The same point sinks *sufficiency* from the other end: a human animal can persist in a persistent vegetative state with the cerebrum destroyed and no psychological continuity preserved. Something living is still there — it breathes, heals, resists infection. The psychological view must say that thing is not me, that I already died. But then a living human organism exists that is *not* identical to any person and was never anyone — a strange new kind of object the theory conjures into existence to keep its books. Biology has no such gap.

**The thinking-animal / "too many thinkers" argument (against the coincidence ontology the thesis needs).** Grant the psychological theorist his best reconstruction — Variant B, where the person and the human animal are two coincident things with different persistence conditions. Then Olson's thinking-animal argument bites. The SEP states it in three premises ([SEP, *Animalism*](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2025/entries/animalism/)):

- (P1) Presently sitting in your chair is a human animal.
- (P2) The human animal sitting in your chair is thinking.
- (P3) You are the thinking being sitting in your chair.

Conclusion: you *are* that human animal. Each premise is near-undeniable. The animal exists (P1) — no one disputes there is a living *Homo sapiens* here. The animal thinks (P2) — it has a functioning brain, sense organs, the full causal apparatus of cognition; if *that* does not think, nothing does, and the naturalist will not credit a thinker with no physical machinery. And you are the thinker here (P3) — you are not eavesdropping on someone else's thoughts. From these three, you are the animal.

The neo-Lockean who wants the person to be distinct from the animal must reject one of these, and every option is a disaster. Reject P2 and you must say this brain-equipped organism does *not* think — yet it is physically identical in every cognitive respect to the "person." Reject P3 and you accept that there are (at least) two thinkers here, the animal and the person, each entertaining this very thought — and now you cannot tell which one you are: when you judge "I am a person, not an animal," the animal is thinking the very same words and is, by your own lights, wrong about itself. That is the "too many thinkers" problem ([SEP, *Animalism*](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2025/entries/animalism/); [Olson, "An Argument for Animalism"](https://philpapers.org/rec/OLSAAF)). Coincident-entity ontology does not solve this; it *is* the problem, doubling the thinkers in every chair.

And here the naturalist tightens the screw, because van Inwagen — the other animalist named in the framing — removes the escape hatch at the level of ontology. On his organicism in *Material Beings*, the only composite objects that exist are living organisms: simples compose something if and only if they are caught up in the life of an organism ([review of van Inwagen, *Material Beings*](https://jwwartick.com/2012/01/02/mb-pvi/); [SEP, *Ordinary Objects*](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ordinary-objects/)). There simply is no second thing — no Lockean "person" object distinct from the animal — for psychology to be the identity criterion *of*. The thesis needs a substance whose persistence conditions are psychological. Naturalistic ontology delivers one substance here, the organism, and its persistence conditions are biological. Psychology individuates a *phase* of that animal, not a *thing*.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P6** in its load-bearing role and **P7** outright, and with them the necessity conclusion C2; I also deny **P4**'s sufficiency.

- **P6 / P7 (necessity).** The fetus shows biology without psychology persisting as *me*, so psychology is not necessary; P7's claim that psychology is the *only* candidate identity-maker is asserted, not earned (the reconstruction concedes this at lines 47–51), and it is false because biological life is a perfectly good — indeed the only causally locatable — identity-maker. The naturalist reason: a persistence condition has to be a condition on *some thing in the world*. The living organism is that thing, traceable by an unbroken metabolic process from fetus to corpse-edge. A "psychological chain" floating free of any single substrate is not the persistence condition of an object; it is a description of a pattern that an object instantiates for a while.

- **P4 (sufficiency).** Fission (reconstruction line 61) already shows psychology can hold one-to-many while identity cannot, so psychology is not sufficient for identity. The animal criterion gives the determinate answer fission lacks: split a brain into two bodies and you have, at most, performed a strange surgery on one animal and built another — biology, not the indeterminate "chain," tells you which living thing is which.

The deepest denial is of the suppressed premise SP and the whole coincidence picture (Variant B): there are not two thinkers per chair. The appearance that we *need* a psychological "person" distinct from the animal is an artifact of taking the first-person, self-conscious mode of access as if it revealed a special object. It does not. It reveals an animal thinking about itself. Deflate the extra entity and nothing in our self-understanding is lost — the animal remembers, intends, and has character, because that is what this kind of animal does. We are not animals *plus* persons. We are animals that, for a phase, are persons. Biology is the identity-maker.
