VERDICT: The thesis is ill-posed at P1–P2: it begins by chopping the self into "person-stages" and then hunts for the relation that re-binds the slices, but the self is given in experience as a single temporal flow that is never assembled out of stages in the first place — so psychological continuity is not a wrong answer, it is an answer to a question the lived self does not raise.

## Case

The argument does not start with a person. It starts with a metaphysical decision, made before any premise is stated, to treat a life as a series of momentary "person-stages" (P1, P2, P3, P5) and then to search for the relation R that makes two such stages count as one and the same. Everything downstream — the contest between psychological continuity and biological continuity — is a contest over *which relation does the re-binding*. My claim is that the re-binding problem is manufactured by the opening move. There is nothing to re-bind, because nothing was ever cut apart except in theory.

Begin with description, not explanation. When I attend to my own ongoing experience, I do not find a row of discrete now-points that I then stitch together. Husserl's analysis of internal time-consciousness shows why. The lived present is not a knife-edge instant; it has width. Every moment of awareness has the structure of *retention–primal impression–protention*: the just-elapsed phase is still held onto as it slips away (retention), the now is given (primal impression), and what is about to come is already anticipated (protention). Husserl calls these "distinguishable yet inseparable moments" and insists the structure is "an identity in a manifold," not an aggregate of atoms ([IEP, Phenomenology and Time-Consciousness](https://iep.utm.edu/phe-time/)). The hearing of a melody is the standard case: I do not hear a tone, then a separate tone, then perform a mental act of association; I hear *the melody*, with the preceding notes still sounding in retention as the present note arrives. The unity is in the givenness, not added to it afterward.

This matters for the thesis directly. Husserl describes the stream of conscious life as "an absolute flowing identity in a manifold — of primal impression, retention and protention" ([IEP](https://iep.utm.edu/phe-time/)). The flow constitutes its *own* identity in the very flowing; it is a "standing-streaming" that grounds all temporal awareness without itself being one more item in time. So the question "what relation R binds an earlier stage to a later stage?" presupposes exactly what time-consciousness shows to be false: that there are first separate stages, and then a binding. In lived experience the holding-together is primitive. You cannot get behind it to find unattached slices waiting to be related, because retention and protention are not relations *between* experiences — they are the form of any single experience whatsoever.

Merleau-Ponty draws the conclusion the thesis needs to hear: the self is not something time happens *to*. "We must understand time as the subject and the subject as time," he writes, distinguishing sharply between the idea that "time is *for* someone" and the deeper claim that "time *is* someone." He explicitly rejects the picture of the self "as successive, atomistic instants" ([IEP](https://iep.utm.edu/phe-time/); [EBSCO Research Starters, *Phenomenology of Perception*](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/phenomenology-perception-maurice-merleau-ponty)). If subjectivity *is* temporality — if I do not have a series of states but *am* the standing flow that throws itself forward into a future and trails a past — then "person-stage" is not a neutral description of the data. It is a theoretical re-coding of a flow into the very atomism phenomenology was written to refute. P1 and P2 are not premises about the self; they are a metaphysics about the self, smuggled in as the format of the question.

Notice what this does to the dispute the framer set up. Both the psychological theorist and the animalist accept the stage-and-relation framing; they only disagree about which relation (psychological connectedness vs. metabolic continuity) does the binding. From where I stand they have already lost the phenomenon together. The fission case — paraded as the thesis's great threat — is the clearest symptom. Fission looks paradoxical (two later stages, one earlier, identity can't be one-to-many) only on the assumption that identity is a *relation* that must be either one-to-one or fail. But the lived self is not a relation between slices that could be made to branch; it is a flow that is *lived through* in the first person. The puzzle is an artifact of the slicing, not a discovery about persons. Where there is no genuine stream of retention-and-protention being lived forward, there is no person there to be "the same" or "different" — and the thought-experiment quietly assumes there determinately *is*, on both branches, precisely so that the arithmetic can look broken.

I am not saying there is no self (that is the no-self road, and not mine). I am saying the self is given as one continuous temporal flow, and that the thesis cannot describe this without first dismantling it into stages it then struggles to reassemble. Ricoeur makes the positive point usable: the right question about a self is not idem — "what is the thing, and what relation keeps it the same thing?" — but ipse, "who?" — answered through the temporal, narrative unfolding of a life, the constancy of one who keeps a promise and owns a past ([SEP, Paul Ricoeur](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ricoeur/); [EBSCO](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/phenomenology-perception-maurice-merleau-ponty)). "Who am I?" is not answered by locating a re-binding relation among slices. It is answered by living, and recounting, a single temporal course. The thesis answers a *what*-question (which relation R?) when the self only ever raised a *who*-question.

So my verdict is not "psychology, not biology." It is: the biconditional "same person if and only if relation R holds between stages" misdescribes its object before either candidate for R is named. P3 and P4 (psychology suffices, nothing more needed) and P6 and P7 (biology insufficient, psychology the only candidate) are answers in a game whose board is wrongly drawn. Reject the board.

## What the thesis presupposes

The unexamined assumption is **stage ontology**: that a life is, at the level of what is real, a sequence of momentary person-stages, and that personal identity is therefore a *relation* that must be found holding between those stages. The whole dispute — psychological continuity *versus* biological continuity — lives inside that assumption and cannot be conducted without it. P1 ("identity consists in whatever relation must hold between an earlier person-stage and a later person-stage") and P2 (the biconditional over stages) are where it enters, dressed as the harmless setup rather than as the substantive — and, I claim, false — metaphysics it is. Phenomenology denies the premise at its source: the self is not first given as slices and then bound; it is given, in the structure of time-consciousness, as one flow whose holding-together is prior to any relation between parts. Remove the slicing and the search for R has no object. The naturalist's "metabolic continuity" and the psychologist's "overlapping chains" are rival glues for a vase that was never broken.
