POSITION: Substance dualist / soul theorist about personal identity (Richard Swinburne, "Personal Identity: The Dualist Theory" 1984; *The Evolution of the Soul* 1986/1997). Personal identity consists in the persistence of one and the same immaterial soul; neither psychological nor bodily/biological continuity constitutes identity — they are at most evidence of it.
VERDICT: The thesis is false, and false in an instructive way — it correctly rejects the bodily criterion but then enthrones a psychological criterion that the very fission cases its tradition relies on prove cannot constitute identity either; what constitutes identity is a third-kind fact, the continuing of the same soul, which no inventory of memory-chains or organisms can settle.

## Case

The thesis says a later person is the same as an earlier one *because, and only because* of psychological continuity. I deny the "only because," and I deny that *any* relation of empirical continuity — psychological or biological — does the constituting work. The relations the thesis trades in are real and they are good evidence; they are simply not what identity *consists in*. That is the suppressed premise the reconstruction itself flags — SP, the claim that no third-kind criterion is even a candidate (01-reconstruction.md, lines 47-51). I occupy exactly that denied corner.

**1. The duplication argument turns the thesis's own best case against it.** The neo-Lockean leans on fission to pry psychology apart from biology. But fission is the soul theorist's argument, not the psychologist's. Take Swinburne's brain-division case: Alexandra's brain is split, the left hemisphere going into one body (Alex), the right into another (Sandra), each resulting person psychologically continuous with the original. The psychological criterion (P3-P4 of the reconstruction) says continuity is sufficient for identity. But Alex and Sandra are two, not one; identity is one-one and transitive; so they cannot both be Alexandra, and there is no non-arbitrary ground for saying she is one rather than the other. The psychologist's options are all losses: say she is neither (then sufficiency, P4, is false — continuity held and identity did not), say she is both (then identity is not transitive, contradiction), or say "identity is not what matters" (then the thesis's "only because identity" clause is abandoned, which is the Parfitian retreat the reconstruction marks as Variant A, lines 71-76). Swinburne's point is that *every* one of these moves concedes the real lesson: **no fact about psychological continuity, however complete, entails an answer to "did Alexandra survive, and as whom?"** The empirical facts can be held fixed and the identity-question stays open. ([Swinburne, "The Dualist Theory"](https://iweb.langara.ca/rjohns/files/2016/11/swinburne_identity_short.pdf); summary of the brain-division argument at [Speaks, Notre Dame](https://academicweb.nd.edu/~jspeaks/courses/2006-7/20208/swinburne-dualism.html))

**2. The same argument refutes the bodily criterion, which is why the thesis is half-right.** Swinburne runs the duplication objection symmetrically against *both* the bodily theory and the memory theory: each "allows the possibility of duplication, where you could bear the claimed identity relation to two future people; but since these people are two and not one, they cannot both be identical to you" (search-confirmed gloss of Swinburne's principal argument). So the thesis is correct that biological-organism continuity does not constitute identity (it agrees with my P6-style verdict). Its error is purely in supposing that the only alternative to biology is psychology. Both criteria fail for the *same* structural reason — duplicability — and that reason is invisible to anything that constitutes identity out of duplicable empirical relations. The candidate that survives the argument is precisely one that is *not* duplicable: a particular, indivisible immaterial substance.

**3. There must be a further fact, and it must be the soul.** Identity is a one-one relation that holds with full determinacy: either Alexandra is Alex or she is not. If no possible enrichment of the physical and psychological description settles this — and the fission case shows it does not — then the truth about identity is a *further truth* over and above all such description. Swinburne's positive account is quasi-Aristotelian: a thing persists by retaining the same essential stuff while parts are gradually replaced; since bodily matter and the brain are shown by the duplication cases not to be the relevant stuff, "there is stuff of another kind, immaterial stuff... and it is the continuity of the latter which provides that continuity" of the person ([Swinburne, "The Dualist Theory," p. 27, quoted in Speaks](https://academicweb.nd.edu/~jspeaks/courses/2006-7/20208/swinburne-dualism.html)). In *The Evolution of the Soul* the claim is put as: bodily continuity and similarity of memory and character are *evidence* of personal identity but do not *constitute* it; personal identity is something further, and in that sense unanalysable into other facts ([Swinburne, *The Evolution of the Soul*, Oxford, ch. on the evidence of personal identity](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-evolution-of-the-soul-9780198236986)). This is what Swinburne calls the **simple view**: identity is not constituted by any complex of more basic relations; it is the strict persistence of one immaterial substance, the soul.

**4. The "evidence vs. constitution" distinction defuses the rivals at a stroke.** Animalism (Olson's fetus argument) and neo-Lockeanism look like an exhaustive dichotomy only if you assume identity must be *constituted* by some observable continuity. Drop that assumption and the data realign cleanly. The fetus is the same person as the adult because the same soul informs both — psychological continuity was never needed for necessity, so Olson's necessity-refutation (00-thesis.md, line 23) misses a soul theorist entirely. Fission is genuinely puzzling about *where the soul went*, but it is not a contradiction, because there is a fact of the matter (the soul went with at most one branch, or with neither) that simply outruns our evidence. The reductionist treats "we cannot tell who survived" as "there is no fact about who survived"; the soul theorist treats it as exactly what you would expect if identity is a further fact — epistemically inaccessible precisely because it is not constituted by the evidence.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P7** and the suppressed premise **SP** (01-reconstruction.md, lines 27, 49). P7 asserts that psychological continuity is the *only* candidate sufficient to constitute identity; SP excludes soul/substance criteria from candidacy without argument. My reason is Swinburne's: the eliminative argument that gets you to psychology rules out biology by the duplication objection, but *that very objection rules out psychology too*. Any relation built from duplicable empirical materials inherits the fission problem. So the argument that was meant to install psychology actually clears the field of every empirical-continuity criterion and leaves standing only a non-duplicable particular — the soul. The thesis's "only because" is the load-bearing falsehood: there is a third kind of constituting fact, and it is the one the council's framing quietly assumed away.

Secondarily I deny **P4** (sufficiency). Even granting psychological continuity holds in both fission branches, identity fails to hold in at least one of them, so continuity is not sufficient. The psychologist can save P4 only by paying with the "only because identity" clause (Parfit's "identity is not what matters"), at which point the thesis has been abandoned rather than defended.

I do *not* deny **P6** — I affirm it. Biological continuity does not constitute identity. The thesis and I agree against the animalist on this. Our quarrel is solely over what replaces the body: a memory-chain (theirs) or a soul (mine), and the duplication argument that sinks the body sinks the memory-chain alongside it.
