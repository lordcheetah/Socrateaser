POSITION: Parfitian reductionist (Derek Parfit, *Reasons and Persons*, Part III) — personal identity just consists in physical/psychological continuity holding, there is no further deep fact, and "identity is not what matters in survival."
VERDICT: The thesis is half right and fatally over-stated: psychological continuity is what we should care about, but the "because, and ONLY because *identity*" framing is the error, because the relation that matters (Relation R) can hold one-to-many in fission, and so cannot be identity at all.

## Case

I accept far more of this thesis than its named opponents do, and that is exactly why I am the most dangerous reader of it. I grant the spirit of P3: what we care about when we care about "survival" is psychological — overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, and character. I deny the *form* the thesis wraps around that truth. The fatal move is the conjunction of P2 ("two stages are one and the same person *if and only if* the identity-making relation holds") with the "only because identity" clause. Identity is the wrong job for psychological continuity to do.

**Reductionism first (P1 of the thesis's own structure, but read deflationarily).** On my view, the fact of a person's continued existence "just consists in" the holding of certain more particular facts — psychological continuity and connectedness, and the physical continuity of enough brain to carry it — and these facts "can be described without either presupposing the identity of this person, or explicitly claiming that the experiences in this person's life are had by this person" (*Reasons and Persons*, §79, the Reductionist thesis). Crucially, "there is no further fact" — no deep, separately existing entity, no Cartesian Ego whose all-or-nothing presence settles the matter (§81, "The Unimportance of Identity"; §80 against the "Further Fact View"). Once you are a Reductionist, the question "but is the later person *really, literally identical* to the earlier one?" can in some cases be *empty* — a verbal question with no hidden fact behind it (§87). The thesis treats identity as a deep target that psychology either hits or misses. There is no such target.

**Then fission breaks the "only because identity" clause.** Take Parfit's "My Division" (§89, drawing on Wiggins's case): my body is fatally injured, my brain is divided, and each half is transplanted into the body of one of my two brothers. Each resulting person is fully psychologically continuous with me. Now run the argument the thesis cannot survive:

1. By the thesis's own P3/P4, psychological continuity is *sufficient* for identity. So each resulting person is me.
2. But identity is one-one and transitive: Lefty and Righty cannot both be me without being each other, and they are plainly two people, living separate lives. (*Reasons and Persons*, §89.)
3. So at most one is me — yet "there is no difference between Lefty and Righty in their relation to me" to ground a choice; any such choice is arbitrary.
4. Could it be that I survive as *neither*? Parfit's hammer: I would clearly survive if *one* transplant succeeded and the other half were destroyed. "How could a double success be a failure?" (§89). It is absurd that adding a second successful transplant *kills* me.

The thesis offers only two exits, both of which destroy it as stated:

- **Sufficiency fails** (the animalist's reading of P4): then psychological continuity is *not* enough for identity, and the "only because psychology" clause is false.
- **Sufficiency holds but identity does not** (my reading): then the original person does not survive *as a single identical person*, yet everything that matters — the memories, the intentions, the character — is doubly preserved. Fission is "about as good as ordinary survival" (§90).

The second exit is the correct one, and it dissolves the thesis's framing. What is preserved in fission is **Relation R: psychological connectedness and/or continuity, with the right kind of cause** (*Reasons and Persons*, §78, §90). R can branch — it can hold between me and *two* later people. Identity, being one-one, cannot. Therefore the relation we actually care about *is not identity*. "Personal identity is not what matters" (§95). The thesis says we are the same person later "because, and only because" psychological continuity links us. I answer: psychological continuity (R) is indeed what links what matters — but precisely *because* it can do so one-to-many, it cannot be what *identity* consists in. The thesis conscripts R into the role of identity, and fission shows R is the wrong shape for that role.

This is why I am inside the psychological tradition and still reject the thesis. I do not reach for biology (I leave Olson to the animalists). I agree psychology is what matters. My objection is internal: the thesis mistakes *what matters* for *identity*, and Parfit's whole achievement in Part III is to pry those two apart. Identity tracks R only in the ordinary, non-branching case — and even there it is "identity *because* non-branching R," not R *because* identity. The "only because identity" framing inverts the explanatory order.

This also matches the reconstruction's own Variant A, which the framer rightly flags as "a genuine variant, not a rescue of the same thesis." Exactly. My position *is* that variant promoted to the main claim: psychological continuity is the relation that matters for prudence, anticipation, and concern, *regardless* of whether it constitutes numerical identity — and fission proves it sometimes does not.

## What I deny and why

I deny **P2** as the thesis uses it (the strict biconditional that makes identity the thing constituted) and the **"only because identity"** clause of the conclusion. My characteristic reason is the fission dilemma: in My Division, the identity-making relation the thesis names (psychological continuity) holds one-to-many, while identity is necessarily one-one; the relation therefore cannot *be* identity. The thesis is forced to choose between giving up sufficiency (abandoning its psychological commitment) or giving up the claim that what holds is *identity* (abandoning its form). I take the second horn and conclude, with Parfit, that "identity is not what matters."

I further deny the **suppressed Further-Fact assumption** behind the whole "because" framing — that there is always a determinate deep fact about whether the later person *is* the earlier one (reconstruction's SP and the Direction-1 architecture). On Reductionism there is no such further fact (§81, §87); in fission and other cases the identity question can be empty. A thesis that says we persist "because, and *only* because" a relation that holds *identity* presupposes identity is always a deep, determinate, one-one fact awaiting an explanation. It is not. So the explanandum the thesis offers — strict numerical identity grounded in psychology — is the wrong thing to be explaining, and the right thing (R, what matters) is not identity.
